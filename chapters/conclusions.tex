\chapter{Conclusions}
\label{chp:conclusions}

\section{Summary}

This thesis presented a fused gradient-ascent navigation controller that enables a micro-drone to navigate toward a light source using a combination of bearing angle estimation and a self-built light intensity map.

The controller was validated in simulation, demonstrating obstacle avoidance capability that the bearing-only baseline cannot achieve, and then successfully ported to a CrazyFlie~2.1 equipped with a custom miniaturised photodiode PCB.

Real-world experiments confirmed that the SNR-based signal processing pipeline effectively isolates the target beacon from ambient light, and that the fused controller navigates correctly in both open-space and single-obstacle scenarios.

\section{Limitations}

The operating range of the system is constrained by the photodiode sensor circuit: at large distances the SNR between the modulated beacon and ambient light becomes insufficient for reliable detection, while at close range the photodiodes saturate, creating a narrow effective operating band.

Navigation is currently limited to a fixed flight altitude, so the bearing estimate and the gradient map the drone produces are both two-dimensional.

Extending to full three-dimensional navigation would require a 3D gradient map and additional photodiodes to sense elevation---ideally a full spherical arrangement rather than the current horizontal ring---along with significantly increased computational complexity.

Real-world obstacle avoidance and multi-waypoint navigation were each demonstrated successfully, showing that the controller reliably performs the core tasks it was designed for.

Validating it across more complex, multi-obstacle environments is left as future work to push an already-working system toward optimal performance.
