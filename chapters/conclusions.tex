\chapter{Conclusions}
\label{chp:conclusions}

This thesis set out to let a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone navigate to several light beacons and around the obstacles between them, using only on-board light sensing. Chapter~\ref{chp:introduction} frames this challenge. The work builds on two single-beacon methods of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}: a bearing estimation algorithm and a gradient estimator. The bearing sums each photodiode's reading as a vector, so it points toward the strongest light the instant the array of photodiodes senses it. But it keeps no record of the space the drone has flown through. The gradient-based method on the other hand infers the slope of the light field from a single photodiode and the drone's recent motion. This lets it follow the field. It does however, leave this method slow. It is also sensitive to the drift of the odometry of the drone. Each method reaches a single beacon in open space. Closing these two gaps, within the chosen drone's budget, is the problem this thesis takes on. The rest of this chapter revisits how each part of the work contributes to that goal, organised around the three research questions of Chapter~\ref{chp:introduction}. It then sets out the limitations that bound the result.

Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} develops the controller at the centre of the thesis. It is a fused controller that combines the bearing with a gradient estimated from a light map the drone builds as it flies. The map is the persistent spatial memory neither prior method had\todo{This claim is still not true!}. As the drone moves, it writes the strongest reading of its photodiode array into a grid cell indexed by position. A value measured earlier therefore stays available once the beacon leaves direct view\todo{"once the beacon leaves direct view" why is this relevant and here?}. Because the map is built from the full eight-photodiode array, it records a usable value at each visited location whichever way the drone faces. This removes the dependence on heading and motion that limits the single-photodiode method. The controller then estimates the gradient from this map, by fitting a plane to the cells around the drone. We select weighted least squares for this fit, after comparing five candidate estimators on the same field. It returns a usable direction across almost the whole map. Its computational cost is low and constant, which the microcontroller can afford. A state machine sequences a mission and decides, at each moment, whether to steer on the bearing, on the bearing and gradient blended together, or on the gradient alone once the bearing is lost.\todo{Back reference to sections that certain things were talked about in more often!}

The first research question asks whether such a drone can carry the light sensing it needs on board and recover a reliable beacon signal in flight. Two parts of the thesis answer it together. 

Firstly, chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} presents the sensing board. It replaces an earlier Teensy prototype. That prototype, at about 44~grams and 90~millimetres across, was far too large and heavy for the CrazyFlie. The new board fits eight surface-mount photodiodes and their amplifiers into about 6~grams and 42~millimetres, within the 13~gram and 44~millimetre budget the platform allows. It carries no processor of its own and offloads all computation to the CrazyFlie. It therefore spends neither mass nor power on hardware the CrazyFlie already provides.

Secondly, chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} then recovers the beacon from the board's raw output. Each photodiode reports a single voltage in which the beacon is buried under ambient light, electrical interference, and vibration. The pipeline takes a Fourier transform of each channel and reads the height of the peak at the beacon's modulation frequency as the recovered signal. It then divides that peak by the noise floor of the spectrum to report a signal-to-noise ratio, which states how far the reading can be trusted. Building the pipeline up one stage at a time shows what makes it work: estimating the noise floor with a median, so the occasional interference spike does not inflate it. The median floor more than doubles the in-flight ratio, from about 15 to about 32. The hardware anti-alias filter complements it by keeping the floor low. Together the board and the pipeline let a constrained micro-drone carry its light sensing on board and return a reliable beacon signal in flight, which answers the first research question.

The second research question asks whether the drone can then navigate to several distinct beacons in turn. Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon} answers it in two stages. Each beacon is modulated at its own frequency. A single frequency analysis of one recording therefore holds a separate peak for each beacon. The controller selects a target by reading the peak at its frequency. A static bench test \todo{Again what static bench. The same test shows that the signal-to-noise ratio gives a far cleaner field than the raw magnitude. Dividing by the noise floor normalises away both the per-sensor gain differences and the broadband interference. The full system then flies the CrazyFlie to two beacons in turn, the state machine\todo{Which state machine?} running its phases once per beacon and advancing on arrival. This shows that a constrained micro-drone can navigate to several distinct light beacons in turn using only on-board light sensing, which answers the second research question.

The third research question asks whether the drone can navigate around an obstacle that blocks its line of sight to a beacon. Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle} answers it first in simulation and then in flight. In the simulated scene where both baselines fail, the bearing-only controller collides with the pillar and the single-photodiode gradient stalls against it. The fused controller instead blends the gradient into its heading and curves cleanly around the pillar to the beacon. The avoidance comes from the fusion itself, not from any fallback. On the real drone, two flights show the controller drawing on the map only as far as each situation needs. In the first, the drone passes through enough of the obstacle's shadow that the beacon signal drops below the bearing's trust threshold. The state machine then enters its recovering phase, following the gradient from the stored map alone until the beacon returns. In the second, the drone skirts the edge of the shadow, keeps a usable bearing throughout, and rounds the box on the blended heading without ever falling back on the map. In both cases it reaches a beacon the obstacle had hidden, which answers the third research question.

The contribution is not tied to the CrazyFlie. Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} built the blimp and the simulator this work uses. In a collaboration with them, the same board and fused controller also fly on the real blimp. Only the low-level motor control is adapted to its different airframe. On the blimp they reach several beacons and avoid obstacles just as they do on the quadrotor. This shows the navigation depends on the light sensing rather than on the specifics of the platform.

The three research questions are therefore answered in the affirmative: the sensing is feasible within the drone's budget, the drone reaches several beacons in turn, and it routes around an obstacle that hides its beacon. The prior work this thesis extends could reach only a single beacon in open space. The board and fused controller developed here give a constrained micro-drone both the multi-beacon traversal and the obstacle avoidance it lacked, using only on-board light sensing. They do so across two different airframes.

The operating range of the system is set by the photodiode sensor circuit we built. This is a property of the current circuit rather than a fundamental limit: more carefully designed hardware, for instance higher-order band-pass filters that also reject ambient light, should widen the usable range at both ends.

The navigation is confined to a fixed altitude by our algorithm and hardware. Both directions we compute are planar, since the photodiodes sit in the horizontal ring, as shown in the design of the PCB in Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}. The algorithms we use and described in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} resolve direction only in the horizontal plane and the light map is a 2D grid indexed by the drone's horizontal position. Extending this to full three-dimensional navigation is mostly a hardware problem. The algorithm itself adapts without much trouble: the map becomes an $O(G^3)$ volume rather than an $O(G^2)$ grid for a side of $G$ cells, and the local gradient fit simply gains a third coordinate, which raises the computation but not prohibitively. The harder part is the hardware. The present ring carries its $N$ photodiodes around a single circle, the eight of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}; covering the sphere at the same angular spacing would need roughly $N$ such rings stacked over elevation, raising the count to about $N^2$. It is this array, not the added computation, that is the real bottleneck.

The real-world results are demonstrations rather than a statistical measure of reliability. We flew many more missions than the handful shown in Chapters~\ref{chp:multibeacon} and~\ref{chp:obstacle}, and the drone reached its beacon on most of them. But we did not run a controlled repeatability study. These flights therefore show that the multi-beacon traversal and the obstacle avoidance work, rather than how often they succeed.

The approach also assumes a static environment. The beacons and the obstacle stay fixed throughout every experiment. The light map of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} is built on that assumption: each reading is written into a cell and trusted thereafter, with no mechanism to detect that the field has since changed. A beacon that moved, or an obstacle that shifted after the drone had mapped its shadow, would therefore leave stale values in the map that the controller would still follow. The present results bound the approach to environments that do not change while the drone navigates them. The dynamic case is left for future work.
