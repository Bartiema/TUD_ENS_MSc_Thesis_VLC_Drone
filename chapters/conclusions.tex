\chapter{Conclusions}
\label{chp:conclusions}

In this thesis, we ask a central question for autonomous drone deployment: how can autonomous navigation be provided on a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone with minimal changes to the environment? We address this question with a visible-light navigation system that uses lightweight on-board light sensing to guide the drone toward modulated light beacons, requiring only minimal modification to existing lighting infrastructure. To make our system more robust in cluttered indoor environments and real applications, we develop a complete system that enables the drone to visit multiple targets in sequence and avoid obstacles that partially block the direct path to the light source.

The work builds on the light-guided navigation system proposed by Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}, especially their bearing-angle estimation and gradient-based light-source seeking algorithms. The bearing estimate provides an immediate direction toward a visible beacon, but it keeps no memory of the space the drone has already observed. The gradient method, in contrast, can follow changes in the light field, but it is slower because it must infer direction from measurements gathered during motion. These methods are insufficient for the more realistic missions considered in this thesis. They do not by themselves provide robust sequencing across multiple beacons, and a bearing-only controller can still fail when an obstacle weakens or blocks the direct light signal, even though it is reported as the most efficient method in open-space source seeking.

This thesis set out to overcome these limitations within the constraints of a micro-drone. It pursues two goals: reaching several beacons in succession, and reaching a beacon when an obstacle blocks the direct path. Meeting these goals required solving four challenges, which follow from the three research questions of Chapter~\ref{chp:introduction}. Table~\ref{tab:rq-mapping} maps the research questions onto these challenges and goals. The following paragraphs discuss each challenge and how this thesis addresses it.

The first challenge is whether the sensing is feasible at all. We find that it is not the limiting factor. The custom board of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} provides eight-direction light sensing for under half of the drone's payload budget. It carries no processor of its own and adds no functionality the drone does not need. Useful directional light sensing is therefore well within the reach of a micro-drone. The difficulty lies in the navigation, not in the sensor.

The second challenge is to recover a trustworthy beacon signal in flight. Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} develops the signal pipeline that does this. The decisive factor is how the pipeline estimates the noise floor. Using a median rather than a mean more than doubles the in-flight signal-to-noise ratio, from about 15 to about 32. The hardware anti-alias filter matters too, but only in combination. On its own it barely changes the ratio, yet removing it from the full pipeline lowers the in-flight ratio from about 32 to 22. Recovering the signal in flight therefore depends less on the measurement itself than on rejecting the noise around it.

The third challenge supports the first goal: distinguishing several beacons. We modulate each beacon at its own frequency, so a single recording carries every beacon as a separate peak, and the controller chooses its target by frequency. With this, the drone reaches several beacons in succession in real flight, as Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon} demonstrates. The first goal is therefore met: where the prior single-beacon methods could reach only one beacon, the drone now completes a mission across many.

The fourth challenge supports the second goal, which is the central problem of the thesis: reaching a beacon when an obstacle blocks the direct path. The enabling idea is spatial memory. The fused controller of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} builds a map of the light field as it flies and estimates a gradient from it. The drone therefore retains a direction to follow even when the beacon is hidden. The contrast with the prior methods is sharp. As Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle} shows, the bearing-only controller flies straight into the obstacle, and the single-photodiode gradient stalls against it. Both fail immediately. The fused controller instead detects when the beacon is occluded and curves around the obstacle without stopping, reaching the beacon in both simulation and real flight. The two real flights show this in two regimes. In the first, the bearing is lost behind the obstacle, and the drone navigates on the stored map alone. In the second, the blended heading alone is enough to round the obstacle, without ever falling back on the map. The second goal is therefore met: the drone keeps its efficiency while avoiding the obstacle, rather than crashing in a cluttered environment.

Underpinning both goals is the fused controller, the core technical contribution of this thesis. It adds a spatial memory of the light field to the bearing and gradient primitives of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}, and decides at each moment which estimate to trust. This is what lets the drone both sequence beacons and route around obstacles, rather than reach only a single beacon in open space.

Taken together, the four challenges are solved, and the two goals with them. The drone carries its own light sensing, recovers the beacon in flight, visits several beacons in turn, and routes around an obstacle that hides one. We demonstrate this in simulation and in real flight. The same board and controller also carry over to a second airframe. In a collaboration with Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}, whose blimp and simulator this work builds on, the system reaches several beacons and avoids obstacles on their blimp as well. The navigation therefore depends on the light sensing, not on the platform.

The advance over the prior work is concrete. Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} reached a single beacon in open space. This thesis extends that to the missions indoor deployment requires: several targets visited in turn, and obstacles routed around. It does so within the budget of a micro-drone, using only the modulated lamps already present in the space. Light-guided navigation thus moves a step closer to a practical, low-infrastructure positioning method for the smallest indoor drones.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Even though our system provides a promising, novel sensing approach for indoor autonomous drone operation, some limitations remain in the current implementation. The first is its operating range, which is bounded by the photodiode sensing circuit. When the drone is far from a beacon, the modulated signal becomes weak relative to steady background light, causing the SNR to fall below the level needed for reliable navigation, as measured in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}. When the drone is very close to the beacon, the photodiodes can instead approach saturation, causing the recovered signal to flatten; the close-range dip observed in Section~\ref{sec:snr-field} suggests this upper bound. The usable region therefore lies between these two limits, where the beacon is neither too weak to distinguish from the background nor too strong for the sensor front end to measure linearly. This limitation comes from the current circuit implementation rather than from visible-light navigation itself. A more carefully designed sensing front end, such as one with improved dynamic range or higher-order band-pass filtering to suppress ambient light, could widen the usable range at both ends.

Second, the current system is limited to fixed-altitude navigation because both the sensing board shape and the controller are planar. The photodiodes are arranged in a horizontal ring, as described in the PCB design in Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}, so the bearing estimate only resolves direction within that plane. Similarly, the algorithms described in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} operate on a two-dimensional light map indexed by the drone's horizontal position. Extending the method to full three-dimensional navigation is therefore limited more by hardware than by computation. Algorithmically, the map would become an $O(G^3)$ volume rather than an $O(G^2)$ grid for $G$ cells, and the local gradient fit would simply include a third coordinate. This increases computation, but not prohibitively. The larger challenge is sensing coverage. The present board places $N$ photodiodes around a single horizontal ring, with $N=8$ in Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}. Covering a sphere with comparable angular spacing would require multiple rings over elevation, increasing the sensor count to roughly $O(N^2)$. Thus, the main bottleneck for three-dimensional navigation is the size, weight, and power cost of the photodiode array, rather than the gradient algorithm itself.

Third, the real-world results are demonstrations rather than a measure of reliability. We flew many more missions than the few shown in Chapters~\ref{chp:multibeacon} and~\ref{chp:obstacle}, and the drone reached its beacon on most of them, but we did not run a controlled repeatability study. This matters for deployment: a system trusted to navigate on its own must be characterised by how often it succeeds, not only by whether it can. Quantifying that success rate across varied conditions is left to future work.

Last, we assume the drone flies in a static environment. In all experiments, the beacons and obstacles remain fixed while the drone navigates. The light map described in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} is built under this assumption: each measurement is stored in a grid cell and then reused as if the underlying light field remains unchanged. If a beacon moves, or if an obstacle shifts after the drone has mapped its shadow, the map can contain stale values that no longer match the environment. The controller may then follow an outdated gradient. The present results therefore apply to environments that remain stable during navigation, while dynamic environments are left for future work.
