\chapter{Conclusions}
\label{chp:conclusions}

This thesis set out to let a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone navigate to several light beacons and around the obstacles between them, using only on-board light sensing. Chapter~\ref{chp:introduction} frames this challenge. The work builds on two single-beacon methods of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}: a bearing estimation algorithm and a gradient estimator. The bearing, defined in Section~\ref{sec:bearing}, sums each photodiode's reading as a vector, so it points toward the strongest light the instant the array of photodiodes senses it. But it keeps no record of the space the drone has flown through. The gradient estimator of Section~\ref{sec:gradient-based-source-seeking} instead infers the slope of the light field from a single photodiode and the drone's recent motion. This lets it follow the field, but only slowly, since the drone must keep moving to gather readings. Its estimate also drifts with the drone's odometry. Each method reaches a single beacon in open space. Yet neither tells one beacon from another. The bearing also gives no sign that an obstacle stands in the drone's path. Steering on it alone, the drone can collide with an obstacle that partly hides the beacon, as Section~\ref{sec:existing-methods-fall-short} demonstrates. Closing these two gaps, within the chosen drone's constraints, is the problem this thesis takes on. The rest of this chapter revisits how each part of the work contributes to that goal, organised around the three research questions of Chapter~\ref{chp:introduction}. It then sets out the limitations that bound the result.

Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} develops the controller at the centre of the thesis. It is a fused controller that combines the bearing with a gradient estimated from a light map the drone builds as it flies. The light map, described in Section~\ref{sec:light-intensity-map}, records the light field across the area the drone has flown: as the drone moves, it writes the strongest reading of its photodiode array into a grid cell indexed by its position. Because the map is built from the full eight-photodiode array, it records a usable value at each visited location whichever way the drone faces. This removes the dependence on heading and motion that limits the single-photodiode method. The controller then estimates the gradient from this map by fitting a plane to the cells around the drone, as Section~\ref{sec:gradient-estimation} sets out. We select weighted least squares for this fit, after comparing five candidate estimators on the same field in Section~\ref{sec:gradient-estimator-comparison}. It returns a usable direction across almost the whole map, at a low and constant computational cost the microcontroller can afford. Finally, the state machine of Section~\ref{sec:state-machine} sequences a mission and decides, at each moment, whether to steer on the bearing, on the bearing and gradient blended together, or on the gradient alone.

The first research question asks whether such a drone can carry the light sensing it needs on board and recover a reliable beacon signal in flight. Two parts of the thesis answer it together. 

Firstly, Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} presents the sensing board. It replaces an earlier Teensy prototype, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:old-prototype}. That prototype, at about 44~grams and 90~millimetres across, was far too large and heavy for the CrazyFlie. The new board fits eight surface-mount photodiodes and their amplifiers into about 6~grams and 42~millimetres. This is within the 13~gram and 44~millimetre limit that Section~\ref{sec:platform} derives from the platform. Table~\ref{tab:requirements} records that the finished board meets the set requirements. The new board carries no processor of its own and offloads all computation to the CrazyFlie. It therefore spends neither mass nor power on hardware the CrazyFlie already provides.

Secondly, Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} recovers the beacon from the board's raw output. Each photodiode reports a single voltage in which the beacon is buried under ambient light, electrical interference, and vibration. The pipeline of Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} takes a Fourier transform of each channel and reads the height of the peak at the beacon's modulation frequency as the recovered signal. It then divides that peak by the noise floor of the spectrum to report a signal-to-noise ratio, which states how far the reading can be trusted, as Figure~\ref{fig:snr-spectrum} illustrates. Building the pipeline up one stage at a time shows what makes it work: estimating the noise floor with a median, so the occasional interference spike does not inflate it. As Table~\ref{tab:filter-snr} reports, the median floor more than doubles the in-flight ratio, from about 15 to about 32. The hardware anti-alias filter complements it by keeping the floor low, an effect Table~\ref{tab:filter-hw} isolates. Together the board and the pipeline let a constrained micro-drone carry its light sensing on board and return a reliable beacon signal in flight, which answers the first research question.

The second research question asks whether the drone can then navigate to several distinct beacons in turn. Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon} answers it in two stages. Each beacon is modulated at its own frequency. A single frequency analysis of one recording therefore holds a separate peak for each beacon. The controller selects a target by reading the peak at its frequency. On the static bench of Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-setup}, the experiment of Section~\ref{sec:freq-separation} confirms that two beacons are recovered cleanly and at the same time, each one rising to a clear peak at its own beacon. Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq} maps the two recovered fields and shows that the signal-to-noise ratio gives a far cleaner field than the raw magnitude. Dividing by the noise floor normalises away both the per-sensor gain differences and the broadband interference. The full system then flies the CrazyFlie to two beacons in turn, as Figure~\ref{fig:traverse-2wp} shows. The state machine of Section~\ref{sec:state-machine} runs its phases once per beacon and advances on arrival. This shows that a constrained micro-drone can navigate to several distinct light beacons in turn using only on-board light sensing, which answers the second research question.

The third research question asks whether the drone can navigate around an obstacle that blocks its line of sight to a beacon. Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle} answers it first in simulation and then in flight. In the simulated scene where both baselines fail, the bearing-only controller collides with the pillar and the single-photodiode gradient stalls against it, as Figures~\ref{fig:bearing-fail} and~\ref{fig:gradient-fail} show. The fused controller instead blends the gradient into its heading and curves cleanly around the pillar to the beacon, which Figure~\ref{fig:fused-sim} plots. The avoidance comes from the fusion itself, not from any fallback. On the real drone, two flights show the controller drawing on the map only as far as each situation needs. In the first flight, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-good}, the drone passes through enough of the obstacle's shadow that the beacon signal drops below the bearing's trust threshold. The state machine then enters its recovering phase, following the gradient from the stored map alone until the beacon returns. In the second flight, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-dodge}, the drone skirts the edge of the shadow and keeps a usable bearing throughout. It rounds the box on the blended heading without ever falling back on the map. In both cases it reaches a beacon the obstacle had hidden, which answers the third research question.

The contribution is not tied to the CrazyFlie. Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} built the blimp and the simulator this work uses. In a collaboration with them, the same board and fused controller also fly on the real blimp, as Section~\ref{sec:blimp-generalisation} reports. Only the low-level motor control is adapted to its different airframe. On the blimp they reach several beacons and avoid obstacles just as they do on the quadrotor. This shows the navigation depends on the light sensing rather than on the specifics of the platform.

The three research questions are therefore answered in the affirmative: the sensing is feasible within the drone's budget, the drone reaches several beacons in turn, and it routes around an obstacle that hides its beacon. The prior work this thesis extends could reach only a single beacon in open space. The board and fused controller developed here give a constrained micro-drone both the multi-beacon traversal and the obstacle avoidance it lacked, using only on-board light sensing. They do so across two different airframes.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The operating range of the system is set by the photodiode sensor circuit we built. Far from a beacon, its modulated signal grows weak against the steady background light, so the SNR falls too low to trust. This is the lower, sensitivity bound of the range, measured in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}. Very close to the beacon the photodiodes saturate instead and the recovered signal flattens, the upper bound, which the faint close-range dip noted in Figure~\ref{fig:snr-field-170} tentatively points to. The field is therefore clean only between these two bounds: not so far that the modulated signal is lost in the background, nor so close that the sensor saturates. This is a property of the current circuit rather than a fundamental limit: more carefully designed hardware, for instance higher-order band-pass filters that also reject ambient light, should widen the usable range at both ends.

The navigation is confined to a fixed altitude by our algorithm and hardware. Both directions we compute are planar, since the photodiodes sit in the horizontal ring, as shown in the design of the PCB in Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}. The algorithms we use and described in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} resolve direction only in the horizontal plane and the light map is a 2D grid indexed by the drone's horizontal position. Extending this to full three-dimensional navigation is mostly a hardware problem. The algorithm itself adapts without much trouble: the map becomes an $O(G^3)$ volume rather than an $O(G^2)$ grid for a side of $G$ cells, and the local gradient fit simply gains a third coordinate, which raises the computation but not prohibitively. The harder part is the hardware. The present ring carries its $N$ photodiodes around a single circle, the eight of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}; covering the sphere at the same angular spacing would need roughly $N$ such rings stacked over elevation, raising the count to about $N^2$. It is this array, not the added computation, that is the real bottleneck.

The real-world results are demonstrations rather than a statistical measure of reliability. We flew many more missions than the handful shown in Chapters~\ref{chp:multibeacon} and~\ref{chp:obstacle}, and the drone reached its beacon on most of them. But we did not run a controlled repeatability study. These flights therefore show that the multi-beacon traversal and the obstacle avoidance work, rather than how often they succeed.

The approach also assumes a static environment. The beacons and the obstacle stay fixed throughout every experiment. The light map of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} is built on that assumption: each reading is written into a cell and trusted thereafter, with no mechanism to detect that the field has since changed. A beacon that moved, or an obstacle that shifted after the drone had mapped its shadow, would therefore leave stale values in the map that the controller would still follow. The present results bound the approach to environments that do not change while the drone navigates them. The dynamic case is left for future work.
