\chapter{Multi-Beacon Experiments}
\label{chp:multibeacon}

A central aim of this thesis is to reach several modulated light beacons in succession, a capability the single-beacon methods of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} do not provide. This chapter validates that capability in two stages, each isolating a different question. We first confirm, on a static bench, that the sensing separates two beacons by their modulation frequency, since the whole multi-beacon scheme of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} rests on reading each beacon from its own frequency bin. We then fly the complete fused controller on the CrazyFlie~2.1 through missions that visit the beacons one after another, and examine both where the sequencing holds and where it breaks.

\section{Experimental Setup}

We validate multi-beacon navigation in two stages, on two platforms, separating the sensing question from the system question. The first stage isolates the sensing: on a static bench we ask whether a single frequency analysis can recover two beacons at once and tell them apart, with no drone in the loop to add noise of its own. Only once the sensing is confirmed in isolation do we ask the harder question, whether the complete controller can act on that sensing in flight and step through a sequence of beacons. Confirming the sensing first means that any failure observed in flight can be attributed to the controller or the platform rather than to an inability to separate the beacons.

The bench study uses the original Teensy~4.1 prototype of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} rather than the flight board, because the question at this stage is one of sensing alone and the prototype is the better instrument for it. The prototype carries its own processor and is free of the size, weight, and power limits the flight board must respect, so it can sample faster and log continuously over a long session. Its sixteen photodiodes sample at 1600\,Hz, well above the 500\,Hz of the flight board, which places the Nyquist limit of Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} at 800\,Hz and leaves both beacon frequencies far below it, where their spectra are clean and free of aliasing. Rather than fly the sensor, we move it by hand across a regular grid of measurement positions, spaced 0.2\,m apart over an area of about one by one and a half metres, and record a separate measurement at each cell. Sampling on a static grid removes the drone's own motion and vibration from the experiment, so the recovered fields reflect the sensing alone, and it yields a dense, repeatable map of each beacon's signal across space against which the separation can be judged. Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-setup} shows the arrangement: two lamps modulated at 150 and 200\,Hz stand at opposite ends of the swept area. The room is dark throughout, so the only light beyond the two beacons comes from the computer monitors at the back of the bench, which run at full brightness about a metre away and flicker at 144\,Hz, an interferer we return to in Section~\ref{sec:freq-separation}.

Two beacons, modulated at 150 and 170\,Hz, stand in an open room; the drone takes off from a fixed origin and is given a mission that visits them in turn. We modulate the flight beacons at 150 and 170\,Hz rather than the bench pair of 150 and 200\,Hz, a tighter spacing that confirms the separation does not depend on the particular frequencies, provided no beacon falls on another's harmonic, as Section~\ref{sec:multibeacon-separation} requires. Figure~\ref{fig:flight-setup} shows the arrangement, as a photograph of the room alongside a top-down sketch of its geometry: the two lamps stand a few metres ahead of the take-off point, one roughly straight ahead and the other off to the left, so reaching them in sequence requires the drone to turn substantially between targets. We draw this geometry only as a reference to the rough arrangement of the room, not as an exact match to it, so the distances in it should not be read as measured truth. We fly two missions, one of two waypoints and one of three, to test whether the controller steps through its targets in sequence, for both a short mission and a longer one.

\section{Separating Beacons by Frequency}
\label{sec:freq-separation}

The multi-beacon scheme of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} depends on the sensing recovering every beacon from a single recording. A single frequency analysis of a photodiode signal holds each beacon's peak at its own modulation frequency, so the beacons can be read in parallel rather than one at a time, and the controller selects a beacon simply by reading the peak at its frequency. We test this directly on the bench. At each position in the swept area we take one recording and read the SNR at both 150 and 200\,Hz from it, and Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq} maps each beacon's SNR across the area. Switching the controller's attention from one beacon to the other is then only a matter of choosing which peak to read, a selection made in software at no extra sensing cost, so we do not test it separately.

The result confirms that the two beacons are recovered independently and simultaneously. Each lamp is modulated at a known, preset frequency, so the recovered fields serve as a check against that ground truth rather than as its source. As the top row of Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq} shows, each beacon's SNR field rises to a clear peak at its own lamp, and because the two lamps stand at opposite ends of the area the two fields peak in opposite corners, which confirms that each reading belongs to its own beacon. The per-sensor magnitudes $S_n$ that form these fields are the same signals from which the bearing and the gradient of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} are both built, so the separation demonstrated here carries through to both of the controller's directional cues, not to the light map alone.

The bottom row of Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq} shows why the controller navigates on SNR rather than on the raw signal magnitude. The magnitude fields are markedly noisier than the SNR fields above them, for two reasons. First, they carry the gain differences between the sixteen individual photodiodes, since each sensor converts the same incident light into a slightly different magnitude. Second, they carry the stray 144\,Hz light from the monitors, which sits only 6\,Hz from the 150\,Hz beacon, close enough that its energy leaks into the 150\,Hz beacon's part of the spectrum and lifts the field away from the lamp. Both effects corrupt the magnitude as a navigation cue: the first distorts the relative readings across the array, and the second adds signal where no beacon is present.

The SNR fields are clean because the SNR divides each peak by the local noise floor, the median magnitude of the spectrum away from the beacon, as defined in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}. This division normalises away both confounds. The per-sensor gain spread cancels because a sensor's gain scales its peak and its noise floor by the same factor, so their ratio is independent of the gain. The broad monitor wash is suppressed because its energy raises the noise floor that the ratio divides out, rather than lifting the beacon's peak alone. This is the same reasoning that motivated the SNR pipeline of Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}, seen here directly in the data: the 150\,Hz field still carries a faint trace of the monitor interference, but far less than the magnitude field, and well within what the controller's SNR gate tolerates.

The separation seen here does not depend on the prototype's particular sensing. The flight board senses with eight photodiodes at 500\,Hz rather than the prototype's sixteen at 1600\,Hz, but the result depends on neither number. It follows only from reading each beacon in its own frequency bin, which holds for any sensor count and any sample rate that keeps the beacons below the Nyquist limit, as the flight pipeline of Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} ensures. The flights confirm this with a different pair of frequencies, modulating the two beacons at 150 and 170\,Hz rather than 150 and 200\,Hz and separating them just as cleanly, which shows that the exact frequencies do not matter. The only constraint is that no beacon falls on another's harmonic: a square-wave beacon carries energy at its odd harmonics as well as at its fundamental, and a fundamental that landed on another beacon's harmonic would add energy to the wrong bin. This is exactly the spacing scheme of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}.

\section{Sequential Multi-Target Traverse}

With the sensing confirmed, we turn to the complete system and ask whether the drone visits several beacons in turn in real flight. Sequencing the beacons is the task of the state machine of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}. It runs its searching, aligning, approaching, and dwelling phases once per beacon, and advances to the next beacon on arrival. Arrival is registered when the overall SNR rises above an arrival threshold, reached only close to a beacon. This threshold is tuned per flight, because the SNR attained at a beacon depends on the ambient light and the lamp brightness of the session, both of which vary between flights, as Section~\ref{sec:ambient} shows. We fly the two missions described above on the CrazyFlie, with the beacons modulated at 150 and 170\,Hz, and record both the drone's path and the controller's internal state throughout. Each mission is a single demonstration flight, so these results show that the traversal works rather than measuring how often it succeeds over many trials, which we leave to future work.

Figure~\ref{fig:traverse-2wp} shows the two-waypoint flight. The upper panel plots the path, which reaches the first beacon and then the second. Each beacon's path is drawn over the light map the drone builds for that beacon as it flies, whose cells hold the SNR measured there. The map is rebuilt from scratch for each target, as Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} describes, so that the position drift accumulated while approaching one beacon is discarded with its map rather than carried into the next. This is why each panel shows only the cells gathered on the way to its own beacon. The lower panel tracks the controller's state and the active waypoint over time. The state steps through searching, aligning, approaching, and dwelling, and the waypoint index advances by one each time the drone arrives and dwells. The overall SNR, taken as the maximum across the eight channels as in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}, climbs as the drone closes on each beacon and drops back at each handover, when the drone turns toward a target it is still far from. Together these confirm that the controller sequences cleanly from one beacon to the next.

The three-waypoint flight in Figure~\ref{fig:traverse-3wp} extends the mission to a longer sequence, and it also exposes a limitation in the current state machine. The drone sequences correctly through the first two beacons, the state machine running its full cycle for each and the waypoint index advancing as before. On the final beacon it approaches and the overall SNR climbs to about 39, comfortably above the arrival threshold, as the SNR trace shows, so the drone does reach the beacon. The state machine, however, fails to latch this arrival: the state trace stays in the approaching phase even as the SNR passes the threshold, which we attribute to a timing error in the transition logic that prevents it from registering the arrival and settling into the dwelling phase. The drone therefore keeps flying instead of stopping, drifts out of the beacon's narrow light cone, and the SNR collapses into a low region from which the beacon is no longer in view. This is a defect in the arrival logic rather than a failure of the navigation approach, since the sequencing itself is sound for the bulk of the mission, and correcting it is left to future work.
