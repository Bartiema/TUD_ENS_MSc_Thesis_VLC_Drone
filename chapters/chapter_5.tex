\section{Real-World Implementation}

In the real world, ambient light and background illumination corrupt the raw intensity used in simulation, so the system instead measures the SNR at each beacon's modulation frequency to isolate the target light.

A 500\,Hz hardware low-pass filter is populated on the PCB to prevent motor noise and reflection artefacts---which occur at frequencies above the Nyquist limit of the ADC---from aliasing into the FFT and corrupting the SNR estimate.

Beyond the base algorithm's existing DC removal and Hamming window, three additional filters were introduced: block-based estimation, noise floor estimation, and per-channel magnitude IIR filters, to improve signal stability during flight.

\section{Signal Pipeline Validation}

\subsection{Filter Validation}

\textit{Question: are the hardware low-pass filter and the additional software filters necessary, and what effect does each have on signal stability during flight?}

The necessity of the 500\,Hz hardware low-pass filter is shown by comparing SNR measurements taken with and without it, and comparing measurements with and without the software filters then shows the reduction in noise and signal variance each stage contributes.

\subsection{SNR Field Mapping}

\textit{Question: does the SNR field in the real room form a navigable gradient toward the light source?}

Dedicated data-gathering flights construct spatial heatmaps of the SNR field, confirming that the signal increases toward the beacon so that a navigable gradient exists in the real environment.

\subsection{Effect of Ambient Lighting}

\textit{Question: how does ambient room lighting affect the SNR field and navigability?}

Comparative flights with the room lights on and off reveal how much background illumination degrades the SNR signal and how well the modulation-based filtering resists it.

\section{Obstacle Avoidance}

\subsection{Simulation}

\textit{Question: can the fused controller navigate around obstacles that the bearing-only controller cannot handle?}

In simulation the fused controller navigates around an obstacle that defeats the bearing-only baseline, with the state machine entering the recovering phase to follow the gradient out of the occluded region.

\subsection{Real-World Flight}

\textit{Question: does the gradient-based map let the drone navigate around a physical obstacle to reach the light source?}

A flight with a physical obstacle between the drone's start and the beacon shows that the fused controller routes around the obstruction on the CrazyFlie using its accumulated light map.

\section{Generalisation to a Blimp Platform}

\textit{Question: does the approach generalise beyond the platforms validated here?}

Although our own validation was carried out in simulation and on the CrazyFlie~2.1, the bearing and gradient methods this work builds on have also been demonstrated on a lighter-than-air blimp, whose results indicate that obstacle avoidance and multi-beacon navigation extend to that platform as well~\cite{}.
