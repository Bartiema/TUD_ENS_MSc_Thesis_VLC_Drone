\chapter{Multi-Beacon Experiments}
\label{chp:multibeacon}

One of the central aims of this thesis is to reach several modulated light beacons in succession, which the systems proposed in Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} do not provide. We validate this extended system in two stages, each answering a separate question. The first asks whether the photodiode array can distinguish two beacons by their modulation frequencies. We test this with a stationary bench setup, using a fixed test rig that does not fly and is therefore free from flight-induced motion noise and motor noise. The second asks whether the complete fused controller can guide the drone to multiple beacons in sequence. Testing the sensing stage first in a stationary setting ensures that any failure observed in real flight cannot be attributed to the system's inability to separate the beacons.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:multibeacon-setup}

In the first stage, the bench study uses the original Teensy~4.1 prototype from Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} rather than the final sensing board, because the prototype is better suited for a pure sensing test. It carries its own processor and is not constrained by the size, weight, and power limits of the micro-drone, allowing it to sample faster and log data continuously over longer sessions. Its sixteen photodiodes sample at 1600\,Hz, well above the flight board's 500\,Hz, so both beacon frequencies remain well within the sampled band.

To map each beacon across space, we move the Teensy~4.1 board by hand across a regular grid with $0.2,m^2$ spacing, covering an area of roughly $0.5,m^2$, and record one measurement at each grid cell. Moving the board by hand removes the drone's own motion and vibration from the experiment, so the recovered fields reflect the sensing performance alone. This procedure also produces a dense and repeatable static light-intensity map for each beacon, which we use to evaluate how well the sensing pipeline separates and measures the beacon signals.


Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-setup} shows the arrangement. Two lamps, modulated at 150 and 200\,Hz, stand at opposite ends of the experiemnt area. The room remains dark so only the light from computer monitors at the back of the bench introduces minor noise. The monitor runs at full brightness about a metre away and flicker at 144\,Hz, an interferer we return to in Section~\ref{sec:freq-separation}.

The second stage flies the full system on the Crazyflie~2.1, carrying the redesigned eight-photodiode board described in Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} and running the fused controller and state machine introduced in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}. Two beacons, now modulated at 150 and 170\,Hz, are placed in an open room. The drone takes off from a fixed origin and is given a mission that visits the light beacons in sequence. Figure~\ref{fig:flight-setup} shows the layout: the two lamps are placed a few metres ahead of the take-off point, with one roughly straight ahead and the other to the left, so reaching them in sequence requires the drone to make a substantial heading change between targets. The figure provides an indicative reference for the room arrangement rather than an exact measurement, so its distances should not be interpreted as ground-truth values. We evaluate the system with two missions, one traversing two target beacons and the other traversing three, to test the platform under both short and long navigation sequences.

\section{Separating Beacons by Frequency}
\label{sec:freq-separation}

The multi-beacon scheme mentioned in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} depends on the sensors on the Teensy prototype measuring every beacon. A single frequency analysis of a photodiode signal can then detect multiple beacons in parallel rather than one at a time, and the controller can then selects the current target beacon by reading the peak magnitude at that beacon's modulation frequency. We test this directly on the static bench. At each position, we record one sample window and compute the SNR at both 150 and 200\,Hz from the same data. Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq-150hz-snr}\&\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq-200z-snr} visualizes each beacon's SNR across the area. Switching the controller's attention from one beacon to another is then only a matter of choosing which frequency magnitude to use. This choice is made in software and adds no extra sensing cost, so we do not test it separately.

The result confirms that the two beacons are recovered independently and at the same time. As the top row of Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq} shows, each beacon's SNR field rises to a clear peak at its own lamp's positions. Because the two lamps stand at opposite ends of the area, the two SNR maps have peak value in opposite corners, which confirms that each reading belongs to its own beacon.

The bottom row of Figure~\ref{fig:teensy-dualfreq} shows why the controller should navigate using SNR rather than raw signal magnitude. The magnitude fields are much noisier than the SNR fields above them for two reasons. First, they include gain differences across the sixteen photodiodes, since each sensor produces a slightly different voltage response even under the same light exposure. Second, they include stray 144\,Hz light from the monitors, which lies only 6\,Hz away from the 150\,Hz beacon. This is close enough for some of its energy to leak into the 150\,Hz beacon bin, creating a spurious response that can be mistaken for the target beacon. Both effects explain why we use the frequency-domain SNR rather than raw light intensity: gain differences distort the relative readings across the array, while nearby interference adds signal where no beacon is present.


The SNR fields stay clean despite the same two effects, because the SNR divides a beacon's peak by the local noise floor, the median magnitude of the spectrum away from the beacon, as defined in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}. Dividing by this floor removes both the per-sensor gain differences and the monitor interference. The gain differences cancel because a sensor's gain scales its peak and its noise floor by the same factor, so their ratio is independent of the gain. The monitor interference is suppressed because it lifts the noise floor and the beacon's peak together, so dividing one by the other cancels most of its effect. This is the same reasoning that motivated the SNR pipeline of Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}, seen here directly in the data. The 150\,Hz SNR field still carries a faint trace of the monitor interference, but far less than the magnitude field, and well within what the controller's SNR gate tolerates.

The frequency separation demonstrated here is not specific to the prototype. The flight board reproduces it with only eight photodiodes at 500\,Hz, using the different pair of 150 and 170\,Hz, as the traverses of the next section show. The frequencies themselves are a free choice. The only requirement is that no beacon's modulation frequency coincides with a harmonic of another, since a square-wave beacon also carries energy at its harmonics, as Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} explains.

\section{Sequential Multi-Target Traverse}
\label{sec:multibeacon-traverse}

With the sensing confirmed, we turn to the complete system and ask whether the drone visits several beacons in turn in real flight. Sequencing the beacons is the task of the state machine of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}. It runs its searching, aligning, approaching, and dwelling phases once per beacon, and advances to the next beacon on arrival. Arrival is registered when the overall SNR rises above an arrival threshold, reached only close to a beacon. We set this threshold once for the test environment, tuning it so the arrival condition triggers only near a beacon, and then hold it constant across these flights. We fly the two missions described above on the CrazyFlie, with the beacons modulated at 150 and 170\,Hz, and record both the drone's path and the controller's internal state throughout. Each mission is a single demonstration flight, so these results show that the traversal works rather than measuring how often it succeeds over many trials, which we leave to future work.

Figure~\ref{fig:traverse-2wp} shows the two-waypoint flight. The upper panel plots the path, which reaches the first beacon and then the second. Each beacon's path is drawn over the light map the drone builds for that beacon as it flies, whose cells hold the SNR measured there. The map is rebuilt from scratch for each target, as Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} describes, so that the position drift accumulated while approaching one beacon is discarded with its map rather than carried into the next. This is why each panel shows only the cells gathered on the way to its own beacon. The lower panel tracks the controller's state and the active waypoint over time. The state steps through searching, aligning, approaching, and dwelling, and the waypoint index advances by one each time the drone arrives and dwells. The overall SNR, taken as the maximum across the eight channels as in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}, climbs as the drone closes on each beacon and drops back at each handover, when the drone turns toward a target it is still far from. Together these confirm that the controller sequences cleanly from one beacon to the next.

The three-waypoint flight in Figure~\ref{fig:traverse-3wp} extends the mission to a longer sequence. With only two physical beacons available, the third waypoint sends the drone back to the first beacon, the 150\,Hz lamp, so the mission runs 150\,Hz, then 170\,Hz, then 150\,Hz again. This longer run also exposes an implementation defect in the current state machine. The drone sequences correctly through the first two waypoints, the state machine running its full cycle for each and the waypoint index advancing as before. On the final waypoint it approaches the 150\,Hz beacon and the overall SNR climbs to about 39, comfortably above the arrival threshold, so the drone does reach it. The state machine, however, fails to latch the arrival. The state trace stays in the approaching phase even as the SNR passes the threshold, which we attribute to a timing error in the transition logic. The drone therefore keeps flying instead of stopping. It drifts out of the beacon's narrow light cone, and the SNR collapses into a region where the beacon is no longer in view. The defect lies in the arrival logic, not in the navigation approach: the sequencing is sound for the bulk of the mission, and correcting it is left to future work.
