\chapter{Future Work}
\label{chp:futurework}

This chapter sets out the directions that would build on the thesis. We first consider improvements to the sensing hardware. We then turn to extensions of the navigation itself. We close with a characterisation and robustness study that would turn our demonstrations into a measured account of how well the approach works.

\section{Improving the Sensing Hardware}

The operating range is the most immediate target for a hardware redesign, since it is the main limitation set out in the conclusions of Chapter~\ref{chp:conclusions}. A transimpedance amplifier with adjustable or switched gain would let the front end follow the signal as the drone moves, backing off near a beacon where the photodiodes now saturate and amplifying far from it where the signal is weak, so the clean band widens at both ends. Higher-order band-pass filters tuned to the beacon band would help further, both by passing the modulation more selectively and by rejecting the ambient light that otherwise eats into the sensor's range, the effect measured in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}.

The blimp flights consistently showed a higher SNR than we obtained on the CrazyFlie, as noted in Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle}, with the same photodiode board mounted in the same place, which points to the airframe itself as the cause. We suspect the difference is the motors: a buoyant platform stays aloft without constantly driving them, so they run only when it moves and at far lower power, coupling much less vibration and electrical interference into the board than the CrazyFlie's always-spinning rotors. Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} identified exactly this motor disturbance as the main cost of flight. A sustained higher SNR would directly extend the usable sensing range, letting the drone pick out the beacon direction from further away, so confirming this cause and quantifying the gain would be worthwhile, ideally through a controlled side-by-side comparison of the two platforms carrying the same board.

The photodiode geometry itself is worth revisiting. The current horizontal ring, laid out as Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} describes, is deliberately chosen to keep overhead ambient light out of the sensors, but it pays for this by responding weakly to light arriving from above or below the plane. A geometry that spread the photodiodes over more of a sphere would sense those directions too, at the cost of letting in more ambient light, a trade-off a smarter front end could absorb. The same redesign would also support faster, more agile flight. Such flight tilts the drone and shifts where each photodiode points, so we currently keep it gentle and near-level to hold the bearing of Chapter~\ref{chp:background} valid, which is defined only in the horizontal plane. Actively compensating for the measured tilt would instead let the controller correct the bearing error that aggressive manoeuvres introduce.

\section{Extending the Navigation}

Lifting the navigation into three dimensions would let the drone seek beacons above or below its own height, rather than only in the plane it flies today. The algorithm side is the easier half, since the light map already generalises to a volume and the gradient fit to a third coordinate, as Chapter~\ref{chp:conclusions} sets out. The harder half is the hardware: resolving elevation as well as horizontal direction would need photodiodes spread over a sphere rather than the horizontal ring of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}, the same redesign raised under the sensing hardware above, so the two could be pursued together.

A broader range of obstacle environments would test the controller more thoroughly than the single box used in Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle}. Multiple obstacles, narrow corridors, or concave arrangements that could trap the gradient in a local maximum would each stress the map-based recovery of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} in a different way, and some may expose cases where following the stored gradient is not enough to leave an occluded region. Charting where the approach holds and where it breaks down would turn the demonstrations of this thesis into a fuller picture of its navigation performance.

\section{Characterisation and Robustness}

The controller exposes a number of tunable parameters, listed in Appendix~\ref{app:parameters}, and a systematic study of how each affects navigation would turn their current hand-tuned values into practical guidelines for new environments. This fits naturally with the controlled repeatability trials that Chapter~\ref{chp:conclusions} notes we did not run, since flying many missions under fixed conditions while varying one parameter at a time would measure both how reliably the controller succeeds and how that success depends on its settings. Together these would give the statistical picture that our demonstration flights, by design, do not.

The arrival conditions deserve a closer look before any real deployment. The controller registers arrival from a single SNR threshold, as the state machine of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} defines, and a longer mission in Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon} showed this can fail to latch. A more robust scheme would make the transition between beacons more dependable, for instance combining the signal level with the drone's estimated proximity, or requiring the condition to hold over several frames before the drone commits to it.
