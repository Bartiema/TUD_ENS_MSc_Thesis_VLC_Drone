\chapter{Future Work}
\label{chp:futurework}

This chapter sets out the directions that would build on this work. We first consider improvements to the sensing hardware. We then turn to extensions of the navigation itself. We close with a characterisation and robustness study that would turn our demonstrations into a measured account of how well the approach works.

\section{Improving the Sensing Hardware}

The operating range is the most immediate target for a hardware redesign, since it is the main limitation set out in the conclusions of Chapter~\ref{chp:conclusions}. A transimpedance amplifier with adjustable or switched gain would let the front end follow the signal as the drone moves. Near a beacon, where the photodiodes now saturate, it would back off. Far from it, where the signal is weak, it would amplify. The clean band would then widen at both ends. Higher-order band-pass filters tuned to the beacon band would help further. They would pass the modulation more selectively, and reject the ambient light that otherwise reduces the sensor's usable range, the effect measured in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}.

The motor-noise measurements of Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle} show the CrazyFlie's motors are the main cost of flight, and that vibration and electrical interference contribute in roughly equal measure. This opens a clear hardware direction. Soft-mounting the board or the motors to damp the vibration should recover the mechanical half of the loss, while better shielding and supply filtering would address the electrical half. The photodiode geometry itself is worth revisiting. The current horizontal ring, laid out as Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} describes, is deliberately chosen to keep overhead ambient light out of the sensors. But it pays for this by responding weakly to light arriving from above or below the plane. A geometry that spread the photodiodes over more of a sphere would sense those directions too, at the cost of letting in more ambient light. A more capable front end could accommodate that trade-off. The same redesign would also support faster, more agile flight and navigation in 3D. Such flight tilts the drone and shifts where each photodiode points. We therefore keep the flight gentle and near-level, to keep the bearing of Chapter~\ref{chp:background} valid, which is defined only in the horizontal plane. Actively compensating for the measured tilt would instead let the controller correct the bearing error that aggressive manoeuvres introduce.

\section{Extending the Navigation}

Resolving elevation as well as horizontal direction would need photodiodes spread over a sphere, rather than the horizontal ring of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}. This is the same redesign raised under the sensing hardware above, so the two could be pursued together.

A broader range of obstacle environments would test the controller more thoroughly than the single box used in Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle}. Multiple obstacles, narrow corridors, or concave arrangements that could trap the gradient in a local maximum would each stress the map-based recovery of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} in a different way. Some may expose cases where following the stored gradient is not enough to leave an occluded region. 

A further step would be to relax the static-environment assumption noted in Chapter~\ref{chp:conclusions}. A beacon or an obstacle that moves after the drone has mapped it would leave stale values in the light map. Handling this would require the controller to detect that a stored cell no longer matches the field and to refresh or discard it, rather than trusting every reading indefinitely. Charting where the approach holds and where it breaks down would turn the demonstrations of this thesis into a fuller picture of its navigation performance.

The way the controller fuses the bearing and the gradient is itself a target for refinement. At present it blends them with fixed, equal weights whenever both are valid, as the control output of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} defines. Otherwise it hands fully to whichever one the state machine still trusts. This gated hand-off treats each direction as either fully trusted or ignored, with nothing in between. A finer scheme would let the two weights $w_B$ and $w_G$ vary continuously with a per-input confidence, so the more reliable direction dominates the blended heading. The bearing already carries such a measure in the summed magnitude of the photodiode readings, which the controller uses as its validity score, and the gradient in the $R^2$ of its plane fit. Weighting each direction by its own measure would let the drone lean on the map where the bearing is weak, such as deep in an obstacle's shadow, and on the bearing where it is strong. This is smoother than switching between the two at a hard threshold.

\section{Characterisation and Robustness}

The controller exposes a number of tunable parameters, listed in Appendix~\ref{app:parameters}. A systematic study of how each affects navigation would turn their current hand-tuned values into practical guidelines for new environments. This fits naturally with the controlled repeatability trials that Chapter~\ref{chp:conclusions} notes we did not run. Flying many missions under fixed conditions, while varying one parameter at a time, would measure both how reliably the controller succeeds and how that success depends on its settings. Together these would give the statistical picture that our demonstration flights, by design, do not.

The arrival conditions deserve a closer look before any real deployment. The controller registers arrival from a single SNR threshold, as the state machine of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} defines. A longer mission in Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon} shows this can fail to latch. A more robust scheme would make the transition between beacons more dependable. It could combine the signal level with the drone's estimated proximity, or require the condition to hold over several frames before the drone commits to it.
