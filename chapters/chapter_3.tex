\section{The Original Prototype}

Meeting the size, mass, and power limits of a micro-drone requires hardware designed specifically for that platform, which the first attempt did not yet achieve. We initially realised the bearing and gradient algorithms on a prototype built around a Teensy~4.1 microcontroller \todo{Not true, some of the parts of the algorithms were tested on the teensy, but never built for it. It was a prototyping and testing board always.}. This prototype read the photodiodes correctly and served its purpose as a proof of concept, demonstrating that a ring of photodiodes can recover a bearing toward a light source, but it was never intended to fly. It was assembled from three separate parts: the Teensy that ran the algorithms, a circular carrier board that distributed power and provided sixteen connectors, and sixteen small boards that each held a single photodiode and plugged into the carrier. This modular construction was convenient to assemble, but its size, mass, and power draw placed the prototype far beyond the budget of a micro-drone, and it carried considerably more computation than the navigation algorithms require.

The size and mass of the prototype followed from the components it was built from. Each photodiode was supplied as a self-contained module, the Texas Instruments OPT101, which integrates a photodiode and its transimpedance amplifier in a single package. Such integrated modules are convenient to wire, but their packaging occupies far more board area than the bare components would. The carrier accommodated sixteen of these modules, twice the eight that Huang et al.\ found sufficient to recover a reliable bearing~\cite{huang2026lta}, as Chapter~\ref{chp:background} sets out\todo{What is the pupose of the "as Chapter 2 sets out"?}. The Teensy~4.1, in turn, is a general-purpose microcontroller whose processing capacity far exceeds the modest demand of sampling eight slowly varying light signals. \section{Design Requirements}

The redesigned board had to retain the sensing principle proven by the prototype while overcoming its shortcomings and fitting within the budget of the CrazyFlie~2.1, the micro-drone this work targets. Section~\ref{sec:platform} derived that budget from the platform: of the roughly 15~grams the CrazyFlie can lift, the FlowDeck~V2 odometry deck already claims about 2~grams, leaving on the order of 13~grams for the sensing board, which must additionally fit within the drone's 44~millimetre frame and draw only the current its small battery can supply. Table~\ref{tab:requirements} summarises these requirements together with the figures the finished board achieves, and every design decision below is shaped by the need to stay within them.

        Photodiodes        & 8                     & 8 \\

Three design decisions follow directly from this budget. First, the board carries eight photodiodes rather than the prototype's sixteen, the number Huang et al.\ found sufficient to recover a reliable bearing~\cite{huang2026lta}, which halves both the sensor count and the board area they occupy. Second, it replaces the bulky integrated modules of the prototype with discrete surface-mount photodiode and amplifier components, which provide the same function in far less area and mass. Third, it carries no microcontroller of its own. Rather than duplicating the prototype's Teensy, the board offloads all computation to the CrazyFlie's existing processor, so it only has to sense and digitise the light and spends neither mass nor power on a processor the platform already provides.

\section{Circuit Design}

The circuit must recover a measurable voltage from each photodiode's faint current and present all eight to the flight controller in a form that can be compared channel against channel. Each of the eight photodiodes therefore feeds a transimpedance amplifier, which turns the small current the photodiode generates into a voltage the rest of the circuit can read, the standard way of reading a photodiode that Graeme sets out in detail~\cite{graeme1996photodiode}. A photodiode generates a current proportional to the light falling on it, but this current is far too small to measure directly, so the amplifier both converts it to a voltage and scales it into a useful range. The eight amplifiers come from shared quad operational-amplifier packages, chosen for their low noise and rail-to-rail output, which lets each amplifier swing its output across almost the full supply range and so use the converter's entire measuring span. \todo{This paragraph feels ordered wierd, as if it starts over half way through. The converter scans all eight channels in turn and sends the readings to the CrazyFlie over a Serial Peripheral Interface (SPI) bus, a standard four-wire link for short-range communication between chips, here carrying a clock, a chip-select, and a data line in each direction.\todo{Why are we explaining the details of SPI? Because both the bearing and the gradient compare the eight channels against one another, the readings must stay consistent and comparable across channels, which two further components ensure. A precision voltage reference holds a stable, accurate output voltage despite changes in temperature and supply. It sets both the level the amplifier outputs swing around and the converter's full-scale range, so that a given light intensity always maps to the same digital value. A low-dropout regulator derives a clean, constant supply from a slightly higher and noisier input\todo{What input?}. It conditions the power the board draws from the CrazyFlie, so that switching noise on that supply does not corrupt the faint photodiode signals. \section{PCB Layout}

With the circuit designed, the layout turns it into a physical board the drone can carry, and two concerns shape it: even angular coverage and tolerance of the drone's electrical noise. We place the eight photodiodes at equal 45\textdegree{} intervals around the board, so that together they cover the full 360\textdegree{} evenly. This even spacing matters because the bearing algorithm compares the photodiodes against one another to find the direction of the light, so any gap in the coverage would leave directions the ring senses poorly making it harder to estimate a bearing in that direction. The board's outline is shaped to carry each photodiode out to a point at its own angle, with the amplifiers and the converter grouped in the centre, as Figure~\ref{fig:new-board} shows. The board mounts on the CrazyFlie through the two expansion headers seen there, which carry its power, its ground, and the SPI bus, so it needs no wiring of its own.

The layout must also tolerate the electrically noisy environment of a flying drone. The motors draw large, rapidly switching currents that radiate electromagnetic interference, which can couple into the faint photodiode signals and corrupt the readings. To reduce this, the board carries a ground plane beneath the signal traces, which both shields the traces and gives the return currents a low-impedance path back to the supply. This follows the standard electromagnetic-compatibility practice that Ott sets out~\cite{ott2009emc}.

The finished board weighs approximately 6~grams and measures roughly 42~millimetres across, comfortably within the 13~gram mass limit and the 44~millimetre footprint it was designed to meet, as Table~\ref{tab:requirements} records. Before integrating the board with the drone, we verified it on its own. We connected the assembled board to a standalone Teensy microcontroller, used here only as a bench instrument rather than as the prototype's flight processor, and confirmed that each of the eight channels responded to light. With this basic check passed, any fault observed later in flight could be attributed to the integration or the software rather than to the board itself.
