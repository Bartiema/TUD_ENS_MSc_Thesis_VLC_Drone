\chapter{PCB Hardware Design}
\label{chp:hardware}

A central question of this thesis is whether a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone can carry the light sensing it needs on board. Thus, in this section we detail our onboard visible light sensing hardware design to meet with the retrictions. The bearing and gradient algorithms of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} both read from an array of photodiodes, so the platform needs a board that holds those photodiodes, converts the raw eletrical voltage into light intensity signals, and transfers the signal to drone's board for further process. The board must do all of this within the mass and size budget set in Section~\ref{sec:platform}. This chapter develops that board. We first examine an earlier Teensy-based prototype, which established the sensing principle but remained far too large and heavy to fly. We then derive the requirements the flight board must meet. Last, we present our board design with customized circuit and physical layout that satisfy these requirements. 

\section{The Original Prototype}

Meeting the size, mass, and power limits of a micro-drone requires hardware designed specifically for that platform. Our earlier prototype, built around a Teensy~4.1 microcontroller, did not meet these constraints because it was developed as a rapid algorithm testbed rather than as hardware for drone integration. We used it to develop and evaluate parts of the bearing and gradient algorithms, and to confirm the sensing principle that a ring of photodiodes can recover a bearing toward a light source. However, the prototype was never intended to be carried by a micro-drone. It consisted of three separate modules, including the Teensy that ran the algorithms, a circular carrier board that distributed power and provided 16 connectors, and 16 small boards that each held one photodiode and could be plugged into the carrier. This modular construction made the prototype convenient to assemble and modify, but its size, mass, and power draw are far beyond the budget of a micro-drone, while its computational capacity also exceeded what the navigation algorithms require.


The prototype’s size and mass were dominated by the photodiode modules it used. Each photodiode was a self-contained Texas Instruments OPT101 module, which integrates a photodiode and its transimpedance amplifier in a single package. Such integrated modules are convenient to wire, but their packaging occupies far more board area than a simple photodiode would. The carrier accommodated sixteen of these modules, twice the number later required by the flight board. The Teensy~4.1, meanwhile, is a general-purpose microcontroller whose processing capacity far exceeds the requirements of our experiment, which only needs to sample and process eight light signals. The prototype board itself was roughly 90~millimetres in diameter, more than twice the diameter of the drone board. The assembled prototype weighed about 44~grams, more than three times the 13~gram budget available for the sensing hardware. Figure~\ref{fig:old-prototype} shows a partially assembled prototype.

\section{Design Requirements}

The redesigned board must retain the sensing principle demonstrated by the prototype while overcoming its shortcomings and fitting within the budget of the Crazyflie~2.1, the micro-drone targeted in this work and introduced in Section~\ref{sec:platform}. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:platform}, the Crazyflie~2.1 can lift only roughly 15~grams of additional payload. Since the FlowDeck~V2 odometry deck already occupies about 2~grams of this budget, only 13~grams remain for the sensing board. The board's size must also fit within the drone’s 44~millimetre frame and draw only the current that its small battery can supply. Table~\ref{tab:requirements} summarises these requirements together with the final hardware profile we managed to achieve.

Three design decisions follow directly from this budget. First, the board carries eight photodiodes rather than the prototype's sixteen, the number Huang et al.\ found sufficient to recover a reliable bearing~\cite{huang2026lta}, which halves both the sensor count and the board area they occupy. Second, it replaces the bulky integrated modules of the prototype with a simple surface-mount photodiode and amplifier components, which provide the same function in far less area and mass as OPT101. Third, it carries no microcontroller of its own. Rather than duplicating the prototype's Teensy, the board offloads all computation to the CrazyFlie's existing processor, so it only has to sense and digitise the light and spends neither mass nor power on extra processor. 

A final set of constraints follows from the board’s mounting point, the Crazy-Flie’s expansion deck connector. The connector sets where the mounting headers sit, supplies the board’s power, and offers a choice of digital interfaces, such as I2C, UART, and the Serial Peripheral Interface (SPI), over which the board can return its readings. \section{Circuit Design}

The circuit must convert the light received by each photodiode into a digital value that the Crazyflie can read, and it must do so consistently across all eight photodiodes so that their readings can be compared. Figure~\ref{fig:signal-chain} shows the resulting signal chain. Each photodiode output is first passed through a transimpedance amplifier and an optional filter. A shared analogue-to-digital converter then samples all eight photodiodes' reading and sends the measurements to the Crazyflie. For these measurements to be meaningful, the ADC also requires a precise voltage reference, and the full signal chain must be powered by a regulated supply. We describe each stage in turn, together with the component chosen for it, as summarised in Table~\ref{tab:components}.

The chain begins at the photodiode, which converts incoming light into a small electrical current. Four requirements must be satisfied for our navigation task. First, the sensor must respond to the visible light spectrum rather than to other wavelengths, such as infrared. Second, it must respond fast enough to capture the beacon’s few-hundred-hertz on-off modulation. Third, it must provide a directional response, measuring a beacon more strongly when it faces the source than when the source lies off to the side, since the bearing is recovered from signal differences across sensors. Fourth, it must be small enough for eight sensors to fit within the board’s size budget. To meet these requirements, we choose the Vishay VEMD4200, a PIN photodiode in a $2\times1.25$,millimetre 0805 surface-mount package. Compared with phototransistors, another common light-sensing option, PIN photodiodes provide faster response and a more linear light-to-current relationship, whereas phototransistors often trade sensing speed and linearity for higher gain. The VEMD4200 has a spectral response matched to visible light, so it responds strongly to ordinary lamps. Its half-sensitivity angle of $\pm 55$\textdegree{} keeps each sensor directional while still providing a sufficiently wide field of view. As a result, neighbouring sensors observe different signal strengths as the beacon direction changes, preserving the contrast needed for bearing estimation.


The electrical current a Vishay VEMD4200 produces is far too small to measure directly, with usually only several $\mu$A, thus each channel reading must first be converted it to a electrical voltage and further scaled to a useful range, which is the standard transimpedance configuration that Graeme sets out in detail~\cite{graeme1996photodiode}. Two properties matter for the readings to stay comparable. The amplifier must add as little noise as possible to the raw reading, and its output reading range must span across the full voltage range the converter can process, so the converter's resolution is fully used rather than a fraction of it being used and the data being compressed. We use the Texas Instruments OPA4350, a low-noise operational amplifier with a rail-to-rail output, meaning its output can swing almost the whole way between its two supply voltages and so cover that range. A general-purpose amplifier would fall short on both counts, leaving part of the converter's range unused and adding more noise to the faint signal. The eight amplifier outputs are then digitised and passed to the Crazyflie. We use a single 12-bit analogue-to-digital converter, the Texas Instruments ADS7953, which scans all eight channels in sequence. This allows the board to collect one frame of eight photodiode readings and send it to the main drone board. Using one shared converter saves board area and power compared with placing one converter on each channel. It also avoids routing eight analogue signals through the drone connector, where they would be more exposed to electrical noise from the platform, including motor noise. The converter returns the readings over the SPI bus, and on the drone it repeats the scan at 500~Hz, fast enough to capture the beacons’ on-off modulation, as Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} details.


The converter measures each voltage against a reference, and any drift in that reference makes the same light yield different readings over time and between channels. We therefore supply a dedicated precision voltage reference, the Texas Instruments REF5025A, rather than relying on the supply rail, which varies with load and temperature. It holds a stable, accurate level that sets both the point the amplifier outputs swing around and the converter's full-scale range, so a given light intensity always maps to the same digital value. \textcolor{red}{The last sentence is confusing. it does not provide a lot what are the swing and converter's full-scale range, and it sure does not link to the conclusion.}

The analogue front end is sensitive to noise on its own supply, and the rail the board draws from the CrazyFlie carries switching noise from the rest of the drone. We condition it with a low-dropout regulator, the Texas Instruments TPS73133, which derives a cleaner, constant voltage from that higher and noisier rail. A low-dropout regulator is a linear regulator, so unlike a switching regulator it introduces no switching noise of its own, which would otherwise reach the photodiode signals. \textcolor{red}{this paragraph with the previous one is not that imporatant for me. so try to bring it up together and shortly. just mentioned why we need it and we put it is enough. no need to dig into the spec}

Finally, different deployment environments introduce different noise profiles. For example, outdoor settings may contain less high-frequency artificial lighting noise but stronger low-frequency variations from sunlight and shadows. Fixing one filter choice in the hardware would therefore tie the board to a specific environment. To keep the design adaptable, each photodiode channel includes an optional first-order filter between the amplifier and the converter. This hardware filter can be configured for the target environment by changing the resistor and capacitor values. A solder bridge and unpopulated component pads allow either a low-pass or high-pass filter to be installed as required by the target environments. As shown in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}, we use a low-pass filter to attenuate power-supply noise from the drone, which otherwise corrupts the light signal.

\section{PCB Layout}

With the circuit designed, the layout turns it into a physical board that the drone can carry. Two concerns shape this layout: even angular coverage and tolerance to the drone’s electrical noise. \textcolor{red}{I at first did not see what we do to the electrical noise. please merge this one with the next paragraph} We place the eight photodiodes at equal 45\textdegree{} intervals around the board so that, together, they sample the full 360\textdegree{} horizontal plane evenly. This spacing is important because the bearing algorithm compares the photodiode readings against one another to infer the light direction. Any large gap in coverage would leave some directions weakly sensed by the array, making the bearing estimate less reliable. The board outline extends each photodiode outward along its assigned angle, while the amplifiers and converter are grouped near the centre, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:new-board}. The board mounts directly onto the Crazyflie through the two expansion headers shown in the figure, which provide power, ground, and the SPI bus, so no additional wiring is required.

The layout must also tolerate the electrically noisy environment of a flying drone. The motors draw large, rapidly switching currents that radiate electromagnetic interference, which can couple into the faint photodiode signals and corrupt the readings. To reduce this, the board carries a ground plane beneath the signal traces, which both shields the traces and gives the return currents a low-impedance path back to the supply. This follows the standard electromagnetic-compatibility practice that Ott sets out~\cite{ott2009emc}.

The final board weighs approximately 6~grams and measures roughly 42~millimetres across, comfortably within the 13~gram mass limit and the 44~millimetre footprint it is designed to meet, as Table~\ref{tab:requirements} records. Before integrating the board with the drone, we verify it on its own. We connect the assembled board to a standalone Teensy microcontroller, used here only as a bench instrument rather than as the prototype's flight processor, and confirm that each of the eight channels responds to light. With this basic check passed, any fault observed later in flight can be attributed to the integration of the drone with the environment or the algorithm behavior rather than to the board itself.
