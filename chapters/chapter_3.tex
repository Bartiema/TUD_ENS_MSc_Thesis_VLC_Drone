\chapter{PCB Hardware Design}
\label{chp:hardware}

A central question of this thesis is whether a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone can carry the light sensing it needs on board. Thus, in this section we detail our onboard visible-light sensing hardware design to meet these restrictions. The bearing and gradient algorithms of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} both read from an array of photodiodes, so the platform needs a board that holds those photodiodes, converts the raw electrical voltage into light-intensity signals, and transfers the signal to the drone's board for further processing. The board must do all of this within the mass and size budget set in Section~\ref{sec:platform}. This chapter develops that board. We first examine an earlier Teensy-based prototype, which established the sensing principle but remained far too large and heavy to fly. We then derive the requirements the flight board must meet. Last, we present our board design with a customised circuit and physical layout that satisfy these requirements.

\section{The Original Prototype}

Meeting the size, mass, and power limits of a micro-drone requires hardware designed specifically for that platform. Our earlier prototype, built around a Teensy~4.1 microcontroller, did not meet these constraints because it was developed as a rapid algorithm testbed rather than as hardware for drone integration. We used it to develop and evaluate parts of the bearing and gradient algorithms, and to confirm the sensing principle that a ring of photodiodes can recover a bearing toward a light source. However, the prototype was never intended to be carried by a micro-drone. It consisted of three separate modules, including the Teensy that ran the algorithms, a circular carrier board that distributed power and provided sixteen connectors, and sixteen small boards that each held one photodiode and could be plugged into the carrier. This modular construction made the prototype convenient to assemble and modify, but its size, mass, and power draw are far beyond the budget of a micro-drone, while its computational capacity also exceeded what the navigation algorithms require.


The prototype's size and mass were dominated by the photodiode modules it used. Each photodiode was a self-contained Texas Instruments OPT101 module, which integrates a photodiode and its transimpedance amplifier in a single package. Such integrated modules are convenient to wire, but their packaging occupies far more board area than a simple photodiode would. The carrier accommodated sixteen of these modules, twice the number later required by the flight board. The Teensy~4.1, meanwhile, is a general-purpose microcontroller whose processing capacity far exceeds the requirements of our experiment, which only needs to sample and process eight light signals. The prototype board itself was roughly 90~millimetres in diameter, more than twice the diameter of the drone board. The assembled prototype weighed about 44~grams, more than three times the 13~gram budget available for the sensing hardware. Figure~\ref{fig:old-prototype} shows a partially assembled prototype.

\section{Design Requirements}

The redesigned board must retain the sensing principle proven by the prototype while overcoming its shortcomings and fitting within the budget of the CrazyFlie~2.1, the micro-drone this work targets and introduced in Section~\ref{sec:platform}. As mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:platform}, the CrazyFlie~2.1 can lift only about 15~grams of extra payload. The FlowDeck~V2 odometry deck already takes up about 2~grams, leaving only 13~grams for the sensing board. The board must additionally fit within the drone's 44~millimetre frame and draw only the current its small battery can supply. Table~\ref{tab:requirements} summarises these requirements together with the figures the finished board achieves.

Three design decisions follow directly from this budget. First, the board carries eight photodiodes rather than the prototype's sixteen, the number Huang et al.\ found sufficient to recover a reliable bearing~\cite{huang2026lta}, which halves both the sensor count and the board area they occupy. Second, it replaces the bulky integrated modules of the prototype with discrete surface-mount photodiode and amplifier components, which provide the same function in far less area and mass. Third, it carries no microcontroller of its own. Rather than duplicating the prototype's Teensy, the board offloads all computation to the CrazyFlie's existing processor, so it only has to sense and digitise the light and spends neither mass nor power on a processor the platform already provides.

A final set of constraints follows from the board's mounting point, the CrazyFlie's expansion deck connector. The connector sets where the mounting headers sit, supplies the board's power, and offers a choice of digital interfaces, such as I2C, UART, and the Serial Peripheral Interface, or SPI, over which the board can return its readings. The board therefore communicates with the CrazyFlie entirely through this connector, using SPI for the converter readout.

\section{Circuit Design}

The circuit needs to turn the light at each photodiode into a digital value the CrazyFlie can read, and must do so identically across all eight channels so that their readings can be compared. Figure~\ref{fig:signal-chain} shows the resulting signal chain. Each photodiode feeds a transimpedance amplifier and an optional filter, a shared analogue-to-digital converter then samples all eight channels and sends them to the CrazyFlie, and a precision reference and regulated supply support the chain throughout. We describe each stage in turn, together with the part chosen for it, which Table~\ref{tab:components} collects.

The chain begins at the photodiode, which converts incident light into a small electrical current. Four requirements follow from the task. First, the sensor must respond to the visible-light lamps the beacons are built from. Second, it must follow their few-hundred-hertz on-off modulation. Third, it must give a directional response, reading a beacon it faces more strongly than one off to the side, since the bearing is recovered from these differences between sensors. Fourth, it must be small enough that eight fit within the board's size budget. We use the Vishay VEMD4200, a PIN photodiode in a $2\times1.25$\,millimetre 0805 surface-mount package. Being a PIN photodiode, it is fast and linear, whereas a phototransistor would trade speed for gain, so it follows the modulation cleanly. Its spectral response is matched to the human eye and peaks in the visible band, so it responds to the ordinary lamps strongly. Its half-sensitivity angle of $\pm 55$\textdegree{} keeps each sensor directional while it still responds out to the sides. Neighbouring sensors therefore share sight of a beacon, without the array losing the contrast the bearing depends on.

The current a photodiode produces is far too small to measure directly, so each channel converts it to a voltage and scales it into a useful range, the standard transimpedance configuration that Graeme sets out in detail~\cite{graeme1996photodiode}. Two properties matter for the readings to stay comparable. The amplifier must add little noise of its own, and its output must be able to reach across the full voltage range the converter measures, so the converter's resolution is used over its whole span rather than a fraction of it. We use the Texas Instruments OPA4350, a low-noise operational amplifier with a rail-to-rail output, meaning its output can swing almost the whole way between its two supply voltages and so cover that range. A general-purpose amplifier would satisfy neither requirement, leaving part of the converter's range unused and adding more noise to the faint signal. The OPA4350 also carries four amplifiers in one package, so two packages serve all eight channels in place of eight separate devices.

The eight amplifier outputs are then digitised and passed to the CrazyFlie. We use a single 12-bit analogue-to-digital converter, the Texas Instruments ADS7953, that scans all eight channels in turn, so one chip digitises the whole array. This saves the board area and power of a converter per channel, and avoids digitising on the flight controller, which would carry eight analogue signals across the connector and into the drone's electrical noise. Its 12-bit resolution is far finer than the noise in the photodiode signal, so quantisation is not what limits the reading. The converter returns the readings over the SPI bus, and on the drone it repeats the scan at 500~Hz, fast enough to capture the beacons' on-off modulation, as Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} details.

The converter measures each voltage against a reference, and any drift in that reference makes the same light yield different readings over time and between channels. We therefore supply a dedicated precision voltage reference, the Texas Instruments REF5025A, rather than relying on the supply rail, which varies with load and temperature. It holds a stable, accurate level that sets both the point the amplifier outputs swing around and the converter's full-scale range, so a given light intensity always maps to the same digital value.

The analogue front end is sensitive to noise on its own supply, and the rail the board draws from the CrazyFlie carries switching noise from the rest of the drone. We condition it with a low-dropout regulator, the Texas Instruments TPS73133, which derives a cleaner, constant voltage from that higher and noisier rail. A low-dropout regulator is a linear regulator, so unlike a switching regulator it introduces no switching noise of its own, which would otherwise reach the photodiode signals.

Finally, the noise a board must reject depends on where it is deployed, so fixing one filter into the design would tie the board to a single environment. To keep it adaptable, each channel leaves room for an optional first-order filter between the amplifier and the converter, a stage that passes frequencies on one side of a chosen cut-off and attenuates the other. A solder bridge or a pair of unpopulated pads lets a low-pass or high-pass filter be fitted as a deployment requires. Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} uses these pads, populating them with an anti-aliasing low-pass filter for flight.

\section{PCB Layout}

With the circuit designed, the layout turns it into a physical board the drone can carry, and two concerns shape it: even angular coverage and tolerance of the drone's electrical noise. We place the eight photodiodes at equal 45\textdegree{} intervals around the board, so that together they cover the full 360\textdegree{} evenly. This even spacing matters because the bearing algorithm compares the photodiodes against one another to find the direction of the light, so any gap in the coverage would leave directions that the ring senses poorly, making it harder to estimate a bearing toward a light arriving from there. The board's outline is shaped to carry each photodiode out to a point at its own angle, with the amplifiers and the converter grouped in the centre, as Figure~\ref{fig:new-board} shows. The board mounts on the CrazyFlie through the two expansion headers seen there, which carry its power, its ground, and the SPI bus, so it needs no wiring of its own.

The layout must also tolerate the electrically noisy environment of a flying drone. The motors draw large, rapidly switching currents that radiate electromagnetic interference, which can couple into the faint photodiode signals and corrupt the readings. To reduce this, the board carries a ground plane beneath the signal traces, which both shields the traces and gives the return currents a low-impedance path back to the supply. This follows the standard electromagnetic-compatibility practice that Ott sets out~\cite{ott2009emc}.

The finished board weighs approximately 6~grams and measures roughly 42~millimetres across, comfortably within the 13~gram mass limit and the 44~millimetre footprint it is designed to meet, as Table~\ref{tab:requirements} records. Before integrating the board with the drone, we verify it on its own. We connect the assembled board to a standalone Teensy microcontroller, used here only as a bench instrument rather than as the prototype's flight processor, and confirm that each of the eight channels responds to light. With this basic check passed, we can attribute any fault observed later in flight to the integration or the software rather than to the board itself.
