\chapter{Background \& Related Work}
\label{chp:background}

This chapter sets the work in context. We first review how drones navigate indoors without GPS, and locate the gap this thesis closes. We then introduce the drone platform whose limited budget shapes the design. We follow with the visible-light theory the later chapters rely on. We close with the navigation algorithms of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} that this work extends, and the simulator in which it is developed.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

Small unmanned aircraft increasingly perform tasks once carried out by people, such as inspecting structures, monitoring the environment, and tracking stock in warehouses~\cite{floreano2015drones}. Many of these tasks take place indoors.\todo{Citation needed: a source or figure for how much drone work is indoors / GPS-denied (Harry's ``X\% of applications'').} Indoors, a drone cannot rely on GPS, which is blocked or too coarse to navigate by~\cite{zafari2019indoor}. It must instead find its heading or position from other signals, either carried on board or already present in the space.

Indoor navigation methods differ mainly in the signal they use. We group them into radio, acoustic, and vision based approaches, and judge each by two costs that matter for a small drone: the infrastructure it adds to the space, and the sensing and computation it asks the drone to carry. Table~\ref{tab:methods-comparison} summarises the comparison, and the rest of this section develops it.

The radio methods locate the drone against fixed anchors placed in the environment. Ultra-wideband anchors measure how long a pulse takes to travel between an anchor and the drone, which places the drone to within tens of centimetres~\cite{zafari2019indoor}.\todo{Citation needed: a UWB-specific reference for this accuracy claim.} WiFi fingerprinting instead matches the signal strengths the drone hears against a map of measurements surveyed in advance, which is coarser, on the order of a metre~\cite{zafari2019indoor}. WiFi therefore covers a building on hardware it already has, whereas ultra-wideband is more accurate but needs dedicated anchors. Acoustic positioning follows the same anchor-based principle with ultrasonic beacons, trading radio for sound.\todo{Citation needed: an acoustic indoor-positioning reference.} All three share one cost in our setting: they require infrastructure installed or surveyed before the first flight, beyond what the space already contains.

Vision takes a different route and builds its own reference as it flies. A camera captures a stream of images, and visual simultaneous localisation and mapping, or visual SLAM, tracks features across those images to estimate both the drone's motion and a map of its surroundings, so it needs no anchors at all~\cite{murartal2017orbslam2}. The cost moves onto the drone instead. Visual SLAM needs a capable camera and enough on-board computation to process the images in real time, which a micro-drone with little payload and power cannot provide.

These approaches leave a gap for a small drone. Radio and dense visible-light positioning demand infrastructure added to every space, whereas vision demands a camera and computation the drone cannot carry. Light closes both gaps at once. The spaces a drone works in are already lit, so an ordinary lamp becomes a navigation beacon once it is modulated, and nothing is added to the room but the modulation itself~\cite{pathak2015vlc}. The drone then carries only a light sensor, and reading that sensor is light enough for its onboard processor.

Visible-light positioning takes the accurate end of this idea. It triangulates a photodiode-equipped receiver from several modulated ceiling luminaires, each identified by its own modulation, and reaches centimetre-level accuracy indoors~\cite{kuo2014luxapose, zhuang2018vlp}. Triangulation needs at least three sources in view at once, so the method assumes a dense, fixed grid of known lights. That grid is costly to provide. The luminaires must be installed where their light overlaps and surveyed to precise known positions, and many spaces cannot arrange their lighting so regularly, as street or aisle lighting shows. The accuracy is real, but it depends on exactly the dense infrastructure a small drone is meant to avoid.

A single beacon places a far smaller demand on the space. With only one source a drone cannot triangulate a position, since triangulation needs three. It can, however, recover the direction of the light, and a direction is enough to fly to that light and act there. This is a lighter approach rather than a compromise. It keeps the existing-infrastructure advantage of light, adds only one modulated lamp, and asks nothing of the drone beyond a light sensor. Huang et al.\ demonstrate such single-beacon navigation on a micro-drone, guiding it to a modulated light with no addition to the space beyond that one modulation~\cite{huang2026lta}.

On their own, the base methods we build on reach a single beacon in open space but go no further. They keep no lasting memory of the light field, so a drone running them cannot route around an obstacle that blocks its view of the beacon, and they do not by themselves visit several beacons in turn. Providing this missing navigation, with a custom sensing board, a fused controller, and a light map the drone builds as it flies, is the subject of this thesis. We describe the base methods in detail in Section~\ref{sec:algorithms}.

\section{Drone Platform}
\label{sec:platform}

The real-world experiments in this thesis run on a Bitcraze CrazyFlie~2.1, a quadrotor small and light enough to rest in the palm of a hand~\cite{crazyflie21}. Its weight, size, and power budget are what rule out the heavier methods reviewed above. A capable camera and the companion computer that visual navigation needs, or the fixed anchors and prior survey that radio and dense visible-light positioning need, all exceed what this platform can carry or set up. The budget is therefore the reason the navigation rests on light sensing alone, and it sets the requirements the photodiode board of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} must meet.

The margins are small. The CrazyFlie~2.1 lifts roughly 15~grams of payload, of which the FlowDeck~V2 optical-flow deck the drone also carries takes about 2~grams, leaving on the order of 13~grams for the sensing board~\cite{crazyflie21, flowdeckv2}. The board must also fit within the drone's frame, a diagonal of at most 44~millimetres, and draw only the small current the drone's battery can spare. There is no room for a sensor or processor the navigation does not strictly need.

The CrazyFlie also reports an onboard estimate of its own position and heading, on which the light map of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} is built.

\section{Visible Light Communication \& Sensing}

Visible light communication modulates a light faster than the eye can follow~\cite{pathak2015vlc}. A person sees an ordinary, steady light, but a photodiode sampling it quickly enough reads the modulation as a signal. The same lamp therefore serves at once as illumination and as a beacon the drone can recognise. In this work each beacon is a light switched on and off at a fixed frequency, which is enough to tell it apart from steady background light and from beacons at other frequencies.

How strongly a beacon appears at the drone depends on more than distance. Two angles also matter: the angle at which the light leaves the source, and the angle at which it meets the photodiode. Each contributes a cosine factor, and the intensity falls with the square of the distance, a relationship known as the Lambertian model~\cite{komine2004vlc}. Figure~\ref{fig:lambertian} shows the geometry. Writing $\alpha$ for the angle off the source's axis, $\beta$ for the angle of incidence at the sensor, and $d$ for the distance, the received intensity is proportional to $\cos^{m}\!\alpha \, \cos\beta / d^{2}$, where the order $m$ describes how narrowly the source beams its light.

This dependence on angle as well as distance makes a single intensity reading ambiguous. A strong reading can mean a nearby source seen from the side or a distant source seen head-on, and one photodiode cannot tell the two apart, as Figure~\ref{fig:ambiguity} illustrates. This ambiguity is the reason one light cannot fix an absolute position, and the reason positioning systems combine many overlapping sources. We avoid it by treating the light as a direction to follow rather than a position to solve for.

\subsection{The Light Signal}
\label{sec:lightsignal}

The drone senses this light with photodiodes. A photodiode produces a small electrical current proportional to the light striking it, and the board converts that current into a number. The navigation reads one number per photodiode, which we call the per-sensor light signal and write $S_n$ for sensor $n$. It measures how strongly that sensor sees the beacon. How $S_n$ is recovered differs between the two settings this thesis uses. On the real drone it is extracted from a noisy voltage, as Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} describes, and in simulation it is read directly from a modelled sensor, as Section~\ref{sec:simulation} describes. For the algorithms that follow, it is enough that each sensor reports one value for how strongly it sees the beacon.

\section{Algorithms from Huang et al.}
\label{sec:algorithms}

The navigation in this thesis builds on the light-based methods of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}. They propose three algorithms for guiding a drone with a single light, which trade sensing hardware against how directly the drone reaches the source. We describe the two this thesis builds on, the bearing and the gradient, in detail, and the third, an extremum-seeking method, only to set it aside. Table~\ref{tab:algorithms-comparison} sets all three side by side.

\subsection{Bearing Angle Estimation}
\label{sec:bearing}

The first method, which Huang et al.\ call bearing angle guidance, recovers the direction of the light from an array of photodiodes~\cite{huang2026lta}. Each photodiode in the array faces a different, known direction, and responds most strongly to light arriving from where it faces. When a beacon lies to one side, the photodiodes facing it report a stronger signal than those facing away. Comparing the photodiodes against one another therefore reveals the direction of the strongest light.

The bearing combines these per-sensor readings into a single direction. Each photodiode contributes a vector pointing the way it faces, weighted by how strongly it sees the beacon, and the vectors are summed. Figure~\ref{fig:bearing-vectorsum} shows this for a ring of eight photodiodes. Using the per-sensor light signal $S_n$ of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal} and writing $\theta_n$ for the direction sensor $n$ faces, the bearing $\psi_B$ is the angle of the weighted sum,
\begin{equation}
\psi_B = \operatorname{atan2}\!\left( \sum_{n} S_n \sin\theta_n,\; \sum_{n} S_n \cos\theta_n \right).
\label{eq:bearing}
\end{equation}
The sensor facing the beacon contributes the most, so the sum points toward the strongest light. Spacing eight photodiodes evenly around the ring covers every direction in the horizontal plane, so whichever way the beacon lies, at least one sensor faces it. The result is a direction in that plane, which suits a drone flying at a roughly fixed height.

Recovering a direction by comparing an array of sensors is well established in radio. Amplitude-comparison direction finders estimate a transmitter's bearing from its strength across several differently pointed antennas, taking the arctangent of the combined responses much as we do here, with photodiodes and light in place of antennas and radio~\cite{iqbal2020amplitude}.

The bearing's strength is that it is immediate and accurate. It points at the beacon from a single set of readings, with no need to move or explore, which makes it an excellent reactive heading~\cite{huang2026lta}. Its limitation is that it has no memory. The bearing is computed afresh from each new set of readings and keeps no record of the field the drone has passed through. It cannot represent an obstacle between the drone and the beacon, and it cannot recall where the light was once the drone can no longer see it. A drone steering on the bearing alone flies straight at the strongest light, with no way to route around anything in its path.

\subsection{Gradient-Based Source Seeking}

Where the bearing is reactive and memoryless, gradient-based source seeking lets the drone use the history of what it has sensed. It treats the light as a field that grows toward the beacon, and moves in the direction the field increases fastest. Following that gradient leads to the peak of the field, which for a light field is the beacon itself. Because the gradient is built from measurements gathered across a region, it can keep guiding the drone when the direct view of the beacon is briefly lost, which the bearing cannot.

The difficulty is measuring the gradient, and Huang et al.\ propose two ways to do it~\cite{huang2026lta}. The first, dither extremum seeking, uses a single photodiode and adds a small oscillation, or dither, to the drone's path. As the drone wiggles, it reads how the light changes and infers the uphill direction. This is the classical extremum-seeking approach to source seeking without knowledge of one's own position~\cite{ariyur2003extremum, zhang2006sourceseeking, cochran2009sourceseeking}. Its drawback is the dither itself. The constant oscillation makes for indirect, inefficient flight, and the probing motion is easily overwhelmed by disturbances. This thesis does not use it.

The second, dither-free gradient ascent, removes the oscillation. It still uses a single photodiode, but instead of probing it estimates the gradient from the drone's recent motion. The drone records where it has been and how strong the light was there, then fits a plane to those recent measurements by least squares, and the slope of that plane is the gradient~\cite{huang2026lta}. This avoids the wasteful dither, but it leans on a single sensor and on dead-reckoned position. It is therefore slow, since the drone must keep moving to gather points, and sensitive to drift. This dither-free estimate is the gradient method this thesis builds on. Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} shows how estimating the gradient from all eight photodiodes and a map of the field, rather than from one sensor and recent motion, removes these weaknesses.

\subsection{Comparing the Algorithms}

Table~\ref{tab:algorithms-comparison} sets the three methods side by side. The bearing is fast and accurate but needs the full photodiode array and keeps no memory. The two gradient methods need only one photodiode, but pay for it, extremum seeking with its inefficient dithering and dither-free ascent with its dependence on motion and dead reckoning. This thesis builds on the bearing and the dither-free gradient, and combines them so that the bearing's immediacy and the gradient's memory cover each other's weaknesses, the design developed in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}.

\section{Simulation Environment}
\label{sec:simulation}

Much of this work is developed in simulation before it is flown. Simulation lets us repeat a scenario exactly as often as we need, compare controllers under identical conditions, and let a controller fail without risking the real drone. It is the natural place to study the problem and design the controller. We use it to show where the existing methods fail, to compare gradient estimators on the same light field, and to develop the fused controller before carrying it onto the real drone.

We use the Webots simulator and the environment that Huang et al.\ built for their own work~\cite{huang2026lta}. It models both the flight of their lighter-than-air blimp and the light its sensors receive. The flight dynamics were validated in that work against published aerodynamic data and real flights, so we take the physical behaviour as faithful rather than re-establishing it here.

The light sensing in simulation is deliberately idealised. Webots does not ray-trace the modulated light, which would be costly to compute and is not what we set out to study, since we want to evaluate the navigation algorithms rather than the optics. Each photodiode is therefore a Webots light sensor that reports the irradiance reaching it directly from the modelled source. The per-sensor light signal $S_n$ of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal} is read straight from this sensor, without the modulation, noise, and frequency analysis the real platform needs. The bearing, the gradient, and the map are all built on $S_n$ in either setting, so the navigation is identical in both, and only the real-world experiments of the later chapters exercise the signal recovery.
