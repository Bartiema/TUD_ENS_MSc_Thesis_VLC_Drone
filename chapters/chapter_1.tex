\chapter{Background \& Related Work}
\label{chp:background}

In this section, we review how drones navigate GPS-denied indoor environments using alternative sensors and identify the remaining challenges of these sensors. We then introduce a drone platform that weighs only \textcolor{red}{X grams}, whose compact form factor and low mass have drawn increasing research attention for operation in cluttered indoor environments. Since this work aims to improve the robustness of indoor autonomous drone navigation, we first review the light-guided navigation algorithms proposed by Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}, which enables drone navigation using single light anchor with minimal modification to existing lighting infrastructure. Building on their work, this thesis proposes an extension that further demonstrates the potential of visible-light sensing for reliable indoor drone navigation.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

Small unmanned aircraft are now widely used for tasks such as structural inspection, environmental monitoring, and warehouse inventory tracking~\cite{floreano2015drones}. Many of these applications take place indoors, where the indoor drone market alone is estimated to grow from USD~254~million in 2024 to USD~614~million by 2032~\cite{intelmarket2026}. However, unlike outdoor drones that can rely on GPS for navigation, indoor drones immediately lose reliable GPS signals due to the roofs and surrounding structures that block the signal, making GPS-based positioning inaccurate~\cite{zafari2019indoor}. Thus, indoor navigation must rely on alternative sensors, which can increase the complexity and payload of the sensors carried by drones or require additional anchor infrastructure to be installed in the environment.

Indoor navigation methods rely on different sensing modalities, and the choice strongly affects the implementation costs. We compare these approaches along two cost dimensions. One cost falls on the drone: the sensor and computation it must carry, both of which are tightly constrained by a micro-drone’s limited payload capacity and onboard processing resources. The other cost falls on the deployment of the system: the infrastructure that must be put in the environment, which determines the complexity when deploying the method in a new environment. Table~\ref{tab:methods-comparison} summarises the main approaches against these two costs.

One family of methods locates the drone by receiving signals from infrastructure installed in the environment beforehand. Two approaches take advantage of electromagnetic signals, i.e., radio signals. Ultra-wideband anchors measure how long a pulse takes to travel between an anchor and the drone, providing positioning information of tens of centimetres accuracy~\cite{queralta2020uwb}. WiFi fingerprinting instead matches the signal strengths the drone measures against a map of measurements surveyed in advance, where its accuracy is reported to be within a metre~\cite{zafari2019indoor}. Comparing two methods, there is a big trade-off to make both neither promising solution. WiFi covers a building using existing hardware, yet its accuracy has limited to the meter level. Whereas ultra-wideband is more accurate but requires dedicated anchors to be reinstalled in a new environment. Another interesting method uses sound: acoustic positioning localises the drone from the travel time of ultrasonic pulses transmitted from fixed beacons~\cite{sun2025acoustic, altena2023acoustic}. All three share one cost in our setting: they require infrastructure to be installed or surveyed before the first flight.

To address these challenges, researchers have explored vision-based approaches that remove the need for external anchors by building its own map as the drone flies. A camera captures a stream of images of the surrounding environment for further process. Visual simultaneous localisation and mapping, or visual SLAM, then tracks features across these images to estimate the drone’s motion and build a map of its surroundings~\cite{murartal2017orbslam2}. This requires no infrastructure in the space. However, the cost shifts to the drone itself: visual SLAM requires a high-resolution camera and powerful on-board computation to process images in real time, which a micro-drone with limited payload and a tight power budget cannot provide.

Each of these approaches is unsuitable for a small drone in one of two ways. Radio and acoustic methods add extra infrastructure to every space, while vision approach adds a camera and computation that the drone cannot carry. Visible-light signals, on the other hand, provide a potential solution to address these challenges. Modern indoor spaces are already well lit by existing lighting infrastructure. Thus, if we can utilize these light sources as transmitters, we can avoid installing additional anchors in the room; an ordinary lamp can already used as a navigation beacon~\cite{pathak2015vlc}. The drone then needs to carry only a lightweight light sensor, whose energy consumption and mass are well within a micro-drone’s budget.

Visible-light positioning is among the most accurate indoor navigation approaches based on light signals, i.e., visible light or infrared light in the frequency range of \textcolor{red}{A to B, with citation}. Through triangulation, such systems can localise a receiver with centimetre-level accuracy by using a photodiode to read signals from several modulated ceiling luminaires~\cite{kuo2014luxapose, zhuang2018vlp}. However, triangulation requires the photodiode to receive signals from at least three light sources at the same time. As a result, the system needs a dense and fixed grid of light sources to achieve the claimed accuracy, which can be costly to provide. The light infrastructure must also satisfy two requirements: \textit{1) multiple light sources must overlap densely so that the receiver is guaranteed to observe at least three different signals, and 2) each light beacon must be installed at a precise location that is measured in advance.} Many spaces cannot arrange lighting so regularly, such as street lamps or aisle lighting bulbs. Thus, although visible-light positioning can provide accurate position information, it depends on dense infrastructure that is often nontrivial to deploy in every target environment.


A single light beacon, on the other hands, provide an alternative that requires far smaller demand on the installment. Yet with only one source a drone can detect in the flight time, the drone cannot provide positioning information through triangulation, due to the hard constrain that triangulation requires at least three light sources. It can, however, recover the direction of the light, which is sufficient for the drone to navigate toward a target light source. \colortex{red}{TODO refine this later. Recovering a direction, rather than a full position, from an array of sensors provides an alternative way to support navigation. Prior work has explored this idea using radio signals, where multiple antennas receive signals from different directions and estimate the transmitter’s bearing angle by comparing signal strength across the antennas~\cite{iqbal2020amplitude}. Single-beacon light navigation applies the same principle, but uses photodiodes and light instead of radio signals and bulky antennas.


As demonstrated by Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}, single-beacon light-guided navigation enables a drone to reach a single target in an unobstructed open space. However, two problems remain before this approach can support fully autonomous drone operation. \textit{First, if an obstacle lies between the drone and the transmitter, it can block the light signal and cause the navigation mission to fail. Second, autonomous drone applications often require the drone to visit multiple targets in a predefined order, whereas the previous approach tracks only one light beacon at a time and therefore cannot directly support more complex missions.} This thesis addresses these limitations through a custom sensing board and a controller that builds and follows a map of the light field, thereby improving the robustness of autonomous drone navigation under single-beacon guidance. We will first review the methods we builds on in our system in Section~\ref{sec:algorithms}.


\section{Drone Platform}
\label{sec:platform}

Through this thesis, we built our platform based on Bitcraze CrazyFlie~2.1, an open-source research quadrotor weighing about 27~grams and small enough to be held in one hand~\cite{crazyflie21}. We chose it because it is openly programmable and provides an interface that is easily to integrate the customized sensors. However, its limited payload capacity and onboard computation constrain contrain both the sensor choices and algorithmic complexity it can support. The CrazyFlie can localise itself accurately with the Lighthouse positioning deck, which tracks infrared signals emitted by base stations placed around the room~\cite{lighthousedeck}. Yet this option, like the radio and dense visible-light methods discussed in Section~\ref{sec:related-work}, depends on infrastructure installation and calibration before the first flight. Since our goal is to enable navigation with minimal changes to the space, so we set such methods aside and instead adopt a simpler setup in this thesis.

Although the Crazyflie offers only a small payload budget, it still leaves several grams for an additional sensor board. In our measurements, the Crazyflie~2.1 can lift roughly 15~grams of payload. Because this thesis uses the Flow Deck~V2 for onboard odometry and ground-truth recording, part of this budget is already occupied, leaving only about 13~grams for the sensing hardware added in this work~\cite{crazyflie21, flowdeckv2}. The added hardware must also operate within the limited current available from the drone’s small lithium battery. Together, these payload and power constraints define the design requirements for the photodiode board presented in Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}.


\section{Visible Light Communication \& Sensing}

Visible light communication modulates a light faster than the eye can perceive~\cite{pathak2015vlc}. A person sees an ordinary, steady light, but a photodiode sampling it quickly enough reads the modulation as a signal. In this work each beacon is a light switched on and off at a fixed frequency, which is enough to distinguish it from steady background light and from beacons at other frequencies.

How strongly a beacon appears at the drone depends on more than distance. Two angles also matter: the angle at which the light leaves the source, and the angle at which it meets the photodiode. Each contributes a cosine factor, and the intensity falls with the square of the distance, a relationship known as the Lambertian model~\cite{komine2004vlc}. Figure~\ref{fig:lambertian} shows the geometry. Writing $\alpha$ for the angle off the source's axis, $\beta$ for the angle of incidence at the sensor, and $d$ for the distance, the received intensity is proportional to $\cos^{m}\!\alpha \, \cos\beta / d^{2}$, where the order $m$ describes how narrowly the source concentrates its light. Intuitively, the light weakens as either the source or the sensor turns away from the line between them, and the order $m$ sets how sharply the source's contribution falls off, higher for a more tightly focused source.

This dependence on angle as well as distance makes a single intensity reading ambiguous. A strong reading can mean a nearby source seen from the side or a distant source seen head-on, and one photodiode cannot tell the two apart, as Figure~\ref{fig:ambiguity} illustrates. This ambiguity is the reason one light cannot fix an absolute position, and the reason positioning systems combine many overlapping sources. We avoid this problem by treating the light as a direction to follow rather than a position to solve for.

\subsection{The Light Signal}
\label{sec:lightsignal}

The drone senses this light with photodiodes. A photodiode produces a small electrical current proportional to the light striking it, and the board converts that current into a value. The navigation reads one value per photodiode, which we call the per-sensor light signal and write $S_n$ for sensor $n$. It measures how strongly that sensor sees the beacon. How $S_n$ is recovered differs between the two settings this thesis uses. On the real drone it is extracted from a noisy voltage, as Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} describes, and in simulation it is read directly from a modelled sensor, as Section~\ref{sec:simulation} describes. For the algorithms that follow, it is enough that each sensor reports one value for how strongly it sees the beacon.

\section{Algorithms from Huang et al.}
\label{sec:algorithms}

This thesis builds on the single-beacon algorithms of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}. They propose three: bearing angle guidance (BAG), dither-free gradient ascent (DGA), and dither extremum seeking (DES). We build on BAG and DGA. DES we describe only to set it aside. Table~\ref{tab:algorithms-comparison} compares all three.

\subsection{Bearing Angle Estimation}
\label{sec:bearing}

Bearing angle guidance recovers the direction of the light from an array of photodiodes~\cite{huang2026lta}. Each photodiode in the array faces a different, known direction, and responds most strongly to light arriving from where it faces. When a beacon lies to one side, the photodiodes facing it report a stronger signal than those facing away. Comparing the photodiodes against one another therefore reveals the direction of the beacon.

The bearing combines these per-sensor readings into a single direction. Each photodiode contributes a vector pointing the way it faces, weighted by how strongly it sees the beacon, and the vectors are summed. Figure~\ref{fig:bearing-vectorsum} shows this for a ring of eight photodiodes. Using the per-sensor light signal $S_n$ and writing $\theta_n$ for the direction sensor $n$ faces, the bearing $\psi_B$ is the angle of the weighted sum,
\begin{equation}
\psi_B = \operatorname{atan2}\!\left( \sum_{n} S_n \sin\theta_n,\; \sum_{n} S_n \cos\theta_n \right).
\label{eq:bearing}
\end{equation}
The sensor facing the beacon contributes the most, so the sum points toward the strongest light. Spacing eight photodiodes evenly around the ring covers every direction in the horizontal plane, so whichever way the beacon lies, at least one sensor faces it. The result is a direction in the plane the photodiodes lie in.

BAG is immediate and accurate. From a single set of readings it points at the beacon, with no need to move or explore, which makes it a strong reactive heading~\cite{huang2026lta}. Its weakness is that it has no memory. Each estimate uses only the latest readings, so the drone steers toward whatever light is strongest at that instant, even when that light is a reflection. The readings alone do not record the space the drone has crossed, so the bearing cannot tell that an obstacle stands between the drone and the beacon. Steering on the bearing alone, the drone heads directly toward the strongest light and cannot plan a path around an obstruction.

\subsection{Gradient-Based Source Seeking}

Where the bearing is reactive and memoryless, gradient-based source seeking lets the drone use the history of what it has sensed. It treats the light as a field that grows toward the beacon, and moves in the direction it estimates the field increases fastest. Following that gradient leads to the peak of the field, which for a light field is the beacon itself. Because it is built from measurements gathered across a region, the gradient still gives a direction when the beacon is briefly out of direct view.

The difficulty is measuring the gradient, and Huang et al.\ propose two ways to do it~\cite{huang2026lta}. The first, dither extremum seeking, uses a single photodiode and adds a small oscillation, or dither, to the drone's path. As the drone oscillates, it measures how the light changes and infers the uphill direction. This is the classical extremum-seeking approach to source seeking without knowledge of the drone's own position~\cite{ariyur2003extremum, zhang2006sourceseeking, cochran2009sourceseeking}. Its drawback is the dither itself. The constant oscillation produces indirect, inefficient flight, and the probing motion is easily overwhelmed by disturbances. This thesis does not use it.

The second, dither-free gradient ascent, removes the oscillation. It still uses a single photodiode, but instead of probing it estimates the gradient from the drone's recent motion. The drone records where it has been and how strong the light was there, then fits a plane to those recent measurements by least squares, and the slope of that plane is the gradient~\cite{huang2026lta}. This avoids the dither, but it depends on a single sensor and on position estimated by dead reckoning, by accumulating the drone's own motion with no external reference, so the estimate drifts over time. It is therefore slow, since the drone must keep moving to gather points, and sensitive to that drift. This dither-free estimate is the gradient method this thesis builds on. Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} shows how estimating the gradient from all eight photodiodes and a map of the field, rather than from one sensor and recent motion, removes some of these weaknesses.

\subsection{Comparing the Algorithms}

Table~\ref{tab:algorithms-comparison} sets the three methods side by side, with the path length and travel time Huang et al.\ measured for each on the same task~\cite{huang2026lta}. The bearing reaches the source most directly, in 7.3~metres and 8.4~seconds, at the cost of the full photodiode array. Both single-photodiode methods are markedly slower. Dither-free ascent takes 8.6~metres and 21.4~seconds, and the dithering extremum seeker is the slowest and least direct at 12.2~metres and 25.6~seconds, its oscillation lengthening the path. The bearing and the dither-free gradient are the two this thesis builds on, and Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} develops how they are combined.

\section{Simulation Environment}
\label{sec:simulation}

The controller is developed in simulation before it is flown on real hardware. Simulation runs a scenario repeatedly under identical conditions, and lets a controller fail without risking the drone. This makes it well suited to designing and comparing the navigation controllers of this thesis.

We use Webots, an open-source robotics simulator~\cite{webots}, with the model that Huang et al.\ built for their work~\cite{huang2026lta}. The model reproduces the flight of their lighter-than-air blimp and the light its sensors receive. Huang et al.\ validated the flight dynamics against published aerodynamic data and real flights, so we take the physical behaviour as given.

The light sensing in the model is deliberately simplified. Webots can render light by ray tracing, but doing so for a fast-modulated source is expensive and unnecessary here, since we study the navigation rather than the optics. Each photodiode is therefore a Webots light sensor that reads the intensity reaching it directly from the modelled source, and the per-sensor light signal $S_n$ is taken straight from this reading.
