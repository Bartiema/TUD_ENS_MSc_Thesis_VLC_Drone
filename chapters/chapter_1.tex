\chapter{Background \& Related Work}
\label{chp:background}

This chapter sets the work in context and gathers the background it rests on. We first review how drones navigate indoors without GPS. The review narrows from radio and vision down to the single-beacon light methods we build on. It then locates the gap this thesis closes. We close with the drone platform and the visible-light theory the later chapters draw on.

\section{Related Work}

Most drones that must navigate where GPS is unavailable fall back on radio or vision. The radio methods locate the drone against fixed anchors placed in the environment. Ultra-wideband beacons time how long a pulse takes to travel between an anchor and the drone, placing it to within tens of centimetres, while WiFi fingerprinting matches the signal strengths the drone hears against a map of measurements surveyed beforehand, usually to within a metre or so~\cite{zafari2019indoor}. The accuracy is good, but it is bought with infrastructure. Every space needs its anchors installed or its fingerprints surveyed before the first flight.

Vision takes a different route, building its own reference as it goes. Visual SLAM tracks features through a camera and at the same time estimates the drone's motion and a map of its surroundings, so it needs no anchors at all~\cite{murartal2017orbslam2}. What it needs instead is a capable camera and enough on-board computation to process a stream of images in real time. For a micro-drone with little payload, little power, and almost no spare computation, that is a heavy demand.

Light offers a cheaper anchor. Visible-light positioning systems reach centimetre-level accuracy indoors by reading the signals of many ceiling-mounted luminaires~\cite{zhuang2018vlp}. Each luminaire is identified by its own modulation, and the system combines them to triangulate a position~\cite{kuo2014luxapose}. The drone then carries only a light sensor, and the lights double as ordinary illumination. The catch is density. These systems assume a whole grid of known, modulated light sources overhead, which is a large installation to require, where our aim is instead to add as little as possible to the spaces the drone already operates in.

A single beacon is enough to navigate from, if the goal is a direction rather than a position. With only a single beacon the drone can no longer triangulate, so it estimates a heading instead. Prior work by Harry Huang does this on a micro-drone. A ring of photodiodes yields a bearing toward the light, and a single photodiode can be made to climb the light gradient by sensing how its signal changes as the drone moves~\cite{huang2026lta}. This needs no infrastructure beyond one beacon, but it is also where the state of the art stops short. The drone can reach a single beacon in open space, yet it keeps no memory of the light field, so it cannot route around an obstacle, and it has no way to tell several beacons apart.

These limitations are the gap this thesis sets out to close, and we build directly on Harry Huang's bearing and gradient methods to do so~\cite{huang2026lta}. Our algorithmic contribution over these methods is threefold. First, we fuse the bearing and the gradient into a single controller. Second, we estimate the gradient from all eight photodiodes rather than one and use those readings to build a spatial map of the light field. Third, we evaluate which gradient estimation method serves this map best. The map gives the drone the memory it needs to route around obstacles, and separating beacons by their modulation frequencies lets it reach several in turn.

\section{Drone Platform}
\label{sec:platform}

The real-world experiments in this thesis run on a Bitcraze CrazyFlie 2.1, a quadrotor small and light enough to sit in the palm of a hand~\cite{crazyflie21}. Its tight weight, size, and power budget are the very constraints that rule out the heavier positioning methods above and push us toward light. The CrazyFlie runs its own low-level flight controller, which keeps it stable and holds it at a set altitude, so our navigation acts in the horizontal plane alone. It issues a target heading and a forward speed as setpoints, and leaves stabilisation and height-keeping to the controller beneath it.

The drone also keeps an onboard estimate of its own position and heading in a fixed world frame, the frame in which we build the map. Like any odometry this estimate drifts slowly over time, a limitation we return to when building the map in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}. Its heading also ties together the two frames the navigation uses. The light a photodiode sees is naturally a direction in the drone's own body frame, relative to where the drone currently points, while the map and the heading we command live in the world frame, and the estimated heading is what converts between the two.

\section{Visible Light Communication \& Sensing}

Visible light communication turns an ordinary light into a signal source by modulating its brightness faster than the eye can follow~\cite{pathak2015vlc}. A receiver watching the light can read this modulation, so the same lamp serves at once as illumination and as a beacon the drone can recognise. In this work each beacon is simply a light switched on and off at a fixed frequency, which is enough to tell it apart from steady background light and from other beacons. Because the light is switched fully on and off, the modulation is a square wave, which carries energy not only at its own frequency but also at its odd harmonics~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}.

How bright a beacon appears at the drone depends on more than distance. The light spreads out as it travels, so the received intensity falls with the square of the distance. It also depends on two angles, the angle at which the light leaves the source and the angle at which it meets the photodiode, each contributing a cosine factor, in what is known as a Lambertian model~\cite{komine2004vlc}. Writing $\alpha$ for the angle off the source's axis, $\beta$ for the angle of incidence at the sensor, and $d$ for the distance, the received intensity is proportional to $\cos^{m}\!\alpha\,\cos\beta / d^{2}$, where the order $m$ is set by how narrowly the source beams its light and equals one for an ideal diffuse emitter. A single intensity reading is therefore ambiguous, because a strong reading could mean a nearby source seen off to the side or a distant source seen head-on. This ambiguity is why one light cannot fix an absolute position, and why positioning systems lean on many overlapping sources. We instead treat the light as a direction and a gradient to follow, rather than a position to solve for.

The drone senses the light with photodiodes, which convert the light falling on them into a small electrical current. A photodiode is directional, responding most strongly to light arriving from the direction it faces and less strongly to light off to the side. One photodiode on its own reports how much light reaches it but not where the light is, while a ring of photodiodes facing different directions can be compared against one another to recover the direction the light comes from, as Section~\ref{sec:bearing} explains.

\section{Light Signals}
\label{sec:lightsignal}

Throughout this thesis the navigation is driven by one quantity per sensor, which we call the light signal and write $m_n$. It measures how strongly photodiode $n$ sees the beacon, and how it is obtained differs between the real platform and the simulation.

On the real drone a single photodiode reports only a raw voltage, in which every beacon and the steady background light are mixed together. To recover how strongly one beacon is seen, we sample each photodiode over a short window and take a frequency analysis of that signal, a fast Fourier transform, which splits it into its separate frequency components~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}. Each beacon then appears as a peak at its own modulation frequency, and the height of that peak is the light signal $m_n$. Because one transform holds every beacon's peak at once, the sensing is parallel across beacons, and the ratio of a peak to the surrounding noise tells us how far a reading can be trusted.

The simulation we use produces this signal in a different way, as described in Section~\ref{sec:simulation}. There each photodiode is a Webots light sensor that reports the irradiance reaching it directly from the modelled light source, so $m_n$ is read straight from the sensor rather than recovered by a frequency analysis. The modulation, the switching on and off, and the harmonics it brings exist only on the real platform; in simulation a beacon is simply a light source whose strength is sampled without that pipeline. The bearing, map, and gradient that follow are built on $m_n$ either way, so the navigation is the same in both settings, and only the real-world experiments exercise the frequency analysis and the beacon separation it provides.

\section{Bearing Angle Estimation}
\label{sec:bearing}

A ring of photodiodes turns those per-sensor readings into a bearing toward the light. Each photodiode faces a known direction, so the bearing algorithm of prior work by Harry Huang weights each sensor's direction by how much light it sees and sums them into a single vector that points toward the beacon~\cite{huang2026lta}. Using the per-sensor light signal $m_n$ of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal} and writing $\theta_n$ for the direction sensor $n$ faces, the bearing $\psi_B$ is the angle of this magnitude-weighted sum,
\begin{equation}
\psi_B = \operatorname{atan2}\!\left( \sum_{n} m_n \sin\theta_n,\; \sum_{n} m_n \cos\theta_n \right).
\label{eq:bearing}
\end{equation}
The sensor facing the light contributes most and the sensors facing away contribute little, so the weighted sum settles on the direction of the strongest light. Spacing eight photodiodes evenly around the ring covers every direction in the horizontal plane, so wherever the light comes from at least one sensor faces toward it and feeds the sum. The bearing it produces is therefore a direction in that plane, which suits a drone that flies at a roughly fixed height.

Recovering a direction from the relative strengths across an array of sensors is well established in radio. Amplitude-comparison direction finders estimate a transmitter's bearing from how strongly it is received across several differently-pointed antennas, taking the arctangent of the combined responses in much the way we do here~\cite{iqbal2020amplitude}, with photodiodes and light in place of antennas and radio. Applying it to visible light on a micro-drone, by contrast, is far less common.

A bearing is a single direction, and that is both its strength and its limit. It tells the drone at once which way the light lies, without any exploration, which makes it an excellent reactive heading. It does, though, rely on a clear line of sight to the beacon. The photodiodes need the direct light to form a clean reading, and although a strong enough sensor could in principle work from reflections, in practice a direct path is required. Beyond that, a bearing carries no information about the space around the drone. It cannot reveal that an obstacle sits between the drone and the beacon, and it cannot remember where the light was once the drone can no longer see it.

\section{Gradient-Based Source Seeking}

Gradient-based source seeking gives the drone a way to climb toward the beacon, where the bearing only gives a direction. The idea is to treat the light as a scalar field that grows toward the beacon and, at each moment, to move in the direction in which that field increases fastest. Following the gradient leads to the peak of the field, which for a light field is the beacon itself, and unlike the bearing it needs no clear line of sight, only local measurements of how the field changes from one place to the next. An established way to find this direction without knowing one's own position is extremum-seeking control, which dithers the path with a small perturbation and reads the gradient off how the measured signal responds~\cite{ariyur2003extremum, zhang2006sourceseeking, cochran2009sourceseeking}.

The hard part is measuring the gradient, and on a small platform it is usually estimated more directly than by dithering. Prior work by Harry Huang reads it from a single photodiode without any perturbation, watching how that one sensor's signal changes as the drone moves and comparing it before and after a small motion to tell whether the drone is heading up the gradient or down it~\cite{huang2026lta}. This is simpler, but it is still slow and fragile, as we show in the simulations of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}. The drone has to keep moving to sense anything at all, and because a single sensor is fixed to the drone's body and sees the beacon only when the drone is yawed to face it, the estimate is easily upset by noise and by the drone's own rotation.

\section{Simulation Environment}
\label{sec:simulation}

Much of this work is developed and tested in simulation before it ever flies. We use the Webots simulator that Harry Huang built for his own work~\cite{huang2026lta}, which models both the flight behaviour of his lighter-than-air blimp and the light its sensors receive. Harry validated its flight dynamics in that work, both against published aerodynamic data and against real flight tests, so we take its physical behaviour as faithful rather than re-establishing it here. Its light sensing, by contrast, is the idealised model of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal}, where each photodiode reads the modelled irradiance directly, without the modulation and noise of the real platform. The simulator also gives us a setting that is reproducible and free of hardware dependencies, in which the same scenario can be run again and again and a controller can fail without risking the real drone.

This makes simulation the natural place to study the problem and design the controller. We use it to show where the existing methods fall down, to compare candidate gradient estimators on the same simulated light field, and to develop the fused controller, before carrying the result onto the real drone. That real drone is a CrazyFlie 2.1 rather than the simulator's blimp, but the airframe does not reach the navigation, which only ever reads the per-sensor light signal and returns a heading and a speed, leaving the rest to each platform's own flight controller. The real-world experiments in the later chapters then test whether what worked in simulation survives the noise, drift, and timing of an actual flight.
