\section{Visible Light Communication \& Sensing}

Visible light communication turns an ordinary light into a signal source by modulating 
its brightness faster than the eye can follow~\cite{pathak2015vlc}. \hlnotein{A receiver watching the 
light can read this modulation}{again try to split it. and watching is weird word here}, so the same lamp 
serves at once as illumination and as a beacon the drone can recognise. In this work each beacon is simply
a light switched on and off at a fixed frequency, which is enough to tell it apart from steady 
background light and from other beacons. Because the light is switched fully on and off, the modulation 
is a square wave, which carries energy not only at its own frequency but also at its 
\hlnote{odd harmonics}{its a unnecessary info here.}~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}.

How bright a beacon appears at the drone depends on more than distance. 
The light spreads out as it travels, so the received intensity falls with the square of the distance.
It also depends on two angles, the angle at which the light leaves the source and the angle at which it
meets the photodiode, each contributing a cosine factor, in what is known as a \hlnotein{Lambertian}{Again,
I think i mentioned this last time. here we are just throwing the lambertian model. why are we talking about this? 
One reason I can think is that we need to demontrate why single anchor fail with the other approach} model~\cite{komine2004vlc}. Writing $\alpha$ for the angle off the source's axis, $\beta$ for the angle of incidence at the sensor, and $d$ for the distance, the received intensity is proportional to $\cos^{m}\!\alpha\,\cos\beta / d^{2}$, where the order $m$ is set by how narrowly the source beams its light and equals one for an ideal diffuse emitter. A single intensity reading is therefore ambiguous, because a strong reading could mean a nearby source seen off to the side or a distant source seen head-on. This ambiguity is why one light cannot fix an absolute position, and why positioning systems lean on many overlapping sources. We instead treat the light as a direction and a gradient to follow, rather than a position to solve for.

The drone senses the light with photodiodes, which convert the light falling on them into 
a small electrical current. A photodiode is directional, responding most strongly to light
arriving from the direction it faces and less strongly to light off to the side. One photodiode 
on its own reports how much light reaches it but not where the light is,
while a ring of photodiodes facing different directions can be compared against one another 
to recover the direction the light comes from, as Section~\ref{sec:bearing} explains.

\section{Bearing Angle Estimation}
\label{sec:bearing}

A ring of photodiodes turns those per-sensor readings into a bearing toward the light. Each photodiode faces a 
known direction, so the bearing algorithm of prior work by Harry Huang weights each sensor's direction by how much
light it sees and sums them into a single vector that points toward the beacon~\cite{huang2026lta}. Using the per-sensor 
light signal $m_n$ of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal} and writing $\theta_n$ for the direction sensor $n$ faces, the 
bearing $\psi_B$ is the angle of this magnitude-weighted sum,
\begin{equation}
\psi_B = \operatorname{atan2}\!\left( \sum_{n} m_n \sin\theta_n,\; \sum_{n} m_n \cos\theta_n \right).
\label{eq:bearing}
\end{equation}

please explain more}, so the weighted
sum \hlnotein{settles}{try to find some words more formal than settle. for example indicate the direction} on the direction of the 
strongest light. Spacing eight photodiodes evenly around the ring covers every direction in the horizontal plane, 
so wherever the light comes from at least one sensor faces toward it and 
feeds the sum. The bearing it produces is therefore a direction in that plane, which suits a drone that flies at
a roughly fixed height.

\section{Simulation Environment}
\label{sec:simulation}

Much of this work is developed and tested in simulation before it ever flies. We use the Webots simulator that 
Harry Huang built for his own work~\cite{huang2026lta}, which models both the flight behaviour of his lighter-than-air blimp and
 the light its sensors receive. Harry validated its flight dynamics in that work, both against published aerodynamic data and
  against real flight tests, so we take its physical behaviour as faithful rather than re-establishing it here. Its light sensing,
   by contrast, is the idealised model of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal}, where each photodiode reads the modelled irradiance directly,
    without the modulation and noise of the real platform. The simulator also gives us a setting that is reproducible and free of
     hardware dependencies, in which the same scenario can be run again and again and a controller can fail without risking the real
      \hlnotein{drone}{First talk about why we want to use it. because it can run multiple time (dont use again and again.) and then briefly
      speak the detail of the simulation. just a bit.}.

This makes simulation the natural place to study the problem and design the controller. We use it to show where the 
existing methods fall down, to compare candidate gradient estimators on the same simulated light field, and to 
develop the fused controller, before carrying the result onto the real drone. That real drone is a CrazyFlie 2.1
 rather than the simulator's blimp, but the airframe does not reach the navigation, which only ever reads the per-sensor
  light signal and returns a heading and a speed, leaving the rest to each platform's own flight controller. The real-world 
  experiments in the later chapters then test whether what worked in simulation survives the noise, drift, and timing of an
