\section{Why Existing Methods Fall Short}

The bearing and single-photodiode gradient methods this work builds on each steer toward a single light, but neither can route around an obstacle that blocks the line of sight, and neither distinguishes more than one beacon.

In simulation, a bearing-only controller drives straight at the light and stalls against an obstacle, never recovering once the direct signal is lost (Figure~\ref{fig:bearing-fail}).

The remainder of this chapter develops a fused, map-based controller that overcomes these limits, with its positive results presented in Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle}.

\section{System Overview}

The navigation controller fuses a bearing angle signal and a gradient ascent signal through a state machine that selects the appropriate navigation mode based on signal strength and map coverage (Figure~\ref{fig:controller-overview}).

\section{Bearing Angle}

The controller's first input is a bearing angle, computed from the differential magnitudes across the photodiode ring by the prior bearing algorithm of Harry Huang~\cite{}.

The bearing is only trusted when its signal strength exceeds a minimum threshold, so that a weak or absent signal does not produce a spurious heading.

\section{Multi-Beacon Traversal}

Each beacon is modulated at a distinct frequency, and the controller traverses a sequence of beacons by retuning the FFT pipeline to the next beacon's frequency once the current one is reached.

Because the pipeline can evaluate several frequencies from the same samples, the beacons can also be monitored in parallel, as demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:freq-separation}.

These frequencies must avoid harmonic relationships, since the FFT cannot distinguish a frequency from its integer multiples; the switch between frequencies was developed with simple static Teensy experiments.

\section{Light Intensity Map}

As the drone flies, it accumulates a spatial map by storing the strongest measured light signal at each visited location, which increases as the drone approaches the light source.

\section{Gradient Estimation}

The controller estimates the light gradient from all eight photodiodes rather than the single photodiode used in prior work, giving a faster and more stable direction of steepest increase.

Five candidate methods for estimating the gradient from the map are considered, from plane fits to a curved surface fit, and their accuracy is compared in this chapter to select the one the controller uses (Figure~\ref{fig:gradient-comparison}).

\section{Controller State Machine}

The state machine sequences the drone through searching, aligning, approaching, dwelling, and recovering phases for each beacon in a multi-target mission.

The recovering phase lets the drone keep moving on the gradient alone when the bearing is lost but the map is still reliable, allowing it to continue past an obstacle that blocks the direct signal.

\section{Control Output}

The controller merges the bearing and gradient directions into a single target heading and sets a forward speed, which are passed to the drone's flight controller.
