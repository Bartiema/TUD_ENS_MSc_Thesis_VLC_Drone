\chapter{Algorithm Design}
\label{chp:algorithm}

\section{System Overview}

The navigation controller fuses a bearing angle signal and a gradient ascent signal through a state machine that selects the appropriate navigation mode based on signal strength and map coverage.

\section{Bearing Angle}

The controller's first input is a bearing angle signal, computed from the differential readings across the photodiode ring introduced in Chapter~\ref{chp:background}.

The strength of this signal---the magnitude of the measured signal at each sensor---indicates how reliably the light source is detected, with a stronger signal yielding a more trustworthy bearing estimate.

When multiple beacons are deployed, each is modulated at a distinct frequency, and these frequencies must avoid harmonic relationships because the FFT used to separate them cannot distinguish a frequency from its multiples.

\section{Light Intensity Map}

As the drone flies, it accumulates a spatial map by storing the maximum measured light signal at each visited location, which increases monotonically as the drone approaches the light source.

\section{Controller State Machine}

When both map coverage and bearing signal strength fall below their respective thresholds, the drone executes a predefined search pattern to gather initial measurements and populate the map.

Once the bearing signal exceeds its strength threshold but the map remains insufficiently populated, the controller drives the drone using the bearing angle alone.

When both the bearing signal and map coverage are sufficient, the controller outputs a heading angle computed as a weighted sum of the bearing direction and the gradient direction from the map.

If the bearing signal is subsequently lost after sufficient map coverage has been accumulated, the controller falls back to pure gradient ascent on the stored map to continue navigating toward the light.

\section{Gradient Estimation}

The local gradient of the light intensity map is estimated from the measurements in the neighbourhood of the drone's current position, and five candidate estimation methods are considered for this purpose.

Ordinary least-squares plane fitting estimates the gradient from the two slope coefficients of a plane fitted to the neighbourhood measurements~\cite{}.

Weighted least squares extends this fit by weighting each sample by the inverse square of its distance to the drone, so that nearer measurements contribute more strongly to the estimate~\cite{}.

Robust RANSAC repeatedly fits a plane to random three-point subsets and retains the fit with the most inliers, reducing the influence of outlier measurements~\cite{}.

A local polynomial fit fits a second-order surface to the neighbourhood and evaluates the gradient from its first-order terms at the drone's position, capturing the local curvature of the field~\cite{}.

Finite differences approximate the gradient from the difference in mean intensity between neighbouring samples in opposing directions along each axis~\cite{}.

The relative accuracy of these methods is evaluated experimentally in Chapter~\ref{chp:simulation}.

\section{Control Output}

The controller outputs a target heading angle; forward motion is either set to a fixed speed or suppressed entirely depending on the angular error between the desired and current heading.
