\chapter{Problem \& Algorithm Design}
\label{chp:algorithm}

This chapter designs the navigation controller at the core of this thesis. Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} showed that a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone can reach a single light source in open space, using either a bearing read from a photodiode array or a gradient climbed with a single photodiode. This thesis targets a harder problem: reaching several modulated beacons in succession while passing obstacles between them, using on-board light sensing alone. The existing methods do not solve this problem, and solving it with extra sensors or an explicit map-building and path-planning pipeline would exceed the platform's weight and power budget. The design question is therefore how to obtain obstacle-aware, multi-beacon navigation from light alone, within that budget.

The controller combines the two existing methods and adds a short spatial memory. As the drone flies, it records the light strength seen by the eight-photodiode array into a map of the area. It estimates the light gradient from this map, which gives a direction toward the source even when the beacon is not directly visible. Building the map from the full array, rather than from a single photodiode, lets the drone record a usable value at each location it visits regardless of its heading; a single fixed photodiode captures the source only while it faces it. The remaining sections develop the controller part by part: the bearing estimate, the separation of simultaneous beacons by modulation frequency, the light map, the gradient estimator and the choice between candidate estimators, the state machine that sequences a mission, and the heading and speed sent to the flight controller.

\section{Why Existing Methods Fall Short}

Both methods were designed and evaluated for a single light source in open space, where each performs well~\cite{huang2026lta}. The task of this thesis adds two elements they were not built for: obstacles between the drone and the beacon, and more than one beacon. The following paragraphs explain why each method falls short under these conditions, and the simulations that follow demonstrate it.

The bearing is an instantaneous estimate, formed from the relative magnitudes of the eight photodiode signals at a single instant. It needs no integration over the drone's motion, so it yields a heading immediately whenever the array receives the beacon's light, whether directly or through a strong reflection. This immediacy is also its limitation. The bearing describes only the direction of the light arriving now and retains nothing about the surrounding space. When an obstacle blocks the beacon, the photodiode magnitudes drop toward the ambient level and the magnitude-weighted direction no longer points at the source. The bearing then yields no usable heading, and nothing in it reveals that an obstacle is the cause or where the beacon now lies.

The single-photodiode gradient, the second method of Huang et al., recovers a direction with one sensor instead of an array, but it is slow and fragile. A single sensor measures one light value at a time, so it cannot sense a spatial gradient directly. It infers the gradient from how that value changes between successive positions, which forces the drone to keep moving to obtain any estimate, as Figure~\ref{fig:single-pd-gradient} illustrates. The estimate also depends on the drone's orientation, because one fixed sensor receives the direct beam only while it faces the source.

We examine these failures in simulation before testing on hardware, for two reasons. A simulated light field carries none of the ambient light or electrical noise of a real flight, so an observed failure can be attributed to the navigation method rather than to the sensing. The simulator also runs the same scenario repeatedly, at low cost and with no risk to the aircraft, which is what a controlled comparison of methods requires.

The comparison uses the Webots environment of Section~\ref{sec:simulation}, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sim-setup}. A single beacon sits above the floor, a pillar stands between the drone and the beacon, and the drone starts to one side, so the pillar obstructs the direct path to the beacon. Each controller flies this same scene, from the same start position to the same beacon, with identical sensing and flight settings, so the navigation method is the only variable between runs. The same environment is used for the experiments of Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle}.

The bearing-only controller steers directly along the measured light direction. The pillar lies slightly off the direct line to the beacon, so the bearing points just past its edge, and the controller follows that direction with no indication that it leads into the obstacle. The drone therefore drives into the pillar, as Figure~\ref{fig:bearing-fail} shows.

The single-photodiode gradient controller does not reach the source either. It follows a slow, irregular path and, on reaching the pillar, stalls against it, as Figure~\ref{fig:gradient-fail} shows. The stall follows from how the gradient is estimated. The method reads the drone's position from the simulator's odometry, and while the drone is pressed against the pillar the odometry still reports forward progress. The estimated gradient therefore keeps pointing in the same direction, and the controller keeps driving the drone into the obstacle rather than turning away.

Neither baseline supports more than one beacon, so neither can perform the multi-beacon traversal this thesis targets. We do not demonstrate the multi-beacon case in simulation. As Section~\ref{sec:simulation} explains, the simulator does not ray-trace the light but reads each sensor's intensity directly, and it does not reproduce the on-off modulation. Without that modulation the beacons cannot be told apart, so beacon separation can only be shown on the real platform.

The two failures are complementary. The bearing is fast and accurate but has no memory beyond the current line of sight. The gradient tolerates a blocked line of sight but is slow and must read from accumulated measurements. A controller that maintains a short spatial memory can therefore follow the bearing while the beacon is visible and the remembered gradient when it is not. The remainder of this chapter develops this controller; its results are reported in Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle}.

\section{System Overview}
\label{sec:overview}

The failures of the previous section define two requirements for the controller. First, the drone must move efficiently toward a visible beacon. Second, it must continue toward the beacon when the direct path is blocked. The controller meets both requirements with two direction estimates derived from the same photodiode array. The first is the bearing, which gives an immediate heading whenever the array receives the beacon's light. The second is a gradient, estimated from a light map that the drone builds as it flies; because the map records where the light was stronger, the gradient still gives a direction when the beacon is not directly visible.

Neither estimate is always trustworthy, so the controller must decide which to use at each moment. The bearing is meaningless when its magnitude is low, which happens when the beacon is weak or blocked. The gradient is meaningless until the map holds enough cells and they fit a consistent slope, measured by the coefficient of determination of Section~\ref{sec:gradient-estimation}. A state machine reads these two validity measures and selects the drone's behaviour: it follows the bearing while the beacon is visible, adds the gradient once the map is dense enough, and follows the gradient alone when the bearing is invalid.

Each control update follows the same sequence, summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:controller-overview}. The per-sensor light signals $S_n$ of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal} are converted into a bearing estimate and written into the light map. The gradient is then estimated from the map, and the bearing and gradient are merged into a single target heading weighted by their validity. The state machine sets the drone's phase and forward speed for the current beacon, and on arrival advances to the next.

\section{Bearing Angle}
\label{sec:bearing-angle}

The bearing itself is the estimator of Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}, derived in Section~\ref{sec:bearing}: each photodiode's facing direction is weighted by its light signal $S_n$, and the bearing is the angle of the resulting vector sum. This section does not repeat that derivation. It describes the two steps the controller adds to turn the raw estimate into a usable heading: a validity gate and temporal smoothing.

The bearing is only meaningful when the array actually receives the beacon. With no beacon in view, every photodiode sees only ambient light, the vector sum has near-zero magnitude, and its angle is set by noise. The controller therefore uses the vector-sum magnitude as the validity measure introduced in Section~\ref{sec:overview}, and acts on the bearing only when this magnitude exceeds a minimum threshold. This rejects headings that are really noise from a weak, distant, or absent beacon.

A bearing that passes this test is still noisy between samples. Reflections, sensor noise, and small changes in attitude make the raw bearing jitter from one frame to the next, and acting on each raw value would make the heading jerk. The controller therefore smooths the bearing with an exponential moving average before passing it to the fusion stage and the state machine. The update is
\begin{equation}
\hat{\psi}_B \leftarrow \hat{\psi}_B + (1-\beta)\,\Delta,
\label{eq:bearing-filter}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta$ is the wrapped angular difference between the new bearing and the current estimate $\hat{\psi}_B$, and $\beta$ sets how much of the past estimate is retained. A larger $\beta$ rejects more noise but responds more slowly to a genuine change in direction.

\section{Multi-Beacon Traversal}

A deployment may place several beacons in the space, and a mission requires the drone to reach each of them in turn. To make the beacons distinguishable, we modulate each one at a distinct frequency. A frequency analysis of a single photodiode signal then yields a separate peak per beacon, so the drone senses all of them at once and tells them apart. The traversal is sequential: the controller selects one beacon as the current target, flies to it, and on arrival selects the next.

Beacon separation relies on a frequency analysis of each photodiode signal. A photodiode reports one value over time, in which every beacon and the steady background light are mixed together. A Fourier transform decomposes that signal into the strength it carries at each frequency. Because each beacon switches on and off at its own rate, its energy concentrates at its own frequency and shows up as a peak there, with a height that reflects how strongly that beacon is seen. Figure~\ref{fig:fft-mock} illustrates this for two beacons modulated at 150 and 200\,Hz: their signals overlap in time but separate into distinct peaks in frequency. This chapter uses only this much of the analysis; the full pipeline that recovers each peak on the noisy real platform is described in Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}.

This is generated data, shown only to illustrate the principle; the real, measured separation is presented in Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon}. Two lights are switched on and off as square waves at 150 and 200\,Hz, and one photodiode sees their sum as an ADC reading between 0 and 4095. Left: a short slice of that reading in time, where the two beacons are not separable. Right: the magnitude of its Fourier transform, where each beacon appears as a peak at its own frequency. The dotted lines mark the odd harmonics that a square wave also produces.}
    \label{fig:fft-mock}

Every photodiode produces this same set of peaks, and the peak heights differ across the array according to the direction each beacon comes from. Feeding the per-beacon, per-photodiode peak heights into the bearing estimator of Section~\ref{sec:bearing} therefore yields a separate bearing for each beacon from a single recording.

The modulation frequencies must be chosen with care. Switching a light fully on and off produces a square wave, which carries energy not only at its fundamental frequency but also at its odd harmonics~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}. The analysis separates a fundamental from its harmonics, because they fall in different frequency bins. A problem arises only when one beacon's fundamental coincides with a harmonic of another, since that harmonic then adds energy to the wrong beacon's bin and the two become difficult to distinguish. We took this into account when choosing the modulation frequencies for our experiments.

\section{Light Intensity Map}

Gradient-based navigation needs a record of how the light varies across space, which the bearing alone does not provide. The controller builds this record as a light map. It divides the horizontal plane into a regular grid and stores one light value per cell, indexed by the drone's estimated position in a fixed world frame. Figure~\ref{fig:light-map} shows such a map after a survey of the area.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/gradient_analysis/light_map.png}
    \caption{A light-intensity map of the simulation, viewed from above. Each grid cell holds the strongest light signal recorded while the drone occupied it, drawn here as a smooth field. The strength rises toward the beacon, marked by the star, and this is the scalar field whose gradient the controller follows. The grey disc is the pillar, and the dashed circle the clearance the drone has to keep around it.}
    \label{fig:light-map}
\end{figure}

Indexing by an estimated position introduces a source of error that the map must tolerate. The drone's on-board position estimate drifts slowly over time, as any odometry does. A drifting estimate files a measurement under the wrong cell, which smears the recorded field across neighbouring cells and blurs the gradient estimated from it.

Two design choices limit this error. First, the map is reset whenever the drone switches to the next beacon. The error accumulated while approaching one beacon is then discarded with its map and does not carry into the next, so drift cannot build up across a whole mission. Second, the grid quantises position: all readings that fall in one cell are combined into a single value. The map therefore grows with the area covered rather than with flight time, and any drift smaller than one cell still leaves a reading in its correct cell.

Each cell stores the strongest light signal $S_n$ measured across the photodiode array at that location, using the per-sensor signal of Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal}. When the drone revisits a cell, the new measurement does not overwrite the stored value but updates it through an exponential moving average,
\begin{equation}
I_{\text{cell}} \leftarrow \alpha\,I_{\text{cell}} + (1-\alpha)\,I_{\text{new}},
\label{eq:map-blend}
\end{equation}
which averages out the sample-to-sample fluctuation seen when the same location is measured repeatedly. The factor $\alpha$ weights the stored value against the new one and is set by default to weight them equally. A smaller $\alpha$ tracks new measurements more closely at the cost of more noise, while a larger $\alpha$ gives a smoother but slower-adapting map.

Across the visited area the map approximates a scalar field that increases toward the beacon, because the measured signal grows as the drone nears the source. This is the spatial memory the bearing lacks. The next section estimates a direction from this field.

\section{Gradient Estimation}
\label{sec:gradient-estimation}

The gradient gives the drone a direction to follow when the bearing is unavailable. It differs from the bearing in what it describes: the bearing points along the light arriving now, whereas the gradient points in the direction in which the stored light field increases fastest, which remains defined even when the beacon is hidden. Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} estimated this direction from a single photodiode, by reading how that sensor's signal changed as the drone moved. That approach is slow, because the drone must keep moving to gather readings, and fragile, because a single fixed sensor measures the source only while it faces it. Our controller instead estimates the gradient from the light map. Because the map is built from the full photodiode array, it records a usable value at each visited location regardless of the drone's heading, which removes the dependence on orientation and motion that limits the single-photodiode method.

The controller estimates the gradient from the map by fitting a plane to it. Around the drone's current position it takes the cells $\{(x_i, y_i, I_i)\}$ and fits $I(x,y) = a x + b y + c$ by weighted least squares, weighting each cell by the inverse square of its distance to the drone so that nearer cells count for more. The fitted coefficients give the gradient $\mathbf{g} = (a, b)$, and the controller steers along $\mathbf{g}$ whenever it navigates by the map.

A gradient estimate, like a bearing, can be unreliable, so the controller needs a measure of its validity. The plane fit provides one directly. It reports how closely the cells follow the fitted plane, through a weighted coefficient of determination
\begin{equation}
R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_i w_i\,(I_i - \hat{I}_i)^2}{\sum_i w_i\,(I_i - \bar{I}_w)^2},
\label{eq:gradient-r2}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{I}_i$ is the plane's value at cell $i$, $\bar{I}_w$ is the weighted mean of the cell values, and $w_i$ are the inverse-distance weights. An $R^2$ near one means the cells lie close to a single plane, so the fitted direction is trustworthy. A low $R^2$ means they do not, so the gradient should not be followed.

Three conditions must hold for the controller to act on a gradient. The fit must use at least a minimum number of cells, because a plane through two or three points is set by noise as much as by the field. Its $R^2$ must exceed a threshold, which rejects fits where the cells do not lie on a consistent slope, as happens where the field is flat or where the map is still patchy. Its magnitude must be large enough to be meaningful, so the drone does not chase a near-flat field. The thresholds, with the controller's other parameters, are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:parameters}. These three conditions define the gradient reliability used by the state machine. While they fail, the drone navigates on the bearing alone. Once they hold, the controller blends the gradient into the heading, or follows it alone when the bearing is lost.

Fitting a plane is well understood, but it is not obvious which estimator performs best on the sparse, unevenly spaced cells of a real map. We therefore implement and compare five candidate methods: ordinary and weighted least-squares plane fits~\cite{bjorck1996lsq, strutz2016wls}, a robust RANSAC fit~\cite{fischler1981ransac}, a local polynomial surface fit~\cite{bjorck1996lsq}, and finite differences~\cite{leveque2007fdm}. We judge them on three criteria, in order of importance: how often each returns a usable estimate, how accurately that estimate points, and how much it costs to compute, since the estimator runs on the drone's microcontroller. Figure~\ref{fig:gradient-comparison} reports the first two on a densely sampled reference map of the simulated light field.

We define these measures over a set of $N$ cells sampled across the reference map. At each cell a method either returns a gradient estimate that passes the reliability gates of the previous section or returns nothing; with $S_m$ the cells where method $m$ succeeds, its success rate is $|S_m| / N$. For each returned estimate $\hat{\mathbf{g}}_i = (\hat{a}_i, \hat{b}_i)$ we take the angular error against the direction straight to the source,
\begin{equation}
e_i = \left| \left( \hat{\theta}_i - \theta_i^\star + 180^\circ \right) \bmod 360^\circ - 180^\circ \right|,
\label{eq:angle-error}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\theta}_i = \operatorname{atan2}(\hat{b}_i, \hat{a}_i)$ is the estimate's heading and $\theta_i^\star = \operatorname{atan2}(y_L - y_i,\, x_L - x_i)$ points from cell $i$ to the source at $(x_L, y_L)$, and the wrap folds the unsigned difference into $[0^\circ, 180^\circ]$. We discard cells within ten centimetres of the source, where the field is too steep to give a meaningful reference, and note that this straight-to-source reference is itself only approximate behind the pillar, where the helpful direction leads around the obstacle rather than at it. We then average $e_i$ two ways: the own-coverage error over a method's own cells $S_m$, and the common-coverage error over the cells $C = \bigcap_m S_m$ that every method estimates.

Coverage is the criterion that matters most. The bearing already gives the controller an accurate heading in open space, so the gradient does not have to be precise there; its job is to be available wherever the drone is, and above all when the bearing is lost behind an obstacle or between beacons. A method that often returns nothing leaves the controller flying blind at exactly those moments. Finite differences return an estimate almost always, at 99.7\%, but from so few cells that the direction is noisy; RANSAC succeeds least often, at 71\%, discarding too many cells as outliers; and the plane and polynomial fits sit between. Weighted least squares returns a usable estimate 96\% of the time, second only to the noisy finite differences.

The raw angular error, by contrast, is a misleading way to rank the methods, because it is entangled with coverage. A method that quietly fails on the hardest cells is never charged for them, so its average error flatters it, while a high-coverage method like weighted least squares is scored everywhere, including the cells right under the elevated source where the measured field bends sharply and behind the pillar where it leads around the obstacle rather than straight at it. This shows directly in the figures: on its own coverage weighted least squares records a mean error of 29 degrees, against 5.6 degrees for the low-coverage RANSAC fit. When we instead score every method on the same common set of cells, the cells that all of them estimate, the gap nearly closes. The plane, polynomial, RANSAC and weighted least-squares fits then agree to within about three degrees, from 5.6 to 8.5 degrees of mean error, with only finite differences staying noticeably noisier at 14.6 degrees; weighted least squares itself falls from 29 to 8.5 degrees, which shows that most of its apparent inaccuracy was simply the price of estimating the difficult cells the other methods decline. The remaining differences of a few degrees are not decisive: the bearing compensates them in open space, and the steep field under the source would not even appear in flight, where the drone follows a partial map and never measures that drop.

Computational cost and robustness settle the choice. Weighted least squares solves one small fixed-size linear system whose runtime is the same on every call, which sits comfortably within the budget of the drone's microcontroller, and its inverse-distance weighting makes it robust to the changes in overall light level that occur as the drone moves nearer to or further from the source. RANSAC, the most accurate method on its own coverage, is the most expensive and the least predictable, since it repeatedly fits and scores random subsets and its running time varies from call to call, which is awkward for a real-time control loop on a small processor. Finite differences are the cheapest but the noisiest. Weighted least squares therefore offers the best combination for this platform: the highest usable coverage, a low and constant computational cost, and robustness to light-level changes, which is why the controller estimates its gradient this way.

\section{Controller State Machine}

\label{sec:state-machine}
The bearing and gradient indicate which way to go, but not how the drone should behave at a given moment. Two situations require an explicit decision. The drone may have no usable estimate. This occurs before it has found a beacon. It also occurs when the light is blocked and the map is still empty. The drone may instead have both estimates and must decide how much to rely on each as it moves. We handle both with a navigation state machine, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:state-machine}, with five states: \emph{searching}, \emph{aligning}, \emph{approaching}, \emph{dwelling}, and \emph{recovering}. The following paragraphs describe each state and its purpose.

In the \emph{searching} state the drone has no trustworthy bearing or gradient estimate, and its purpose is to acquire a beacon. Because the photodiode array already covers every direction, scanning by rotation would reveal nothing new. The drone instead flies a bounded figure-of-eight path, moving out to a fixed search radius and reversing at its edge. This carries it across the surrounding area, so it passes through positions where the beacon's signal is strong and lays down the first cells of the light map at the same time. The drone leaves \emph{searching} as soon as the signal produces a bearing whose magnitude exceeds the trust threshold. It does not act on the gradient while searching; the map built here is used only from \emph{approaching} onward.

In the \emph{aligning} state the drone turns toward the target while holding position, so it points at the beacon before committing to forward motion. It transitions to \emph{approaching} once its heading is within the alignment tolerance of the target direction, $15^\circ$ in this work. Appendix~\ref{app:parameters} lists this and the other controller parameters.

In the \emph{approaching} state the drone flies forward while continuously correcting its heading toward the target. If its heading later drifts beyond a wider re-alignment threshold of $30^\circ$, it returns to \emph{aligning}, so the drone never flies forward while badly misaligned.

The heading followed during \emph{aligning} and \emph{approaching} is not always the bearing alone. While the map is still sparse, too few cells have been recorded for a reliable gradient, so the drone steers by the bearing. Once the gradient passes the reliability conditions of Section~\ref{sec:gradient-estimation}, the controller blends it into the heading. The drone therefore already combines both inputs during a normal approach, well before the bearing is ever lost, which makes the later hand-off to the gradient seamless.

The drone must also detect arrival at a beacon. The measured light strength rises as the drone nears the source, so the drone is treated as arrived once this strength exceeds an arrival threshold, reached only close to the beacon. This threshold is tuned per flight, with its value listed in Appendix~\ref{app:parameters}. On arrival the drone enters the \emph{dwelling} state, holds position for a configurable time, and then moves to the next beacon. The mission ends after the last beacon.

The \emph{recovering} state handles the case where the bearing becomes invalid in flight, for example when an obstacle moves between the drone and the beacon. Its magnitude then drops below the trust threshold, and a controller without memory would have no heading to follow. If the bearing is invalid while the gradient still meets its reliability conditions, the drone enters \emph{recovering} and continues on the gradient direction alone, climbing the stored light field to move out of the obstacle's shadow. It returns to \emph{approaching} as soon as the bearing is valid again, and falls back to \emph{searching} only if the gradient estimate also becomes unreliable.

\section{Control Output}

The controller's primary output is a single target heading, obtained by merging the bearing direction $\psi_B$ and the gradient direction $\psi_G$. Both are angles, so the controller combines them with a weighted circular mean~\cite{mardia2000directional},
\begin{equation}
\psi = \operatorname{atan2}\!\big(w_B \sin\psi_B + w_G \sin\psi_G,\; w_B \cos\psi_B + w_G \cos\psi_G\big),
\label{eq:fusion}
\end{equation}
A plain numerical average is unsuitable here because angles wrap at $360^\circ$. Averaging $359^\circ$ and $1^\circ$ gives $180^\circ$, the opposite of the correct direction, whereas the circular mean returns $0^\circ$.

The weights $w_B$ and $w_G$ follow the validity of each input, as decided by the state machine. When both are valid, the controller sets $w_B = w_G$, so the heading is their bisector. When only one is valid, its weight is one and the other zero, and the controller follows that input alone. When neither is valid, there is no direction to command, and the drone reverts to the \emph{searching} behaviour of Section~\ref{sec:state-machine} rather than holding a fixed heading. With fixed, equal weights, the combination is a gated hand-off rather than a continuous, confidence-weighted fusion.

The controller also sets a forward speed. The drone flies forward only when it points at the target, so the speed is zero while it turns to align and a fixed cruising value once it is aligned and approaching. Holding the speed at zero during a large heading correction keeps the drone from drifting sideways.

The heading and forward speed are sent to the platform's own flight controller as setpoints, and the altitude is held at a fixed height throughout. The navigation controller thus operates only in the horizontal plane and performs no low-level attitude stabilisation or altitude control, which the flight controller already handles. Because the navigation communicates only through a heading and a speed, it is independent of the airframe beneath it. The same implementation runs in the Webots simulation and on the real CrazyFlie, and Chapter~\ref{chp:obstacle} shows it transferring unchanged to a physical blimp.
