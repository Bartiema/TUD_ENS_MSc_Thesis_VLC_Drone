\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chp:introduction}

\section{Motivation \& Problem Statement}

Micro-UAVs have expanded into applications such as indoor inspection, environmental monitoring, and inventory management, yet their capabilities remain constrained by short flight endurance and by restricted navigation in GPS-denied environments such as indoor greenhouses, where GPS is not accurate enough to provide the navigation these tasks require.

Light-based navigation requires only a simple modulator to turn existing lights into navigation beacons while the drone carries all sensing hardware, unlike other positioning and navigation systems that demand a far larger investment of infrastructure, resources, and time.

Existing VLC-based localisation systems achieve high accuracy but require dense lighting infrastructure, and approaches that work with a single light source are typically limited to simple target tracking and cannot navigate around obstacles.

Recent work has shown that a single light source can serve as a navigation beacon for micro-drones using a photodiode-based bearing estimation algorithm, but the resulting navigation remains reactive and does not exploit spatial information about the light environment.

This thesis addresses this limitation by introducing a gradient-ascent controller that fuses bearing information with a spatial light intensity map, enabling more robust and tunable autonomous navigation toward a light source.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis contributes: (1) a miniaturised photodiode PCB designed for weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drones; (2) a fused gradient-ascent navigation controller with tunable parameters for single-light-source navigation; and (3) experimental validation in both simulation and the real world on a micro-drone.

\section{Related Work}

This thesis draws on three threads of prior work---VLC-based indoor localisation, photodiode-based bearing estimation, and gradient-based source seeking---which are examined in detail in Chapter~\ref{chp:background}, and it is distinguished by combining single-light-source bearing sensing with a self-built gradient map to enable obstacle-aware navigation.

Alternative GPS-denied navigation approaches for drones---including UWB, WiFi fingerprinting, and visual SLAM---offer high accuracy but impose computational or infrastructure costs that are prohibitive for micro-drones.
