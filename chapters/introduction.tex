\section{Motivation \& Problem Statement}

Small unmanned aircraft now take on jobs once left to people, from inspecting structures to monitoring the environment and tracking stock in a warehouse~\cite{floreano2015drones}. Many of these jobs are indoors. That is where their navigation runs into trouble. Outdoors a drone can lean on GPS to know where it is. Indoors the signal is blocked or too coarse to navigate by. A drone working inside a warehouse or a greenhouse must find its own way without it~\cite{zafari2019indoor}.

Light offers a cheaper way to navigate indoors, because the spaces a drone works in are already lit~\cite{zhuang2018vlp}. An ordinary light becomes a beacon with no more than a modulator that blinks it faster than the eye can follow. The drone then carries all the sensing it needs on board. Other positioning systems instead demand infrastructure that must be installed and surveyed before the first flight.

Light-based methods face a trade-off between how much they can do and how many lights they require. Visible-light positioning systems reach centimetre-level accuracy~\cite{kuo2014luxapose}. They manage this only by reading a dense grid of modulated ceiling lights, which is a large installation to require. Methods that use a single light source ask far less of the space. They can only steer toward that one light in open space. They cannot tell several lights apart or route around an obstacle.

Prior work by Harry Huang navigates a micro-drone from a single light~\cite{huang2026lta}. A ring of photodiodes recovers a bearing toward the light. A single photodiode climbs the light gradient by sensing how its reading changes as the drone moves. These methods were validated in simulation, and they need no infrastructure beyond one beacon. They keep no memory of the light field, however. The drone can reach a single beacon in open space but cannot route around an obstacle. It also has no way to tell several beacons apart.

This gap is the challenge this thesis takes on. We want a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone to reach multiple light beacons and route around obstacles, using only on-board light sensing. A micro-drone can spare almost no weight, space, or power. That rules out the heavier sensors and the spare computation other approaches rely on. It leaves only sensing light enough to fly.

\section{Research Objectives}

This challenge breaks into three questions, which the thesis takes up in turn:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{rq:feasibility} Can such a drone carry the necessary light sensing on board and recover a reliable beacon signal in flight?
    \item \label{rq:multibeacon} Can it then navigate to several distinct light beacons in turn?
    \item \label{rq:obstacle} Can it navigate around an obstacle that blocks its line of sight to a beacon?
\end{enumerate}

The first asks whether the sensing is feasible within the drone's budget. Chapters~\ref{chp:hardware} and~\ref{chp:realworld} answer it with the photodiode board and the signal pipeline. The second and third ask whether that sensing supports the navigation the prior methods lacked. Chapters~\ref{chp:multibeacon} and~\ref{chp:obstacle} answer them in flight.

\section{Contributions}

To answer these questions, this thesis makes four contributions. Firstly, it presents a miniaturised photodiode board light enough to fly on a micro-drone. This board improves on an earlier Teensy-based prototype. Secondly, it develops a fused controller that combines bearing and gradient seeking. The gradient is estimated from all eight photodiodes, where prior work used only one. Thirdly, it builds multi-beacon and obstacle-avoiding navigation on a light map the drone constructs as it flies. Finally, it validates the approach in simulation and on a CrazyFlie~2.1. The same board and controller are then flown on a lighter-than-air blimp, which shows that the approach generalises across platforms~\cite{huang2026lta}.

\section{Thesis Overview}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{chp:background} reviews related work and the background theory. Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} analyses why existing methods fall short and presents the algorithm design. Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} covers the PCB hardware design.
