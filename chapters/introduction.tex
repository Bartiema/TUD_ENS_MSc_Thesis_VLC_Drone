\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chp:introduction}

\section{Motivation \& Problem Statement}

Micro-UAVs have expanded into applications such as indoor inspection, environmental monitoring, and inventory management, yet their capabilities remain constrained by short flight endurance and by restricted navigation in GPS-denied environments such as indoor greenhouses, where GPS is not accurate enough to provide the navigation these tasks require.

Light-based navigation requires only a simple modulator to turn existing lights into navigation beacons while the drone carries all sensing hardware, unlike other positioning and navigation systems that demand a far larger investment of infrastructure, resources, and time.

Existing VLC-based localisation systems achieve high accuracy but require dense lighting infrastructure, and approaches that work with a single light source are typically limited to simple target tracking and cannot navigate around obstacles.

Recent work by Harry Huang demonstrated light-based navigation for micro-drones using a photodiode bearing algorithm and a single-photodiode gradient method, but this platform can only reach a single beacon in open space and cannot navigate around obstacles~\cite{}.

The central challenge this thesis addresses is enabling a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone to navigate to multiple light beacons and around obstacles using only on-board light sensing.

This thesis addresses this challenge with a fused controller that combines bearing and gradient information, estimates the gradient from all eight photodiodes rather than one, and builds a spatial light map that supports obstacle-aware, multi-beacon navigation.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis contributes: (1) a miniaturised photodiode PCB that improves on an earlier Teensy-based prototype for weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drones; (2) a fused navigation controller that combines bearing and gradient seeking and estimates the gradient from all eight photodiodes, where prior work used a single photodiode; (3) multi-beacon and obstacle-avoiding navigation built on a self-constructed light map; and (4) validation in simulation and on a CrazyFlie~2.1, with prior blimp results showing the approach generalises across platforms.
