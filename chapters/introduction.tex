\section{Motivation \& Problem Statement}

Small unmanned aircraft now take on jobs once left to people. They inspect structures, monitor the environment, and track stock in a warehouse~\cite{floreano2015drones}. Many of these jobs are indoors. That is where their navigation runs into trouble. Outdoors a drone can lean on GPS to know where it is. Light-based methods face a trade-off between how much they can do and how many lights they require. Visible-light positioning systems can reach centimetre-level accuracy~\cite{kuo2014luxapose}, but they manage this only by reading a dense grid of modulated ceiling lights. This does mean that a large installation is required. Methods that use a single light source ask far less of spaces a drone might operate in. However, then they can only steer toward that one light in open space. They cannot tell several lights apart or route around an obstacle.

None of his methods keep memory of the light field, however. The drone can reach a single beacon in open space but cannot route around an obstacle. It also has no way to tell several beacons apart.

This gap is the challenge this thesis takes on. We want a weight-, size-, and power-constrained micro-drone to reach multiple light beacons and route around obstacles using only on-board light sensing. A micro-drone can spare almost no weight, space, or power. That rules out the heavier sensors and the spare computation other approaches rely on. It leaves only sensing that is light enough to fly.

\section{Research Objectives}

This challenge breaks into three questions, which the thesis takes up in turn:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{rq:feasibility} Can such a drone carry the necessary light sensing on board and recover a reliable beacon signal in flight?
    \item \label{rq:multibeacon} Can it then navigate to several distinct light beacons in turn?
    \item \label{rq:obstacle} Can it navigate around an obstacle that blocks its line of sight to a beacon?
\end{enumerate}

The first asks whether the sensing is feasible within the drone's budget. Chapters~\ref{chp:hardware} and~\ref{chp:realworld} answer it with the photodiode board and the signal pipeline. The second and third ask whether that sensing supports the navigation the prior methods lacked. Chapters~\ref{chp:multibeacon} and~\ref{chp:obstacle} answer them.

\section{Contributions}

To answer these questions, this thesis makes four contributions. Firstly, it presents a small photodiode board light enough to fly on a micro-drone. This board improves on an earlier Teensy-based prototype. Secondly, it develops a fused controller that combines bearing and gradient seeking. The gradient is estimated from all eight photodiodes, where prior work used only one. We choose its estimator by comparing several candidates on the same light field. Thirdly, it builds multi-beacon and obstacle-avoiding navigation on a light map the drone constructs as it flies. Finally, it validates the approach in simulation and on a CrazyFlie~2.1.
