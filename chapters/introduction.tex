\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chp:introduction}

\section{Motivation \& Problem Statement}

Micro-UAVs have expanded into applications such as indoor inspection, environmental monitoring, and inventory management, yet their capabilities remain constrained by short flight endurance and restricted navigation in GPS-denied environments.

Light-based navigation offers a cost-effective solution for GPS-denied environments, as only a simple modulator is needed to turn existing lights into navigation beacons while the drone carries all sensing hardware.

Existing VLC-based localisation systems achieve high accuracy but require dense lighting infrastructure, and approaches that work with a single light source are typically limited to bearing estimation, which does not support spatial navigation around obstacles.

Recent work has shown that a single light source can serve as a navigation beacon for micro-drones using a photodiode-based bearing estimation algorithm, but the resulting navigation remains reactive and does not exploit spatial information about the light environment.

This thesis addresses this limitation by introducing a gradient-ascent controller that fuses bearing information with a spatial light intensity map, enabling more robust and tunable autonomous navigation toward a light source.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis contributes: (1) a fused gradient-ascent navigation controller with tunable parameters for single-light-source navigation; (2) a miniaturised photodiode PCB designed to fit the CrazyFlie~2.1 platform; and (3) a real-world experimental validation on a micro-drone.

\section{Related Work}

Visible-light communication systems have achieved centimetre-level indoor localisation accuracy, but typically require dense lighting infrastructure and are not designed for deployment on weight-constrained micro-drones.

Alternative GPS-denied navigation approaches for drones---including UWB, WiFi fingerprinting, and visual SLAM---offer high accuracy but impose significant computational or infrastructure costs that are prohibitive for micro-drones.

Photodiode-based angle-of-arrival estimation provides a lightweight mechanism for determining the direction of a single light source, with prior work showing that 8 photodiodes are sufficient for reliable bearing estimates.

Gradient-based and potential-field navigation methods have been widely used in robotics to guide agents toward a goal using a spatial map, but have not been applied to light-intensity fields for drone navigation.
