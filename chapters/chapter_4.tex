\chapter{Real-World Implementation}
\label{chp:realworld}

This chapter turns the board's raw readings into a signal the controller can trust in flight. We describe the pipeline that recovers each beacon from the noisy photodiode voltage and reports it as a signal-to-noise ratio. We then validate what each filtering stage contributes while the drone hovers. Finally we measure how ambient light eats into the signal.

\section{From Intensity to SNR}

In the Webots simulation each photodiode is a built-in light sensor that reports the light landing on it directly from a modelled point light source, as described in Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal}, so the light signal is read straight off the sensor with no further processing. The real platform allows no such direct reading. On the drone each photodiode reports a single voltage in which the target beacon, the ambient room lighting, sunlight through a window, and reflections off the walls and floor are all summed together. The raw intensity is not meaningless, but on its own it is an unreliable navigation cue. It cannot tell us which part of the light comes from the beacon we are trying to reach and which part comes from everything else.

To separate the beacon from this mixture we give each beacon its own modulation frequency and recover it in the frequency domain, exactly as introduced in Section~\ref{sec:lightsignal}. We sample each channel over a short window and take a fast Fourier transform, in which a beacon appears as a peak at its modulation frequency. The height of that peak is the light signal $m_n$ that the bearing estimator and the light map consume. We call the ratio of the peak to the surrounding noise the signal-to-noise ratio, or SNR, and it tells us how far a reading can be trusted. Modulation does not make the beacon immune to its surroundings: a strong enough background light leaves little of the sensor's range for the beacon's own modulation, so its peak shrinks and can be lost, an effect we return to in Section~\ref{sec:ambient}. What it buys us is the ability to attribute a measured signal to a specific beacon and to reject readings whose SNR is too low to act on. We demonstrate this beacon separation in Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon}.

This frequency analysis only helps if the noise does not masquerade as signal, and in flight there is a great deal of noise to contend with. The spinning motors couple mechanical vibration into the board and draw current that the analogue front end picks up as electrical interference, and light reflecting off nearby surfaces returns delayed copies of the beacon by indirect paths. Much of this energy sits at frequencies well above the beacons' modulation band, and if it is sampled without being removed first it does not simply vanish: it folds back, or aliases, into the lower frequencies where the beacons live and corrupts the very peaks we depend on~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}. Suppressing this noise is the job of the filters described in the rest of this section, split between the analogue domain before sampling and the digital domain after.

The first filter acts in the analogue domain, before the signal is ever sampled. The board populates a first-order low-pass filter with a cut-off of $f_c = 500$\,Hz onto the optional-filter pads provided by the photodiode board of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}, so that high-frequency content is attenuated before it can alias. We sample each channel at $f_s = 500$\,Hz, which places the Nyquist limit at 250\,Hz, and the beacons modulate at 250\,Hz or below, so the signals of interest are sampled correctly. The cut-off was matched to the sample rate rather than tuned tightly to the beacon band. It is best understood as a loose anti-aliasing measure that removes the worst out-of-band energy, not as a sharply placed filter. A first-order roll-off is gentle, so content just above the Nyquist limit is only weakly attenuated and some of it still aliases into the band. What the filter buys us is the removal of the strongest high-frequency energy, while the beacons themselves sit well below Nyquist where they are sampled cleanly.

The remaining filters act in software, on each sampled window, and build on two steps the base frequency analysis already performs. Before the transform it subtracts the mean of the window. This removes the constant offset, so only the fluctuating part of the signal, where the modulation lives, reaches the transform. It also multiplies the window by a Hamming window, so the abrupt ends of the window do not smear energy across the spectrum as leakage, a problem Harris~\cite{harris1978windows} surveys in detail. The transform itself has a frequency resolution of $\Delta f = f_s / N$, which for an $N = 256$ point transform at 500\,Hz is about 1.95\,Hz per bin, fine enough to separate beacons spaced a few hertz apart.

First, we reduce the variance of the spectrum itself. Rather than read a peak from a single transform, we average the magnitude spectra of several successive windows that overlap by half their length. This averaging of overlapping windows is a standard technique introduced by Welch~\cite{welch1967}. For independent windows it would lower the variance of the estimate by a factor of $N_{\text{avg}}$, but the overlap leaves successive windows correlated, so the gain is smaller, and with only $N_{\text{avg}} = 2$ windows it is modest. The cost is latency, since each averaged spectrum spans several windows and the signal then responds more slowly to the drone's motion. We therefore keep $N_{\text{avg}}$ small and accept the marginal smoothing in exchange for a responsive reading.

Second, we make the noise floor robust to spikes. We want an estimate that an occasional motor harmonic or a pickup at the mains frequency cannot inflate, so over the off-target bins we take the median rather than the mean, since the median is barely moved by such outliers. The floor is taken over all spectrum bins except a guard band around the beacon frequency and its first four harmonics, which we exclude so the beacon's own energy is not counted as noise. This costs little, since computing a median means sorting the bins rather than summing them, but at this window size the expense is negligible on the drone's microcontroller.

Third, we smooth the per-channel magnitude over time. Each channel's freshly measured magnitude $\hat{m}_n$ is blended with its previous smoothed value through a first-order exponential moving average, an infinite-impulse-response low-pass filter~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp},
\begin{equation}
m_n[k] = \alpha_m\, m_n[k-1] + (1 - \alpha_m)\,\hat{m}_n[k],
\label{eq:magiir}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_m \in [0,1]$ sets how much of the old value is retained. The same $\alpha_m$ is applied to all eight channels so that their relative magnitudes, which encode the bearing to the beacon, are preserved while the jitter that vibration adds is suppressed. The factor trades responsiveness against smoothness: a larger $\alpha_m$ gives a steadier but slower signal, a smaller one a faster but noisier signal, and we use $\alpha_m = 0.5$. Because this smoothing sits on top of the two-frame averaging of the first filter, the magnitude the controller finally acts on responds more slowly to the drone's motion than either stage would on its own. Smoothing the magnitude on its own would make the SNR lag behind the noise actually present, so the SNR used by the gate is reconstructed as this smoothed magnitude divided by the current-frame noise floor, which keeps the gate calibrated. The values of $\alpha_m$, $N_{\text{avg}}$, the guard band, and the other pipeline parameters are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:parameters}.

\section{Filter Validation}

We test whether each stage of the pipeline earns its place and what it contributes in flight. The drone is held at a fixed pose, 95\,cm from a single 150\,Hz beacon, so the true signal is constant and any variation we record over time is the noise and jitter the filters are meant to remove. From each held run we take two numbers: the mean SNR, which reflects the signal level, and the standard deviation of the SNR, which reflects its jitter.

We build the pipeline up one stage at a time, starting from no filtering and adding the hardware filter, then Welch averaging, then the median floor, then the magnitude smoothing, so each stage's contribution can be read on its own. Each configuration is measured in two states: with the motors off, holding the drone at the flight altitude on a rig, and in free hover with the motors running, since the spinning motors are the main source of the vibration and electrical interference the filters target. The motors-off readings give a clean ceiling, and the gap to the motors-on readings is the cost of flight.

    \label{tab:filter-snr}
    \begin{tabular}{l c c}
        \hline
        Configuration & Motors off & Motors on \\
        \hline
        No filtering        & $23.9 \pm 3.9$  & $13.9 \pm 2.1$ \\
        + Hardware          & $17.4 \pm 1.3$  & $11.4 \pm 2.8$ \\
        + Welch averaging   & $16.3 \pm 0.9$  & $15.1 \pm 4.6$ \\
        + Median floor      & $74.2 \pm 19.2$ & $31.9 \pm 7.5$ \\
        + Magnitude EMA     & $76.6 \pm 17.8$ & $32.1 \pm 5.3$ \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The result in Table~\ref{tab:filter-snr} is dominated by the median noise floor. The hardware filter and Welch averaging, added on their own, change the level only slightly, and the hardware filter even lowers it. The median floor then transforms the result, more than doubling the in-flight SNR from about 15 to 32 and more than quadrupling the motors-off SNR from about 16 to 74. The reason lies in the definition of the SNR as the peak divided by the noise floor: the median ignores the occasional spectral spike that inflates a mean-based floor, so the same peak is divided by a smaller and more representative noise level, and the SNR it yields is correspondingly larger.

The magnitude smoothing adds little to the level but steadies the reading, lowering the in-flight standard deviation from 7.5 to 5.3. The standard deviations grow at the median-floor step only because the SNR values themselves grow, so the cleanest measure of the smoothing is this last step, where the mean barely changes. Flight remains costly throughout: even with the full pipeline the in-flight SNR of about 32 sits well below the motors-off ceiling of about 77, confirming that the spinning motors are the main disturbance the pipeline must absorb.

The hardware filter deserves a second look, since on its own it did not raise the SNR. We therefore measured the full pipeline once more with the filter unpopulated, in Table~\ref{tab:filter-hw}. Removing it drops the SNR from about 77 to 33 with the motors off and from 32 to 22 in flight, so it is not redundant. It does little before the median floor is in place, but the two are complementary: by removing aliased high-frequency energy it keeps the noise floor low, and together they give the largest signal.

Two limitations follow from the setup. In free hover the drone drifts a little, so the motors-on standard deviation includes a small contribution from the changing pose rather than from jitter alone, though the mean is unaffected by unbiased drift. The runs were flown under the same ambient room lighting as the experiment of Section~\ref{sec:ambient}, held constant across all configurations, so the lighting does not confound the comparison between them.

\section{Effect of Ambient Lighting}
\label{sec:ambient}

Ambient light is not really a source of noise; the motors, electrical interference, and reflections dominate that. Instead it raises the steady baseline that each photodiode reads. A photodiode has only a limited range, so a higher baseline leaves less room for the beacon's modulation to swing. The signal then varies less between its high and its low, less energy reaches the beacon's frequency bin, and the SNR drops. In a darkened room the baseline is low, the modulation swings freely, and the SNR rises.

To test this we flew the same two-beacon traverse twice from the same take-off point, once in the dark and once with the room lights on. Under the room lights the photodiodes read roughly 360 to 450\,lux facing up and 120 to 160\,lux to the side at flight altitude, against 12 to 22\,lux and 2 to 22\,lux respectively in the dark. Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-traversal} shows the two flight paths over the light maps the drone built, and Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-snr} shows the overall SNR over each flight, taken as the maximum across the eight channels. The dark flight holds a higher SNR throughout, peaking above 30 against around 20 in the light. This confirms the effect: the lower baseline gives the beacon a stronger signal in its frequency bin. Because the drone steers itself, the two flights follow somewhat different paths, and we flew a single run per condition, so these figures are indicative of the effect rather than an exact like-for-like comparison.
