\chapter{Real-World Implementation}
\label{chp:realworld}

This chapter develops the signal processing pipeline that recovers each beacon from the photodiode signal on the real platform. Unlike in simulation, where the light intensity can be read directly, each photodiode here delivers a single noisy voltage in which the beacon is buried. The pipeline recovers the beacon and reports, alongside each reading, a signal-to-noise ratio that quantifies how far the reading can be trusted. We first describe the pipeline stage by stage, from the analogue anti-aliasing filter through the frequency analysis to the per-channel smoothing. We then validate it in flight, isolating the contribution of each stage to the reported signal-to-noise ratio. Finally, we measure how ambient light affects the pipeline, and show that it lowers the reported ratio.

\section{From Intensity to SNR}
\label{sec:intensity-to-snr}

In simulation the per-sensor light signal requires no recovery. As Section~\ref{sec:simulation} describes, the drone reads $S_n$ directly, and it represents the light intensity alone. However, the real platform cannot perform navigation simply based on the light intensity value. On the drone each photodiode reports a single voltage in which the target beacon, the ambient room lighting, sunlight through a window, and reflections off the walls and floor are summed together. This raw voltage is not meaningless, but on its own it is an unreliable basis for navigation, because it does not separate the target beacon's light from the background noise.

To isolate each beacon from background noise, we modulate each target beacon at a distinct frequency and recover its signal in the frequency domain. Section~\ref{sec:multibeacon-separation} introduced the principle: a Fourier transform decomposes a photodiode signal into the strength carried at each frequency, and a beacon modulated at a fixed rate appears as a peak at that frequency. On the real platform, we apply the same analysis to each channel. For each photodiode, the controller measures the channel magnitude $\hat{S}_n$. This measurement is obtained by collecting a short window of samples and computing a fast Fourier transform over them. The magnitude of the peak at the beacon's modulation frequency is then $\hat{S}_n$, which the controller uses. However, electrical noise from onboard devices can still introduce jittering noise, so we further smooth the readings over time to obtain a more stable signal. Finally, the processed signals $S_n$ are passed to the bearing and gradient estimators, which compute the direction toward the light source.


The height of this peak, $\hat{S}_n$, does not on its own state how reliable the reading is. A faint beacon seen across a bright, noisy room can raise a peak height that does not stand out from the fluctuations around it, whereas a strong beacon stands well clear out of them. We therefore pair every reading with a signal-to-noise ratio, or SNR. The SNR is the height of the beacon's peak divided by the noise floor. The noise floor is the magnitude at the frequencies other than the target beacon's. An SNR of one means the peak is no taller than the noise floor, so the beacon cannot be told apart from the noise and the reading cannot be trusted. A large SNR means the beacon dominates its frequency bin and the reading is reliable. Figure~\ref{fig:snr-spectrum} shows an example, in which a single beacon stands as a peak well above the noise floor. The controller uses the SNR in two ways. First, each SNR value is tied to a beacon: because the value comes from a peak in a specific frequency bin, we attribute it to the beacon whose modulation frequency matches that bin. Second, it gates out any reading whose SNR falls below a threshold. Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon} reports flights that reach several beacons in turn, where the drone is able to identify different target beacons by their own modulation frequency.

Modulation does not make the signal from the beacon immune to the effects of the surrounding environment. A strong enough background light leaves little of the photodiode's sensing range for the beacon's modulation, so the peak can saturate, an effect we return to in Section~\ref{sec:ambient}. What modulation does help with is the ability to attribute a measured signal to a specific beacon and to reject readings whose SNR is too low to act on.

The frequency analysis that recovers the beacon is only useful if noise does not contaminate its frequency bin, and flight introduces a great deal of noise. First, the spinning motors couple mechanical vibration into the board. Second, the motors also draw current that the analogue-to-digital converter for the photodiodes picks up as electrical interference. Third, light reflecting off nearby surfaces returns delayed copies of the beacon by indirect paths. Although most of this noise lies well above the beacons' modulation band, it can fold back, or alias, into the lower frequencies where the beacons lie, and corrupt the peaks the analysis depends on~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the complete pipeline that suppresses this noise, from the sampled voltage to the reported signal $S_n$ and its SNR. It is divided between the analogue domain, before sampling, and the digital domain after it, and the following paragraphs describe each stage in turn.

To prevent high-frequency contributions induced by noise from aliasing into the sampled signal, we apply an initial analogue filtering stage. The photodiode board of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} provides optional-filter pads, onto which we populate a first-order low-pass filter with a cut-off of $f_c = 500$\,Hz,  so that high-frequency content is attenuated before it can alias. Each photodiode signal is also sampled at $f_s = 500$\,Hz. According to the sampling theorem, the highest frequency that can be represented without aliasing is the Nyquist frequency, equal to half the sample rate, which is $250$\,Hz in our system~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}. Any frequency components above this $250$\,Hz limit are folded back into the sampled spectrum and may therefore negatively impact our measured signals. The placed hardware filter is not intended to sharply isolate the beacon band. Instead, its purpose is to attenuate the strongest out-of-band energy before it reaches the ADC, thereby reducing the severity of aliasing. Because the filter is only first order and its cut-off frequency lies above the Nyquist limit, attenuation around and above $250$\,Hz is relatively weak, and some aliased content can still enter the sampled signal. The beacon modulation frequencies, however, lie well below the Nyquist frequency and are therefore sampled faithfully.

However, even with hardware filtering, some noise remains within the $0$--$250$\,Hz band that the $500$\,Hz sampling rate captures. We therefore filter each collected window of samples in software, in five steps. The first two prepare the window for the Fourier transform: subtracting the mean and applying a Hamming window. The remaining three reduce the noise in the result: Welch averaging, a median noise floor, and temporal smoothing.

First, we remove the steady ambient component from the signal. Ambient light introduces a constant offset in the measured intensity, and subtracting this offset leaves only the fluctuating modulated signal for further processing. Second, we multiply the sampled window by a Hamming window, a smooth taper that suppresses the abrupt discontinuity at the window boundaries. Without this taper, the discontinuity would smear energy across the spectrum as spectral leakage, a problem surveyed in detail by Harris~\cite{harris1978windows}. Figure~\ref{fig:windowing} shows both preprocessing steps on a sampled window, together with the leakage suppressed by the Hamming window. The transform has a frequency resolution of $\Delta f = f_s / N$. For an $N=256$ point transform sampled at 500\,Hz, this gives about 1.95\,Hz per bin, which is fine enough to separate beacons spaced a few hertz apart.

Third, we reduce the variance of the spectrum estimate itself. A single FFT gives a noisy estimate of each bin's magnitude, and that noise raises occasional bins in the floor, which lowers the reported SNR. Rather than read the peak from one FFT, we average the magnitude spectra of several successive windows that overlap by half their length. This averaging of overlapping windows is a standard variance-reduction technique introduced by Welch~\cite{welch1967}. For statistically independent windows it would reduce the variance of the estimate by a factor of $N_{\text{avg}}$. The half-window overlap leaves successive windows correlated, however, so the reduction is smaller, and with only $N_{\text{avg}} = 2$ windows it is modest. The cost of averaging is latency: each averaged spectrum spans several windows, so the reported signal responds more slowly to the drone's motion. We therefore keep $N_{\text{avg}}$ small, accepting only marginal smoothing in exchange for a responsive reading. Figure~\ref{fig:welch} contrasts a single noisy spectrum with the averaged one, in which the noise floor is visibly smoother while the beacon peak is unchanged.

Averaging the magnitude spectra of several windows reduces this scatter and leaves a smoother, more representative floor, while the beacon peak is unaffected.}
    \label{fig:welch}
\end{figure}

Fourth, we make the noise floor robust to spikes. The noise floor is the representative magnitude of the spectrum away from the beacon, and it forms the denominator of the SNR. A single loud disturbance, such as a motor harmonic or a pickup at the mains frequency, appears as a tall spurious bin that would inflate a mean-based floor and make every SNR smaller. We therefore estimate the floor with the median of the off-target bins rather than their mean, because the median is barely moved by a few extreme values. We compute it over all spectrum bins except a guard band around the beacon's frequency and its first four harmonics, which we exclude so that the beacon's own energy is not counted as noise. The median costs only a sort of the bins rather than a sum, an expense that is negligible at this window size on the drone's microcontroller. Figure~\ref{fig:snr-spectrum} shows the difference the median makes: the interference spikes lift a mean-based floor noticeably, while the median floor stays on the broadband level the SNR should be measured against.

Finally, we smooth the per-channel magnitude over time, because the magnitude still fluctuates from one frame to the next. We blend each channel's freshly measured magnitude $\hat{S}_n$ with its previous smoothed value through a first-order exponential moving average, an infinite-impulse-response (IIR) low-pass filter that weights recent measurements most heavily while retaining a fading memory of older ones~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp},
\begin{equation}
S_n[k] = \alpha_m\, S_n[k-1] + (1 - \alpha_m)\,\hat{S}_n[k],
\label{eq:magiir}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_m \in [0,1]$ sets how much of the previous value is retained. We apply the same $\alpha_m$ to all eight channels, so that their relative magnitudes, which encode the bearing to the beacon, are preserved while the jitter that vibration adds is suppressed. The factor trades responsiveness against smoothness: a larger $\alpha_m$ gives a steadier but slower signal, a smaller one a faster but noisier signal, and we use $\alpha_m = 0.5$. Figure~\ref{fig:ema} shows the effect of this filtering process, where the smoothed signal is far steadier and lags only slightly where the true signal jitters.

This IIR smoothing sits on top of the Welch averaging, and both introduce some lag in the final magnitude $S_n$, which also delays the rest of the system. However, because we keep the drone's flight speed slow and average only two windows, the induced delay is small enough not to affect flight performance.

The raw per-frame magnitude $\hat{S}_n$ scatters widely, whereas the smoothed magnitude $S_n$ is far steadier. The price is a short lag after the step, which a larger $\alpha_m$ would lengthen.}

The smoothing improves the magnitude our algorithms from Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} act on, but it must not distort the SNR for the validity-check used by our controller from Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}. This check decides, frame by frame, whether a reading can be trusted, so its SNR must reflect the noise present at that moment. Dividing the smoothed magnitude by an equally smoothed noise floor would make the SNR lag a sudden rise in noise, and the controller could keep trusting a reading after it has already become unreliable. We therefore reconstruct SNR each frame as the smoothed magnitude $S_n$ divided by the current-frame noise floor. The numerator is then stable while the denominator tracks the noise present now, which keeps the validity-check calibrated to current conditions. Appendix~\ref{app:parameters} lists $\alpha_m$, $N_{\text{avg}}$, the guard band, and the other pipeline parameters. 


\section{Filter Validation}
\label{sec:filter-validation}

We now test what each stage of the signal processing pipeline of the previous section contributes in flight, and whether each is justified. We hold the drone at a fixed position, 95\,cm from a single 150\,Hz beacon, so that the true signal is constant and any variation recorded over time is the noise and jitter that the filters are meant to remove. From each held run we take two numbers: the mean SNR, which reflects the signal level, and the standard deviation of the SNR, which reflects its jitter.

We do tests with one stage of the pipeline at a time, starting with no filtering and then adding the hardware filter, Welch averaging, the median floor, and finally the magnitude smoothing. This allows the contribution of each filtering stage to be analysed separately. We measure each configuration under two conditions. In the first, the motors are off and the drone is held at a fixed altitude. In the second, the drone hovers freely with the motors running, since the spinning motors are the main source of vibration and electrical interference that the filters target. The motors-off readings provide a clean reference, while the motors-on readings show how the SNR drops once the drone begins flying and motor-induced noise is introduced.

One limitation in this experiment is due to the high degree of freedom of the drone. In free hover the drone drifts slightly, so the recorded SNR from the motors-on experiment includes a small deviation from the changing pose rather than from the environment noise alone, although unbiased drift leaves the mean unaffected.

    \label{tab:filter-snr}
    \begin{tabular}{l c c}
        \hline
        Configuration & Motors off & Motors on \\
        \hline
        No filtering        & $23.9 \pm 3.9$  & $13.9 \pm 2.1$ \\

        + Magnitude EMA     & $76.6 \pm 17.8$ & $32.1 \pm 5.3$ \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The result in Table~\ref{tab:filter-snr} is dominated by the median noise floor stage. Introducing the hardware filter has a slight negative effect on the overall SNR. Welch averaging raises the level only slightly. The median floor then greatly improves the result, more than doubling the in-flight SNR from about 15 to 32 and more than quadrupling the motors-off SNR from about 16 to 74. The reason lies in the definition of the SNR as the peak divided by the noise floor: the median ignores the occasional spike that would increase the mean-based floor, since a mean is affected more by outliers. Because of this, the median-based floor is much smaller than the mean-based floor, so dividing the same peak by it yields a correspondingly larger SNR.

The magnitude smoothing filter increases the SNR value slightly but steadies the reading, lowering the in-flight standard deviation from 7.5 to 5.3. The standard deviation grows at the median-floor filter only because the SNR values themselves increase. This last stage therefore gives the highest SNR and the lowest standard deviation of any configuration. Even with the full pipeline the in-flight SNR of about 32 sits well below the motors-off ceiling of about 77, which confirms that the spinning motors are the main disturbance the pipeline must absorb.

Because the hardware filter initially has a negative effect on the SNR, we ran an additional experiment to justify its presence. We tested the full pipeline once more but without the filter in place, reported in Table~\ref{tab:filter-hw}. Removing the filter drops the SNR from about 77 to 33 with the motors off, and from 32 to 22 in flight. This shows that the filter is not redundant. It has little effect before the median floor stage is in place, but the two are complementary. The hardware filter removes high-frequency energy, which keeps more noise out of the noise floor; combined with the median floor stage, which lowers the floor value further, this yields the highest SNR. Without the hardware filter, even the median-based noise floor can be overwhelmed by noise from high-frequency sources.

\section{Effect of Ambient Lighting}
\label{sec:ambient}

The pipeline of the previous sections recovers the beacon's signal against the background noise, but another factor limits the signal's resolution: the level of ambient light in the room. Ambient light is not itself a dominant source of noise, unlike the motors, electrical interference, and wall reflections. Instead, it introduces a steady offset in the light intensity that each photodiode reads. A photodiode has a limited output range, so a higher nominal value leaves a smaller portion of the sensing range for the beacon's modulation to occupy. The received signal then can only swing less between its high and low states, less energy reaches the beacon's frequency bin, and the SNR drops. In a darkened room this static baseline light intensity value is low, the modulation swings freely, and the SNR rises. Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-baseline} illustrates the mechanism: a low baseline leaves the full sensor range for the beacon's swing, whereas a high baseline pushes the reading toward the upper limit and clips the swing, so less of the modulation survives to reach the beacon's frequency bin.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ambient_baseline.png}
    \caption{How a higher ambient baseline shrinks the beacon's modulation, on generated data. The beacon switches the lamp on and off, which should produce the same swing in both rooms. (a) In the dark the low baseline leaves the whole range free, so the full swing is captured. (b) Under room lighting the high baseline sits close to the sensor's upper limit, so the on-phase is clipped and the captured swing is much smaller, which lowers the energy at the beacon's frequency and so the SNR.}
    \label{fig:ambient-baseline}
\end{figure}

To validate the influence of ambient light, we fly the same two-beacon traversal twice from the same take-off point, once in the dark and once with the room lights on. With the room lights on, the photodiodes read roughly 360 to 450\,lux when facing upward and 120 to 160\,lux when facing sideways at flight altitude, compared with 12 to 22\,lux and 2 to 22\,lux respectively in the dark. Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-traversal} shows the two flight paths over the light maps built by the drone, and Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-snr} shows the overall SNR in each flight, where we show the maximum SNR among the eight channels for each timestamp. The dark flight maintains a higher SNR throughout the experiment, reaching above 30 compared with about 20 when ambient light is present. This confirms the influence of ambient light: a lower baseline intensity leaves more dynamic range for the beacon modulation, allowing more signal energy to appear in the beacon's frequency bin. Because the drone steers itself, the two flights follow somewhat different paths, and we fly a single run per condition, so these results show the effect of ambient light rather than provide a perfect comparison between a room with ambient light and one without.
