\chapter{Real-World Implementation}
\label{chp:realworld}

On the real drone, the per-sensor light signal $S_n$ that the controller depends on is not available directly. Each photodiode delivers a single noisy voltage in which the beacon is buried among ambient light, electrical interference, and mechanical vibration. This chapter develops the signal pipeline that recovers the beacon from that voltage and reports, alongside each reading, a signal-to-noise ratio that quantifies how far the reading can be trusted. We first describe the pipeline stage by stage, from the analogue anti-aliasing filter through the frequency analysis to the per-channel smoothing. We then validate the pipeline in flight, isolating what each stage contributes to the recovered signal. Finally, we measure how ambient light degrades the signal and confirm the mechanism behind it.

\section{From Intensity to SNR}

In simulation the per-sensor light signal requires no recovery. As Section~\ref{sec:simulation} describes, each photodiode is a modelled light sensor that reports the intensity reaching it directly, so $S_n$ is read straight from the sensor. The real platform allows no such direct reading. On the drone each photodiode reports a single voltage in which the target beacon, the ambient room lighting, sunlight through a window, and reflections off the walls and floor are summed together. This raw voltage is not meaningless, but on its own it is an unreliable navigation cue, because it cannot separate the light arriving from the target beacon from everything else the sensor sees.

To isolate the beacon from this mixture, we give each beacon its own modulation frequency and recover it in the frequency domain. Section~\ref{sec:multibeacon-separation} introduced the principle: a Fourier transform of a photodiode signal decomposes it into the strength carried at each frequency, and a beacon modulated at a fixed rate appears as a peak at that frequency, as Figure~\ref{fig:fft-mock} illustrates. On the real platform we apply the same analysis to each channel. We sample the channel over a short window and take a fast Fourier transform, then read the height of the peak at the beacon's modulation frequency as that channel's freshly measured magnitude $\hat{S}_n$. After the smoothing described below, this becomes the signal $S_n$ that the bearing estimator and the light map consume.

The height of this peak, $\hat{S}_n$, does not on its own state how reliable the reading is. A faint beacon seen across a bright, noisy room can raise a peak no taller than the random fluctuations around it, whereas a strong beacon stands well clear of them. We therefore pair every reading with a signal-to-noise ratio, or SNR. The SNR is the height of the beacon's peak divided by the noise floor. The noise floor is the typical magnitude of the spectrum in the bins away from the beacon, against which the peak must stand out. An SNR of one means the peak is no taller than the noise floor, so the beacon cannot be told apart from the noise and the reading cannot be trusted. A large SNR means the beacon dominates its frequency bin and the reading is reliable. Figure~\ref{fig:snr-spectrum} shows an example, in which a single beacon stands as a peak well above the noise floor. The controller uses the SNR in two ways: it attributes each peak to a specific beacon, and it gates out any reading whose SNR falls below a threshold. Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon} reports flights that reach several beacons in turn, each identified by its own modulation frequency.

Modulation does not make the signal the beacon produces immune to the effects of the surrounding environment. A strong enough background light leaves little of the photodiode's range for the detection of the beacon's modulation, so its peak shrinks and can be lost, an effect we return to in Section~\ref{sec:ambient}. What modulation does buy is the ability to attribute a measured signal to a specific beacon and to reject readings whose SNR is too low to act on.

The frequency analysis that recovers the beacon is only useful if noise does not contaminate its frequency bin, and flight introduces a great deal of noise. The spinning motors couple mechanical vibration into the board. They also draw current that the analogue front end picks up as electrical interference. Light reflecting off nearby surfaces returns delayed copies of the beacon by indirect paths. Much of this energy sits at frequencies well above the beacons' modulation band. Sampling it without first removing it does not discard it: the energy folds back, or aliases, into the lower frequencies where the beacons lie, and corrupts the peaks the analysis depends on~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the complete pipeline that suppresses this noise, from the sampled voltage to the reported signal $S_n$ and its SNR. It is divided between the analogue domain, before sampling, and the digital domain after it, and the following paragraphs describe each stage in turn.

The first filter acts in the analogue domain, before the signal is sampled. The photodiode board of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} provides optional-filter pads, onto which we populate a first-order low-pass filter with a cut-off of $f_c = 500$\,Hz, so that high-frequency content is attenuated before it can alias. We sample each channel at $f_s = 500$\,Hz. The sampling theorem places the highest faithfully represented frequency, the Nyquist limit, at half the sample rate, here 250\,Hz~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp}. The beacons modulate at or below this limit, so the signals of interest are sampled correctly. We match the cut-off to the sample rate rather than tuning it tightly to the beacon band, so it is best understood as a loose anti-aliasing measure that removes the worst out-of-band energy, not as a sharply placed filter. A first-order roll-off is gentle, so content just above the Nyquist limit is only weakly attenuated and some of it still aliases into the band. What the filter removes is the strongest high-frequency energy, while the beacons themselves lie well below Nyquist, where they are sampled cleanly.

The remaining filters act in software, on each sampled window. They build on two preprocessing steps that the frequency analysis already performs. First, it subtracts the mean of the window, which removes the constant ambient offset so that only the fluctuating part of the signal, where the modulation lives, reaches the transform. Second, it multiplies the window by a Hamming window, a smooth taper that suppresses the abrupt discontinuity at the window's ends. Without that taper the discontinuity would smear energy across the spectrum as spectral leakage, a problem Harris~\cite{harris1978windows} surveys in detail. Figure~\ref{fig:windowing} shows both steps on a sampled window, together with the leakage that the Hamming window suppresses. The transform has a frequency resolution of $\Delta f = f_s / N$. For an $N = 256$ point transform at 500\,Hz this is about 1.95\,Hz per bin, fine enough to separate beacons spaced a few hertz apart.

First, we reduce the variance of the spectrum estimate itself. A single transform gives a noisy estimate of each bin's magnitude, so rather than read the peak from one transform, we average the magnitude spectra of several successive windows that overlap by half their length. This averaging of overlapping windows is a standard variance-reduction technique introduced by Welch~\cite{welch1967}. For statistically independent windows it would reduce the variance of the estimate by a factor of $N_{\text{avg}}$. The half-window overlap leaves successive windows correlated, however, so the reduction is smaller, and with only $N_{\text{avg}} = 2$ windows it is modest. The cost of averaging is latency: each averaged spectrum spans several windows, so the reported signal responds more slowly to the drone's motion. We therefore keep $N_{\text{avg}}$ small, accepting only marginal smoothing in exchange for a responsive reading. Figure~\ref{fig:welch} contrasts a single noisy spectrum with the averaged one, in which the noise floor is visibly smoother while the beacon peak is unchanged.

Averaging the magnitude spectra of several windows reduces this scatter and leaves a smoother, more representative floor, while the beacon peak is unaffected.}
    \label{fig:welch}
\end{figure}

Second, we make the noise floor robust to spikes. The noise floor is the representative magnitude of the spectrum away from the beacon, and it forms the denominator of the SNR. A single loud disturbance, such as a motor harmonic or a pickup at the mains frequency, appears as a tall spurious bin that would inflate a mean-based floor and make every SNR smaller. We therefore estimate the floor with the median of the off-target bins rather than their mean, because the median is barely moved by a few extreme values. We compute it over all spectrum bins except a guard band around the beacon's frequency and its first four harmonics, which we exclude so that the beacon's own energy is not counted as noise. The median costs only a sort of the bins rather than a sum, an expense that is negligible at this window size on the drone's microcontroller. Figure~\ref{fig:snr-spectrum} shows the difference the median makes: the interference spikes lift a mean-based floor noticeably, while the median floor stays on the broadband level the SNR should be measured against.

Third, we smooth the per-channel magnitude over time. We blend each channel's freshly measured magnitude $\hat{S}_n$ with its previous smoothed value through a first-order exponential moving average, an infinite-impulse-response low-pass filter that weights recent measurements most heavily while retaining a fading memory of older ones~\cite{oppenheim1999dsp},
\begin{equation}
S_n[k] = \alpha_m\, S_n[k-1] + (1 - \alpha_m)\,\hat{S}_n[k],
\label{eq:magiir}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_m \in [0,1]$ sets how much of the previous value is retained. We apply the same $\alpha_m$ to all eight channels, so that their relative magnitudes, which encode the bearing to the beacon, are preserved while the jitter that vibration adds is suppressed. The factor trades responsiveness against smoothness: a larger $\alpha_m$ gives a steadier but slower signal, a smaller one a faster but noisier signal, and we use $\alpha_m = 0.5$. Figure~\ref{fig:ema} shows the effect on a noisy magnitude, where the smoothed signal is far steadier and lags only slightly when the true signal changes. Because this smoothing sits on top of the Welch averaging, the magnitude the controller finally acts on, $S_n$, responds more slowly to the drone's motion than either stage alone.

    \caption{Exponential moving average smoothing, on generated data, as the true signal steps up when the drone nears the beacon. The raw per-frame magnitude $\hat{S}_n$ scatters widely, whereas the smoothed magnitude $S_n$ is far steadier. The price is a short lag after the step, which a larger $\alpha_m$ would lengthen.}

The smoothing improves the magnitude the controller steers on, but it must not distort the SNR the gate relies on. The gate decides, frame by frame, whether a reading can be trusted, so its SNR must reflect the noise present at that moment. Dividing the smoothed magnitude by an equally smoothed noise floor would make the SNR lag a sudden rise in noise, and the gate could keep trusting a reading after a disturbance had already swamped it. We therefore reconstruct the gate's SNR each frame as the smoothed magnitude $S_n$ divided by the current-frame noise floor. The numerator is then stable while the denominator tracks the noise present now, which keeps the gate calibrated to current conditions. Appendix~\ref{app:parameters} lists $\alpha_m$, $N_{\text{avg}}$, the guard band, and the other pipeline parameters.


\section{Filter Validation}

We now test what each stage of the pipeline contributes in flight, and whether each is justified. We hold the drone at a fixed pose, 95\,cm from a single 150\,Hz beacon, so that the true signal is constant and any variation recorded over time is the noise and jitter that the filters are meant to remove. From each held run we take two numbers: the mean SNR, which reflects the signal level, and the standard deviation of the SNR, which reflects its jitter.

We build the pipeline up one stage at a time, starting from no filtering and adding the hardware filter, then Welch averaging, then the median floor, then the magnitude smoothing, so that each stage's contribution can be read on its own. We measure each configuration in two states. In the first, the motors are off and the drone is held at flight altitude on a rig. In the second, the drone hovers freely with the motors running, since the spinning motors are the main source of the vibration and electrical interference that the filters target. The motors-off readings give a clean reference ceiling, and the gap to the motors-on readings is the cost of flight.

Two limitations of this validation setup bear on how the results should be read. First, in free hover the drone drifts slightly, so the motors-on standard deviation includes a small contribution from the changing pose rather than from jitter alone, although unbiased drift leaves the mean unaffected. Second, we fly all runs under the same ambient room lighting, the factor studied in Section~\ref{sec:ambient}, held constant across configurations, so the lighting raises every reading equally and does not confound the comparison between them.

The result in Table~\ref{tab:filter-snr} is dominated by the median noise floor. The hardware filter and Welch averaging, added on their own, change the level only slightly, and the hardware filter even lowers it. The median floor then transforms the result, more than doubling the in-flight SNR from about 15 to 32 and more than quadrupling the motors-off SNR from about 16 to 74. The reason lies in the definition of the SNR as the peak divided by the noise floor: the median ignores the occasional spike that inflates a mean-based floor, so the same peak is divided by a smaller and more representative noise level, and the SNR it yields is correspondingly larger.

The magnitude smoothing adds little to the level but steadies the reading, lowering the in-flight standard deviation from 7.5 to 5.3. The standard deviations grow at the median-floor step only because the SNR values themselves grow, so the cleanest measure of the smoothing is this last step, where the mean barely changes. Flight remains costly throughout: even with the full pipeline the in-flight SNR of about 32 sits well below the motors-off ceiling of about 77, which confirms that the spinning motors are the main disturbance the pipeline must absorb.

The hardware filter warrants closer inspection, since on its own it does not raise the SNR. We therefore measure the full pipeline once more with the filter unpopulated, reported in Table~\ref{tab:filter-hw}. Removing the filter drops the SNR from about 77 to 33 with the motors off, and from 32 to 22 in flight, so the filter is not redundant. It contributes little before the median floor is in place, but the two stages are complementary: by removing aliased high-frequency energy, the hardware filter keeps the noise floor low, and together the two yield the largest signal.

\section{Effect of Ambient Lighting}
\label{sec:ambient}

The pipeline recovers the beacon's signal against the noise inside its frequency band, but a second factor limits the signal from outside the frequency domain: the level of ambient light in the room. Ambient light is not itself a dominant source of noise, which the motors, electrical interference, and reflections supply. Instead it raises the steady baseline that each photodiode reads. A photodiode has a limited output range, so a higher baseline leaves less of that range for the beacon's modulation to occupy. The reading then swings less between its high and low states, less energy reaches the beacon's frequency bin, and the SNR drops. In a darkened room the baseline is low, the modulation swings freely, and the SNR rises. Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-baseline} illustrates the mechanism: a low baseline leaves the full sensor range for the beacon's swing, whereas a high baseline pushes the reading toward the upper limit and clips the swing, so less of the modulation survives to reach the beacon's frequency bin.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ambient_baseline.png}
    \caption{How a higher ambient baseline shrinks the beacon's modulation, on generated data. The beacon switches the lamp on and off, which should produce the same swing in both rooms. (a) In the dark the low baseline leaves the whole range free, so the full swing is captured. (b) Under room lighting the high baseline sits close to the sensor's upper limit, so the on-phase is clipped and the captured swing is much smaller, which lowers the energy at the beacon's frequency and so the SNR.}
    \label{fig:ambient-baseline}
\end{figure}

To confirm this mechanism, we fly the same two-beacon traverse twice from the same take-off point, once in the dark and once with the room lights on. Under the room lights the photodiodes read roughly 360 to 450\,lux facing up and 120 to 160\,lux to the side at flight altitude, against 12 to 22\,lux and 2 to 22\,lux respectively in the dark. Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-traversal} shows the two flight paths over the light maps the drone builds, and Figure~\ref{fig:ambient-snr} shows the overall SNR over each flight, taken as the maximum across the eight channels. The dark flight holds a higher SNR throughout, peaking above 30 against around 20 in the light. This confirms the mechanism: the lower baseline gives the beacon a stronger signal in its frequency bin. Because the drone steers itself, the two flights follow somewhat different paths, and we fly a single run per condition, so these results are indicative of the effect rather than an exact like-for-like comparison.
