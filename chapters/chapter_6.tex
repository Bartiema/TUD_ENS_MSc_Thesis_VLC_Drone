\chapter{Obstacle Experiments}
\label{chp:obstacle}

This chapter tests the central task of the thesis, autonomous drone navigation towards a light beacon when an obstacle blocks the direct path. We first inspect the light field in a real room and show that the obstacle creates measurable differences in the received light signal, leaving a shadowed region that the controller can exploit for navigation. We then evaluate the fused controller in simulation and show that it can avoid the obstacle using only the light signal first. Next, we fly the same obstacle-avoidance mission on the Crazyflie in the real world. Finally, we report how the same sensing board and controller behave when transferred from the quadrotor platform to a blimp-like drone.


\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:obstacle-setup}

We study the obstacle task in two stages, each isolating a different source of difficulty. We first repeat the simulation from Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}, using the same scene in which the bearing-only and gradient-only baselines failed, and show that the fused controller succeeds where they did not. We then move to the real room, flying the Crazyflie~2.1 with the photodiode board from Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} and the full signal pipeline from Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}, to confirm that the controller remains effective under real-flight conditions. In both stages, the obstacle is placed between the drone's start position and the beacon. We deliberately place the box slightly to one side of the direct line of sight of the beacon, rather than fully centered in it. A box placed directly in the middle of the line of sight would hide the beacon along the main light path, leaving the drone with little useful signal at the start and turning the experiment into a blind search. By shifting the box to one side, the obstacle occludes the beacon over only part of the path while leaving a route that the drone can still discover from the light field. This setup is therefore a more meaningful and realistic obstacle-avoidance test. The offset remains small enough that the drone is still initially drawn toward the light, but must detect the obstacle's effect on the light field before colliding with it.


The simulated scene is the one already shown in Section~\ref{sec:existing-methods-fall-short}, with the beacon, the obstacle pillar, and the drone approaching from one side, so we do not repeat it here. Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-setup} shows the real-room geometry. The room carries the same two beacons as the open-space traverses of Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon}, and each obstacle flight targets one of them. The photograph in Section~\ref{sec:multibeacon-setup} shows the same box already in place. As with the open-space setup of Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon}, the top-down geometry of Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-setup} is an indicative reference to the room's layout rather than a guide on the exact placement of the items in the setup.

\section{SNR Field Mapping}
\label{sec:snr-field}

The fused controller relies on the light map when the obstacle occludes the beacon, so before flying the obstacle mission, we first verify that the real-room SNR field behaves as the design assumes. Two properties must hold. First, the light signal must increase toward the beacon, so that a gradient exists for the drone to climb. Second, the obstacle must leave a recognisable mark in the field. We check both properties by directly surveying the light field with a predefined flight path through a set of fixed points, rather than inferring the field from a navigation run.

We map the field with a survey flight that covers the area in a regular back-and-forth raster, or lawnmower pattern. The drone holds a fixed altitude of 0.8\,metres, the same height used during navigation flights, so the survey samples the field through which the controller actually flies. It moves at a slow, steady speed of about 0.2\,metres per second while logging the SNR at each photodiode. The map records the data in an area of about 4.5\,$m^2$, which is divided into 5\,$cm^2$ cells, producing a field map much denser than any single navigation flight requires. This gives four field maps in total: the two beacons, modulated at 150 and 170\,Hz, each mapped once with the obstacle in place and once without it.


These maps serve as a reference, not as a metric survey of the room. The drone's own odometry localises their positions, and that odometry drifts over the time of the flight and depends strongly on the drone's starting angle. Even a small initial heading error will shift where the whole surveyed area lands, so the four maps cannot be laid directly on top of one another, and the distances within any one of them are not a true measurement of the room's geometry. What they do show, reliably, is how the SNR field behaves relative to the beacon. Showing the field relative to the beacon is all we need, because the controller acts on a heading rather than an absolute beacon position. The navigation algorithm of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} is built around this heading-based principle, so the odometry drift that makes the maps unsuitable as a metric survey does not impair the navigation itself.

Without the obstacle, the SNR field rises smoothly toward the beacon. As the obstacle-free panels in Figures~\ref{fig:snr-field-150-noobs} and~\ref{fig:snr-field-170-noobs} show, each beacon field increases from the far side of the room to a clear peak near the corresponding beacon. This confirms that the real-room light field provides the gradient signal required by the controller.


Placing the obstacle creates a region of low SNR behind it, as the with-obstacle panels of Figures~\ref{fig:snr-field-150-obs} and~\ref{fig:snr-field-170-obs} show. Where the box stands between the survey and the beacon, the modulated signal can no longer reach the photodiodes directly, so the recovered SNR drops and leaves a shadow in the field. This shadow is precisely the region in which the bearing becomes unreliable and the drone must navigate on the stored map instead, the situation the recovering phase of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} handles. In these survey maps we draw the obstacle larger than the real box used in the rest of the experiments. The true size of the box is as shown in Section~\ref{sec:obstacle-setup} and is the same as in the experiments of Section~\ref{sec:obstacle-realflight}. We enlarge the box in code because we want to guard against the drift in odometry of the drone. This larger virtual box will make sure that the drone avoids the real box even when its odometry has drifted.

\section{Obstacle Avoidance}

\subsection{Simulation}
\label{sec:obstacle-sim}

After verifying that the surveyed SNR field contains the light-gradient structure required by the controller, we test the avoidance task itself. We first evaluate the fused controller in simulation, using the same scene from Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}, where a pillar blocks the direct line of sight between the drone and the light source. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:fused-sim}, the proposed controller succeeds in the same scenario where the bearing-only and gradient-only baselines failed in Section~\ref{sec:existing-methods-fall-short}. The controller begins with the bearing estimate alone, because the recorded light map is still too sparse at the start to support a reliable gradient. Once the map has enough spatial coverage and the gradient estimate becomes reliable, the controller blends the gradient with the bearing estimate and steers the drone using the combined direction. This blended heading naturally curves the trajectory around the pillar, allowing the drone to avoid the obstacle and reach the beacon without stopping to search again when the direct light signal becomes weak. Importantly, this avoidance behaviour does not come from the drone falling back to the \emph{searching} state and following a predefined search pattern. The drone remains in the \emph{approaching} state, while the gradient information actively pulls it away from the pillar. This simulation therefore validates that the fused controller can avoid the obstacle and reach the target safely in a case where both baseline controllers fail.

\subsection{Real-World Flight}
\label{sec:obstacle-realflight}

However, success in simulation does not guarantee success in the real world, because real flight introduces light signal disturbances that the simulation does not model: ambient light, electrical and motor noise in the photodiode signals, and drift in the drone's odometry. Each of these disturbances can degrade the bearing estimate and the light map on which the controller depends. We therefore fly the obstacle mission on the Crazyflie to confirm that the avoidance behaviour demonstrated in simulation also holds during real-world flight. The drone takes off from the origin and heads toward a single beacon, with the box placed slightly to one side of the straight-line path between the drone and the beacon.

We run the experiment multiple times, but present only two representative runs in which the drone passes close enough to the obstacle to demonstrate the avoidance mechanism. Although the drone reaches the beacon in all runs, many trajectories stay well away from the box, so the obstacle does not significantly affect the flight. The two runs shown here are selected because they bring the drone near the obstacle and therefore expose the controller to the avoidance condition. They also illustrate two different ways in which the controller responds to the obstacle. In both selected flights, the drone reaches the beacon by steering around the box rather than colliding with it, and the controller correctly registers arrival before entering the dwelling state. Because we do not conduct a controlled repeatability trial, these runs demonstrate that the avoidance behaviour is feasible, but we do not quantify the success rate in this evaluation.


We first examine the flight shown in Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-good}, which demonstrates how the controller uses the stored light map when the direct beacon signal becomes unreliable. The upper panel plots the trajectory from the origin as the drone steers past the box, drawn over the light map built during flight, where each cell stores the measured SNR. The lower panel tracks the controller state and the overall SNR over time. Unlike the simulation, this real flight includes a brief loss of reliable bearing information as the drone passes behind the obstacle, causing the controller to enter the \emph{recovering} phase before returning to \emph{approaching}. During the flight, the state machine described in Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} progresses through \emph{searching}, \emph{aligning}, and \emph{approaching}. As the drone moves behind the obstacle, the beacon becomes occluded and the overall SNR drops sharply, causing the controller to enter \emph{recovering} before returning to \emph{approaching}. At this point, the magnitude of the bearing estimate falls below its trust threshold, so the bearing no longer provides a usable heading. This is exactly the situation that a bearing-only controller cannot handle: without memory of the light field, the sudden drop in SNR would leave it with no sufficiently confident direction to follow. The stored map provides that direction. Because the gradient fitted to the map still satisfies its reliability conditions, the state machine enters \emph{recovering}, and the drone continues using the gradient estimate alone, climbing the stored light field to move out of the obstacle's shadow. Once the bearing estimate's confidence rises back above its trust threshold, the controller returns to \emph{approaching}, moves directly towards the beacon, and enters \emph{dwelling}.

The second flight reaches the beacon without entering the \emph{recovering} state, representing the other operating scenario. The difference between the two flights comes from where the drone passes relative to the box's shadow, not from any change in the controller. The first path passes through a deeper part of the shadow, where the beacon is occluded and the bearing estimate becomes unreliable, whereas the second path skirts the shadow's edge. In the second flight, the drone therefore keeps a usable bearing throughout the approach, so the controller remains in the \emph{approaching} state and never needs to rely on the map alone. The blended bearing-and-gradient heading is sufficient to curve the trajectory around the obstacle, as Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-dodge} shows. Together, the two flights show that the controller uses the light map only when the direct light signal drops significantly: it recovers using the gradient when the bearing falls below its trust threshold, and otherwise avoids the obstacle using the fused heading while the bearing remains valid.

\section{Generalisation to a Blimp Platform}
\label{sec:blimp-generalisation}

Our validation spans both the Webots simulation and the Crazyflie~2.1, but the contribution is not intended to be tied to a single platform. We therefore also demonstrate that the sensing system can be deployed on a different airframe. This validation is carried out in collaboration with Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta}, who developed the lighter-than-air blimp and the Webots simulation used in this thesis. The blimp is a closely related micro-drone platform, supported by a helium balloon and driven by a different motor layout. We mount the same light-sensing board and run the same controller proposed in this thesis on their blimp platform, and confirm that it can reach several modulated beacons in sequence and avoid obstacles, as it does on the Crazyflie quadrotor. The same board and algorithm therefore operate across two different airframes, demonstrating that the method proposed in this thesis depends primarily on the light-sensing and control strategy rather than on the specific drone platform.


However, we observe a substantial difference in sensing performance between the blimp and the Crazyflie: the blimp flights achieve a higher SNR than the Crazyflie flights. Since the same photodiode board is mounted in the same position on both airframes, this difference is likely caused by the drone platform rather than the sensor itself. The helium balloon keeps the blimp aloft, so its motors run only when the platform moves and consume much less power. In contrast, the Crazyflie must drive its motors continuously to remain airborne. The following section examines how this platform difference affects the performance of the sensing board.

\subsection{Motor Noise on the CrazyFlie}
\label{sec:motor-noise}

One major difference between the blimp and the Crazyflie is how their motors are used. To remain airborne, the Crazyflie must drive all four motors at a high duty cycle, whereas the blimp platform from the work by Huang et al.~\cite{huang2026lta} relies on buoyancy and uses its motors only lightly. This suggests that the motors are likely the main source of the sensing degradation, so we measure their effect directly. We hold the board at a fixed pose and record the SNR while running an increasing number of motors, both with and without the hardware anti-aliasing filter. Table~\ref{tab:motor-noise} and Figure~\ref{fig:motor-noise} show the results. The SNR falls steadily as more motors spin. With the hardware filter in place, it drops from about 61 with all motors off to about 25 at full throttle, confirming that motor operation is the dominant disturbance.


A motor introduces noise into the board in two ways. First, it draws current, which can create electrical interference. Second, it mechanically shakes the board through motor vibration. To separate these two effects, we repeat the four-motor case with the motors moved onto extension wires, which mechanically isolates the motors from the board while keeping them electrically connected. With the filter in place, this setup recovers about half of the lost SNR, increasing it from about 25 to about 43. This result shows that mechanical vibration and electrical interference both contribute substantially to the sensing degradation. The mechanical component is especially important for the Crazyflie because the airframe that transmits motor vibration is also the structure that provides lift. As a result, the sensing board cannot be fully isolated from motor vibration without changing the drone's mechanical design.


The motors are therefore the main reason the CrazyFlie records a lower SNR than the blimp: because the blimp drives its motors far less, it avoids most of the SNR loss they cause. Motor noise is also hard to exclude since at different duty cycles the motors will introduce different frequencies of noise. Additionally, the mechanical vibrations they cause are hard to avoid, since the motors need a mechanical connection to the drone to even be able to apply the forces to move the drone. We leave both of these issues to future work to investigate and solve futher.
