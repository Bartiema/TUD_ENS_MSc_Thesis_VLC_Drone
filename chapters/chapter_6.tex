\chapter{Obstacle Experiments}
\label{chp:obstacle}

\section{Experimental Setup}

The obstacle experiments test the central task this thesis set out to solve: reaching a light beacon when an obstacle stands in the way, where a purely reactive controller would be stuck. We place a physical obstacle into the route between the drone's start position and the beacon, set a little to one side so that it intrudes on the path without sitting squarely in the line of sight. It sits close enough that the drone must steer around it to arrive. We study the task in two stages. We first repeat the simulation of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}, where the bearing-only and gradient-only baselines already failed, and show that the fused controller succeeds in the same scene. We then move to the real room, flying the CrazyFlie~2.1 with the photodiode board of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware} and the full pipeline of Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld}, to confirm that the behaviour survives the noise of real flight.

The simulated scene is the one already shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sim-setup}, with the beacon, the obstacle pillar, and the drone approaching from one side, so we do not repeat it here. Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-setup} shows the real-room geometry. The room carries the same two beacons as the open-space traverses of Chapter~\ref{chp:multibeacon}, and each obstacle flight targets one of them, with the box set into the route toward it and a little to one side. The photograph in Figure~\ref{fig:flight-setup} shows the same box already in place. As in those traverses, the plotted positions are an indicative reference rather than a measured survey of the room.

\section{SNR Field Mapping}
\label{sec:snr-field}

The fused controller leans on the light map exactly when the beacon is occluded, so before flying the obstacle mission we verify that the real-room SNR field behaves as the design assumes. We need two things to hold: that the field rises toward the beacon, so a gradient exists for the drone to climb, and that the obstacle leaves a recognisable mark on it. We check both by surveying the field directly with a dedicated mapping flight, rather than inferring it from a navigation run.

We map the field with a survey flight that covers the area in a regular back-and-forth raster, a lawnmower pattern. The drone holds a fixed altitude of 0.8 metres, the same height it uses during its navigation flights, and moves at a slow, steady speed while logging the SNR at each photodiode. The map records the field in 5\,centimetre cells over an area of three by one and a half metres. This is a far denser and more even sampling than any single navigation flight builds. This gives four field maps in all: each of the two beacons, at 150 and 170\,Hz, mapped both with the obstacle in place and without it.

These maps must be read as a reference, not as a metric survey of the room. Their positions are localised by the drone's own odometry, which drifts over the course of a flight and depends strongly on the drone's starting angle. Even a small initial heading error shifts where the whole surveyed area lands, so the four maps cannot be laid directly on top of one another, and the distances within any one of them are not a true measurement of the room's geometry. What they do show, reliably, is how the SNR field behaves relative to the beacon, and that is all we ask of them. This is enough because the controller acts on a heading, not on an absolute beacon position. The algorithm of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm} is built around this, so drift in the odometry does not mislead it.

Without the obstacle, the SNR field rises smoothly toward the beacon. As Figures~\ref{fig:snr-field-150} and~\ref{fig:snr-field-170} show, each beacon's field climbs from the far side of the room to a clear peak near the beacon, which confirms that the navigable gradient the controller depends on is present in the real room.

Placing the obstacle opens a region of low SNR behind it. Where the box stands between the survey and the beacon the modulated signal can no longer reach the photodiodes directly, so the recovered SNR drops, leaving a shadow in the field. This shadow is the real-room counterpart of the occlusion the simulation creates with its pillar, and it is precisely the region in which the bearing becomes unreliable and the drone must navigate on the stored map instead. In these survey maps the obstacle is drawn larger than the physical box, whose true size is the one shown later in the flight plots of Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-good}. We made it larger not only in the plots but in the drone's flight code, to leave a margin against odometry drift during the slow raster. The box itself was never bigger; only the footprint the drone treated as off-limits was enlarged, so it kept clear of the real obstacle.

The 170\,Hz field also appears to carry a slight dip near the 150\,Hz beacon, in a spot where the smooth fall-off alone would lead us to expect a somewhat higher SNR. We read this tentatively as an effect of the 150\,Hz beacon: where that beacon is bright it may begin to saturate the photodiodes, flattening their response and lowering the recovered SNR across all frequencies, the 170\,Hz one included. The dip is faint, and the survey cannot separate it cleanly from the ordinary fall-off with distance, so we note it only as a likely trace of the same close-range saturation that bounds the sensor's operating range, and a reminder that the field is clean only in the band where the photodiodes are neither starved nor saturated.

\section{Obstacle Avoidance}

\subsection{Simulation}

In simulation the fused controller solves the task that defeated both baselines of Chapter~\ref{chp:algorithm}. Flying the same scene, from the same start toward the same beacon as the bearing-only and gradient-only runs, the fused controller routes cleanly around the obstacle and reaches the beacon, as Figure~\ref{fig:fused-sim} shows. After a brief opening segment on the bearing alone, the controller switches to its fused mode and from then on steers by the blended bearing-and-gradient heading. This blended heading curves the path around the pillar on its own, so the drone never drives into the obstacle and never has to stop and search. Here the avoidance comes from the fusion of the two cues rather than from the recovering fallback, which this run never needs to enter. The contrast with the baselines is the point. The bearing-only controller collided with the pillar in Figure~\ref{fig:bearing-fail}, and the gradient-only controller stalled in Figure~\ref{fig:gradient-fail}, while the fused controller, holding both the immediate bearing and a memory of the field, rounds the obstacle and arrives.

\subsection{Real-World Flight}

We then fly the obstacle mission on the CrazyFlie. The drone takes off from its origin and heads for a single beacon, with the box set into the route toward it and a little to one side of the straight-line path.

We present two runs that bring the drone close to the obstacle. We flew the mission several times and the drone reached the beacon on every run, but on many of them the path stayed clear of the box, so the obstacle never came into play. The two we show pass close to it, where the controller actually has to contend with the obstacle, and they happen to exercise it in two different ways.

In both flights the drone reaches the beacon, steering around the box rather than into it, and the controller registers its arrival and dwells. We did not run a controlled repeatability trial, so these results show that the avoidance works rather than measuring how reliably it succeeds.

In the first flight the beacon signal weakens enough that the controller falls back on the map, exercising the recovering phase that the simulation never needed. Figure~\ref{fig:obstacle-good} shows the path, in the upper panel, leaving the origin and steering past the box, drawn over the light map the drone built as it flew, whose cells are coloured by the SNR measured there. The lower panel tracks the controller's state and the overall SNR over time. As the drone passes behind the obstacle the beacon is occluded, the SNR falls to a trough, and the bearing becomes unreliable. This is exactly the situation a purely reactive controller cannot handle, and it is where the recovering phase earns its place. The state machine drops into recovering, and the drone keeps moving on the gradient of the stored map alone until the signal returns. It then resumes approaching, closes on the beacon, and dwells.

Here the drone keeps a usable bearing throughout, so the controller stays in its approaching phase and never enters recovering. The two flights together show the controller leaning on the map only as far as it needs to, recovering on the gradient when the signal drops and steering past the obstacle on the fused heading when it does not.

\section{Generalisation to a Blimp Platform}

Our own validation spans the Webots simulation and the CrazyFlie~2.1, but the contribution is not tied to that platform, as our collaboration with Harry Huang shows. Harry developed the lighter-than-air blimp and the Webots simulation this thesis uses, and demonstrated the bearing and single-photodiode gradient methods on it in simulation~\cite{huang2026lta}; those base methods were only ever tested in simulation, never flown on the real blimp. The blimp itself is a closely related micro-drone, held up by a helium balloon and using a different motor layout. We developed the fused controller within his simulation, built the photodiode board of Chapter~\ref{chp:hardware}, and ran the real-world experiments of this thesis on the CrazyFlie. Only then, once that work was complete, did Harry carry the same board and fused controller onto the real blimp, adapting only the low-level control to its motor layout, and validate there that it reaches several modulated beacons in turn and avoids obstacles, as it does on the CrazyFlie~\cite{huang2026lta}. The same board and algorithm therefore work across two quite different airframes, which shows the approach depends on the light sensing rather than on the specifics of the platform.

The blimp flights also show a higher SNR than we obtain on the CrazyFlie. The photodiode board is the same and sits in the same place on the airframe. What differs is that the buoyant platform stays aloft without constantly driving its motors. They run only when it moves, and at far lower power, so they couple much less vibration and electrical interference into the board than the CrazyFlie's do. Chapter~\ref{chp:realworld} identified exactly this motor disturbance as the main cost of flight. A higher SNR would extend the usable sensing range, letting the drone pick out the beacon direction from further away. We return to its cause, and to what it could buy, in the future work of Chapter~\ref{chp:futurework}.

Seen this way, the result closes a loop with the motivation of Chapter~\ref{chp:introduction}. The prior work we set out to extend could reach only a single beacon in open space; the board and fused controller developed here now give that same platform the multi-beacon, obstacle-aware navigation it lacked.
